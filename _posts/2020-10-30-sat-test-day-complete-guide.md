The SAT is not just a test of academic knowledge. It is also a test of preparation, execution, and composure under pressure. Many students spend months mastering algebra, perfecting grammar rules, and completing practice test after practice test, yet still underperform on the actual day because they did not prepare for the logistics, the physical demands, the mental challenges, and the unexpected variables that come with sitting a high-stakes standardized test.

Test day performance is the culmination of every preparation session you have ever done, but it is also its own distinct challenge. The strategies and habits that govern what you do in the twelve hours before, during, and after the test can have just as much impact on your score as an additional month of content review. Students who treat test day planning as an afterthought frequently leave points on the table that their preparation had earned.

![SAT Test Day Guide](/assets/images/blog/blog-90.webp)

This guide covers the complete SAT test day experience with the granular specificity that matters. From the ideal routine the evening before, through every stage of the morning, check-in, the test itself, the break, recovery from adversity, and all the way through what happens after you submit your final answer, everything you need to know is here. Read this guide well before test day, create your personal checklist, and revisit it the night before. The students who perform best on test day are the ones who have already mentally rehearsed every scenario described below.

## The Night Before the SAT

The evening before the SAT is one of the highest-leverage periods in your entire preparation. What you do in those hours can either prime you for peak performance or undermine everything you have built. The single most important principle is this: the night before the test is not a study session. It is a preparation and recovery session.

### Stop Studying at Least Three Hours Before Bed

The impulse to cram on the night before a major test is understandable but counterproductive. Attempting to absorb new material the night before the SAT creates cognitive noise without meaningful benefit. By the eve of your test date, your preparation should already be complete. Cramming grammar rules or algebra formulas at 10 p.m. does not solidify knowledge that took weeks to build; it only adds anxiety and disrupts the sleep cycle that is critical for cognitive performance.

If you feel the urge to do something test-related, limit it to one brief, low-stress activity: flipping through your error log and reminding yourself of two or three patterns you want to watch for, or reading over your personal test-taking strategy notes one final time. Keep this to under fifteen minutes and frame it as a confidence-building review, not a study session.

### Prepare Everything the Night Before

The worst thing that can happen on test day morning is a frantic search for your admission ticket, your ID, or your charging cable. The night before the test, lay out every single item you are bringing in one visible location. This includes your testing device (fully charged), your admission ticket (printed or accessible on your phone depending on your testing center's requirements), your acceptable ID, your snacks, your water bottle, and your backup items.

Place everything in your bag the night before. Set your clothes out. Know your route to the testing center and check if there are any traffic or transit disruptions that might affect travel time. Having every logistical detail settled before you go to sleep eliminates an entire category of morning stress and allows your brain to focus on performance rather than logistics.

### Go to Sleep at the Right Time

Sleep is the single most impactful thing you can do for cognitive performance the night before the SAT. Sleep affects working memory, processing speed, sustained attention, and the ability to access information learned during preparation. Sleep deprivation, even moderate deprivation of one to two hours below your normal level, meaningfully impairs the mental functions the SAT tests.

Aim for your normal sleep duration, which for most students means eight to nine hours. The key word is "normal." This is not the night to attempt an artificially extended sleep. If you normally sleep at eleven and wake at seven, stick to that schedule. Going to bed at nine in an attempt to get ten hours when your body is unaccustomed to it usually results in lying awake for an extended period, which is more stressful than simply maintaining your normal schedule.

Avoid screens for at least an hour before bed. The blue light emitted by phones, tablets, and computers suppresses melatonin production and makes it harder to fall asleep. Read a physical book, listen to calm music, or simply relax in the dark. If anxiety is making it difficult to settle, write down your testing plan on paper, including your wake time, your route, and three things you feel confident about. The act of externalizing your plan onto paper can reduce the mental churn that keeps anxious students awake.

### Managing Pre-Test Anxiety the Night Before

A moderate level of arousal and alertness the night before a high-stakes test is completely normal and even beneficial. The same physiological processes that create anxiety also sharpen focus and increase alertness. The goal is not to eliminate pre-test nerves entirely; it is to prevent anxiety from escalating to a level that disrupts sleep and depletes your resources before the test even begins.

Several evidence-supported techniques help with this. Progressive muscle relaxation involves tensing and releasing muscle groups from the feet upward, systematically reducing physical tension. Spend ten minutes lying down, tensing each muscle group for five seconds and releasing, working from your feet to your calves to your thighs, continuing up through your torso, arms, and finally your face and neck. By the time you complete a full cycle, physical tension has typically diminished significantly, making sleep easier.

Cognitive reframing is another effective technique. Pre-test anxiety is frequently driven by catastrophic thinking: "If I don't score well, my college options will collapse and my future will be ruined." This framing is factually inaccurate. The SAT is one component of a college application, colleges use holistic review, the test can be retaken, and your worth and potential are not determined by a standardized test score. Consciously replacing catastrophic thoughts with accurate ones ("This is one important test that I have prepared for well. Whatever happens, I have options.") reduces the emotional intensity of pre-test anxiety.

If you are a student who has significant test anxiety as an ongoing pattern (not just normal pre-test nerves), and you have not already spoken with your school counselor or a mental health professional about accommodation options, consider doing so well in advance of your next test date. Extended time and separate testing room accommodations are available for students with documented anxiety disorders, and these accommodations can substantially improve the testing experience.

### The Evening Before: What to Avoid

Beyond avoiding last-minute studying, there are several other evening behaviors that reliably impair test day performance and that many students engage in without realizing the cost.

Alcohol and substance use the night before the SAT is more common among older students than people acknowledge, and its impact on cognitive performance the following day is significant. Even moderate alcohol consumption the night before a test impairs memory consolidation, disrupts sleep quality (even if total sleep time is normal), and reduces processing speed and working memory the following morning. This is not moralistic guidance; it is physiological reality that directly affects test scores.

Excessive social media engagement the night before typically increases anxiety rather than relieving it. Seeing posts from other test-takers discussing their preparation, sharing anxiety, or posting about topics that trigger comparison is anxiety-inducing. Treat the evening before the test as a low-stimulus evening.

Intense athletic training or practice the day before the test deserves mention for student-athletes. Moderate activity is beneficial, as discussed above. However, an intense practice, game, or competition the evening before the SAT leaves your body in a recovery state the following morning, with elevated cortisol, depleted glycogen, and potentially disturbed sleep from physical discomfort. If you have scheduling flexibility, avoid intense athletic exertion in the twenty-four hours before a scheduled SAT sitting.

### Avoid Testing Center Research and Score Anxiety Spirals

One of the most common mistakes students make the night before the test is falling into an internet rabbit hole: reading about what happens if Bluebook crashes, looking up horror stories about testing center experiences, checking score statistics, or watching videos about difficult SAT problems. None of this helps. All of it raises anxiety. Close the SAT forums, close the Reddit threads, and resist the urge to seek reassurance from online communities. Your preparation is done. Trust it.

## What to Eat the Night Before and Morning Of

Nutrition on test day is not a minor consideration. The brain requires steady glucose to function at its best, and both under-fueling and over-fueling can impair performance. The goal is stable blood sugar from the evening before through the end of the test.

### The Evening Before

Eat a balanced dinner that is familiar, satisfying, and not excessively heavy. This is not the night to try a new restaurant, eat dramatically more than usual, or consume foods that you know can cause digestive discomfort. Stick to a meal you enjoy and that your body handles well.

Include a mix of complex carbohydrates and protein. Complex carbohydrates (rice, pasta, bread, sweet potatoes) provide lasting energy, while protein (chicken, fish, eggs, beans) contributes to sustained satiety and cognitive function. Avoid high-fat, deep-fried, or unusually spicy meals that can disrupt sleep through indigestion.

Hydration matters too. Drink adequate water throughout the evening. Avoid excessive caffeine in the afternoon and evening, as it can interfere with sleep even if consumed hours before bedtime.

### Test Day Morning Breakfast

Eat breakfast on test day, even if you do not normally eat breakfast. The SAT is a two-plus-hour cognitive effort, and your brain cannot sustain that effort without fuel. A student who skips breakfast is operating at a measurable disadvantage compared to a student who is properly fueled.

The ideal test day breakfast has three characteristics. First, it is familiar. Eating something your body knows and tolerates well eliminates the risk of digestive discomfort during the test. Second, it is balanced. A combination of protein, complex carbohydrates, and healthy fat provides a stable energy release rather than a spike and crash. Eggs with toast, oatmeal with nuts and fruit, a whole-grain bagel with peanut butter, or yogurt with granola all work well. Third, it is timed appropriately. Eat at least thirty to forty-five minutes before your scheduled departure time so your body has begun digesting before you sit down to test.

Avoid foods that cause personal sensitivity. If you know that dairy makes you feel sluggish or that greasy food causes indigestion, this is not the morning to have those. If you have a reliable pre-performance food from athletic or academic experience, use it.

### Caffeine on Test Day

Caffeine can improve focus and alertness, but it must be managed carefully on test day. If you regularly drink coffee or tea, have your normal amount at your normal time. Do not suddenly increase caffeine consumption, as higher doses can cause jitteriness, increased heart rate, and heightened anxiety, all of which interfere with test performance. Do not have caffeine for the first time on test day. The novelty of caffeine's stimulant effects in an unaccustomed body is unpredictable and risky on a high-stakes occasion.

If you drink coffee, finish it at your normal pace. Do not rush it. And bring a water bottle for the test itself, as hydration supports sustained cognitive performance throughout the session.

### Snacks for the Break

You are permitted to bring snacks for the ten-minute break between sections. This is not optional; bring snacks. The break comes approximately mid-way through the testing session, at a point when your blood sugar may be flagging and your energy reserves are being taxed. A small snack during the break can meaningfully support performance in the second half.

Good break snacks are easy to eat quickly, not messy, and provide fast and sustained energy. Options include a banana, a small package of nuts, crackers with peanut butter, a granola bar, or dried fruit. Bring a water bottle and take several sips during the break regardless of thirst. Avoid anything sugary that will cause a rapid spike and subsequent crash within the next hour.

## What to Bring to the Testing Center

Knowing exactly what is required and permitted eliminates one of the most anxiety-producing test day variables: the fear that you will arrive at the testing center missing something critical.

### Required Items

**Acceptable Photo ID.** This is non-negotiable. Without it, you will not be admitted to the test. Acceptable forms of ID typically include a government-issued photo ID (passport, driver's license, state ID card), a school-issued photo ID, or another College Board-accepted identification document. Your ID must include your name exactly as it appears on your registration, your photograph, and your signature (for most accepted forms). Check the College Board's official requirements before test day to confirm your specific ID is acceptable, as requirements are updated periodically.

**Admission Ticket.** Your admission ticket contains your name, test date, testing center, and a photo you uploaded during registration. The proctor uses it to verify your identity against your ID. Some testing centers accept a digital admission ticket displayed on your phone, while others require a printed copy. Confirm the requirement for your specific testing center in advance and bring both formats if there is any uncertainty. Print the ticket even if you believe digital is accepted.

**Your Testing Device, Fully Charged.** The Digital SAT is taken on a device: either a school-provided device (if your school is the testing center) or your own approved laptop or tablet. Your device must be fully charged before you leave for the testing center. Do not rely on being able to charge at the testing center, as outlets may not be available in your assigned room. Bring your charger anyway as a backup and ask the proctor if charging is possible, but treat your device's battery as your primary resource.

**Approved Calculator (If You Choose to Bring One).** The Digital SAT provides Desmos as a built-in calculator on the testing platform, so a physical calculator is optional. However, some students prefer to use a familiar handheld calculator for certain types of calculations. If you bring one, it must be on the College Board's approved list. Prohibited calculators include those with typewriter-style keypads, those that use the internet or communicate wirelessly, and certain models with computer algebra system (CAS) capabilities.

**Number Two Pencils or Pens.** Even though the Digital SAT is taken on a device, you will likely want scratch paper for calculations and note-taking. Testing centers provide scratch paper, but you may want to bring your own pencils or pens for comfort.

### Strongly Recommended Items

**A Backup Charger or Power Bank.** A fully charged external battery pack provides insurance against device failure. If your laptop battery dies or behaves unexpectedly, a power bank can sustain it through the test or buy time while you request a charger.

**Snacks and Water.** As discussed, bring snacks for the break and water for both the break and to have accessible before the test. Some testing centers allow a water bottle on the desk; others require it to remain in your bag. Bring it regardless and follow the proctor's instructions.

**Extra Layers.** Testing rooms can be cold, especially in air-conditioned facilities. A light jacket or sweatshirt that you can put on or take off ensures you remain comfortable regardless of room temperature. Being physically uncomfortable due to cold is a real distraction.

**Watch (Non-Smartwatch).** The testing platform displays a timer on-screen, so a watch is not necessary for time management. However, a simple analog or digital watch (non-smartwatch, non-calculator) provides a secondary reference and some students find it psychologically reassuring. Confirm with your testing center whether watches are permitted.

**A Small Clear Bag for Your Snacks and Belongings.** Many testing centers require belongings to be stored in a clear bag or placed in a visible area during the test. Having a small clear bag prevents any confusion during check-in.

## What Not to Bring

Bringing prohibited items to the testing center creates problems ranging from having your items confiscated to, in serious cases, having your scores cancelled. Know the prohibited list as well as you know the permitted list.

**Phones and Smartwatches During the Test.** Your phone must be powered completely off (not on silent, not on vibrate, completely off) and stored in your bag during the test. If your phone sounds during the test for any reason, the proctor may dismiss you and your scores may be cancelled. A smartwatch is held to the same standard. Power off both devices before you enter the testing room and do not touch them until the break or after the test ends.

**Food and Drinks at the Desk.** Unless you have an accommodation that permits food or drink at your desk, all snacks and drinks must remain in your bag during the test. They are only accessible during the break.

**Notes, Study Materials, and Printed Content.** You may not bring notes, flashcards, textbooks, printed problem sets, or any study materials into the testing room. Do not attempt to review notes while waiting in the testing room. If you are reviewing written notes outside before the test begins, put them away completely before entering.

**Mechanical Pencils (In Some Rooms).** While mechanical pencils are permitted for scratch work, some proctors and testing centers have restrictions on certain types of mechanical pencils, particularly those that might be confused with prohibited devices. Bring standard number two wooden pencils as your primary writing instrument.

**Other Devices.** Tablets not preloaded with Bluebook, e-readers, gaming devices, and any electronics other than your approved testing device should be left at home. Do not bring these items even if they are turned off, as their presence can create confusion and suspicion during check-in.

## The Morning Routine

The morning of the SAT should feel controlled, deliberate, and familiar. Chaos in the morning creates cognitive and emotional load that follows you into the testing room. Controlled mornings produce calm, focused test-takers.

### Wake Up With Time to Spare

Set your alarm to allow at least ninety minutes between waking up and your scheduled departure time. This sounds generous, but it accounts for a real, unhurried breakfast, getting dressed without rushing, reviewing your checklist, and having a buffer if anything takes longer than expected (which on a high-stress morning, things often do).

Set two alarms as a backup. Phone alarms fail rarely, but the peace of mind from a backup alarm costs nothing.

### Morning Movement

Light physical activity in the morning enhances blood flow to the brain, improves mood, and reduces anxiety. This does not mean an intense workout that will leave you tired. A short walk, some gentle stretching, or even five minutes of movement outdoors is sufficient. Fresh air and a few minutes of activity signal to your body and brain that it is time to be alert and engaged.

### Shower and Dress Comfortably

A shower on test day morning helps with alertness and general comfort. Dress in comfortable, layered clothing that you have worn before. Do not wear new shoes on test day, as discomfort is distracting. Dress for the possibility that the room will be cold and that you may want to remove a layer if it is warm. Avoid overly tight clothing or anything that causes physical distraction.

### Brief Mental Preparation

Spend two to three minutes before you leave on intentional mental preparation. This does not mean reciting formulas or reviewing rules. It means centering yourself. Take several slow, deep breaths. Remind yourself that you have prepared for this. Identify one thing you are specifically confident about in each section. Frame the test as an opportunity to demonstrate what you have learned, not as a threat to be feared.

Many high-performers in athletic and academic contexts use a brief pre-performance ritual that signals to their brain that it is time to perform. If you have developed one through practice tests (a specific phrase you say, a physical gesture, a particular song you listen to on the way to the test), use it on test day.

## Arriving at the Testing Center

Arrival logistics deserve specific attention. Arriving too late creates panic and compromises your ability to settle and focus before the test begins. Arriving too early can create extended waiting that feeds anxiety.

### Target Arrival Time

Plan to arrive approximately fifteen to twenty minutes before the scheduled check-in opening time. This allows you to find parking, locate the correct entrance, and settle without rushing, but does not leave you sitting outside for an extended period with nothing productive to do.

Confirm the exact address of the testing center the day before. If you have never been to this location, consider driving the route the day before to verify travel time and identify potential complications like construction detours or tricky parking situations. Know your route so thoroughly that you could drive it without GPS, then use GPS as confirmation rather than direction.

If you are taking public transit, know the specific lines, stops, and transfer points. Have backup options identified in case your primary route is disrupted. Budget extra time for transit variability.

### If You Are Running Late

If unforeseen circumstances have you running late, stay calm and do not speed or take risks to recover time. A few minutes late to check-in is almost never catastrophic; an accident on the way to the test certainly is. Call or text a parent or guardian to notify them, and focus on arriving safely. If you believe you may arrive significantly late, check the testing center's policy on late arrivals.

Most testing centers will not admit students after a certain point following the start of check-in, typically once the test has already begun. This underscores the importance of building buffer time into your travel plan. A forty-five-minute buffer for a testing center thirty minutes away is not excessive; it is prudent.

### The Area Outside the Testing Center

Before you enter, you may see other students reviewing notes, comparing answers, or discussing test strategies. Avoid engaging with this. Do not review notes in the last few minutes before check-in, as cramming at this point adds stress without adding retention. Do not listen to other students' anxiety or allow it to transfer to you. Keep your headphones in, listen to something calming or energizing based on your personal preference, and maintain your mental focus.

## The Check-In Process

Check-in is a formal process managed by testing center staff. Understanding what to expect prevents any confusion from feeling alarming.

### What Happens at Check-In

When you arrive, you will be directed to a check-in area where staff will verify your identity. You will present your admission ticket and your photo ID. The staff member will compare the photo on your ID, the photo on your admission ticket, and your physical appearance. They will check your name against the registration list. This process takes two to five minutes per student.

After identity verification, your belongings may be inspected. Staff may check your bag for prohibited items. This is routine and should not cause concern if you have followed the permitted/prohibited guidelines above.

You will then be directed to your assigned testing room. In some larger testing centers, students are assigned to specific seats within the room. In others, you may choose any available seat. If you have the option to choose, select a seat that minimizes potential distractions: away from windows with distracting views, away from doors through which late arrivals might enter, and away from any environmental source of noise.

### Device Inspection

Staff at some testing centers may ask to inspect your device to confirm it is an approved testing device and that the Bluebook application is properly installed and accessible. Have Bluebook ready to open and demonstrate if asked. Attempting to use an unapproved device is a violation and will result in your dismissal from the test.

### If There Is a Problem With Your ID or Ticket

If there is any issue with your identification or admission ticket during check-in, remain calm and communicate clearly with the testing center staff. Common resolvable issues include name discrepancies (middle name included in registration but not on ID, or vice versa), a forgotten or misplaced admission ticket, or an ID that does not clearly show a signature. Testing center staff are generally experienced with these situations and have procedures for resolving common issues.

If you cannot resolve an issue at check-in, ask to speak with the testing center supervisor. Document what happened and contact the College Board as soon as possible after the event.

## The Testing Room Setup

The testing room environment has a significant impact on the test experience. Understanding what to expect prevents distraction and helps you settle in quickly.

### Room Layout and Physical Environment

Testing rooms are typically standard classrooms or computer labs. You will be seated at a desk or table with sufficient space for your device and scratch paper. The room will be monitored by a proctor throughout the test. There may be one proctor or multiple, depending on the room size.

Room temperature is one of the most frequently mentioned environmental discomforts by test-takers. Testing centers are often heavily air-conditioned, and many students find them colder than expected. Your extra layer, brought as described above, addresses this. If you find the room temperature genuinely disruptive after the test begins, you may quietly raise your hand and mention it to the proctor, who will make a note even if they cannot immediately adjust the temperature.

Lighting is almost always standard classroom fluorescent lighting. If you have a sensitivity to this type of lighting, consider wearing a light hat with a brim if permitted, or positioning yourself away from the most intense fixtures.

### Your Workspace

Once seated, organize your workspace before the proctor begins instructions. Place your testing device in front of you. Have your pencils or pens within reach. Confirm your scratch paper is accessible. If you brought a watch, place it where you can see it comfortably without turning your head significantly. If you have a permitted water bottle, know whether it should go on the desk or in your bag and follow the proctor's guidance.

Take a few slow breaths and settle into your seat. You are here. You are prepared. The familiar environment of a test that you have simulated dozens of times in practice is now real, and the only thing left to do is perform.

### How Seating Affects Performance

If you have choice of seating, the decision deserves brief thought. The front of the room is closer to the proctor, which means you are less likely to be disturbed by late arrivals or by the proctor's movement. It also means you may feel more observed, which some students find activating and others find distracting. The back of the room reduces the sense of being watched but may put you closer to doorways or windows.

Generally, the middle rows of any testing room represent the most neutral environment: away from the door, away from the front, with a clear view of the front of the room if you need to signal the proctor. Seats near windows with active outdoor views or near vents and radiators create environmental distractions. If you arrive early enough to have seat choice, select based on your personal distraction profile.

### Other Students in the Room

Your testing room will likely contain other students who may or may not appear calm, confident, or composed. Do not allow other students' visible anxiety to affect you. Do not compare your preparation to theirs. Do not engage in conversation about the test, your preparation, or your predicted score. Keep your attention on your own experience.

Some students experience what is called "social testing pressure," a heightened sense of competition or comparison when surrounded by peers taking the same test. If you notice this happening, redirect your attention to your own screen, your own pacing, and your own strategy. The student to your left and right are irrelevant to your score. Only the questions on your screen matter.

## The Proctor's Instructions and Test Start

Before the test begins, the proctor will read a scripted set of instructions to the room. These instructions cover the rules of the test, what is and is not permitted during the session, and the procedures for the test itself. Listen carefully.

### What the Proctor Will Cover

The proctor will explain the testing rules: phones must be off, materials not permitted in the test must be in bags, no communication between students is permitted, scratch paper usage procedures, and what to do if you need to use the restroom during the test. Pay attention to the restroom procedure in particular, as it varies by testing center. Most require you to raise your hand, wait for the proctor to acknowledge you, and follow specific protocols for leaving and re-entering the room. Your test timer typically continues running while you are out, so restroom visits during the test are costly.

The proctor will also explain the technical setup: how to launch Bluebook, how to log in, and what to do if you encounter a technical issue. Follow the setup instructions at the pace the proctor directs. Do not rush ahead; do not fall behind.

### The Moment the Test Begins

When the proctor signals the start of the test, take one deliberate breath before beginning. This one-second centering action has a calming neurological effect and is a technique used by high-performers across disciplines. Then read the first question and begin.

The first question of the first module is rarely the hardest. The Digital SAT begins at a moderate difficulty level and adapts from there. Do not let a question that feels hard in the opening minutes destabilize you; difficulty variation is normal and expected.

## Pacing Strategy in Real Time

The Digital SAT consists of two sections: Reading and Writing (RW) and Math. Each section has two modules. Each module is timed separately with a built-in countdown timer displayed on your testing device screen.

### Reading and Writing Module Pacing

Each Reading and Writing module presents 27 questions in a 32-minute window, which yields approximately 1 minute and 11 seconds per question. In practice, some questions require under 30 seconds (clear grammar questions with an obvious answer), while others may require 90 to 120 seconds (complex passage interpretation or rhetoric questions). The goal is to finish each module without running out of time while applying appropriate effort to each question.

Use the following real-time approach:

In the first fifteen minutes, work through questions at a steady pace. Answer questions you feel confident about directly. For questions that give you pause, use the flagging feature (available within Bluebook) to mark them for review and move on immediately. Do not stall on a difficult question when others that you can answer correctly are waiting ahead.

In minutes fifteen to twenty-eight, continue through the remaining questions. At this point you should be nearing the end of the module. If you have flagged questions, you will return to them in the final minutes.

In the final two to four minutes, return to flagged questions. If a flagged question remains unclear after a fresh look, make your best guess and move on. There is no penalty for incorrect answers on the SAT, so leaving any question blank is always the wrong choice.

### Math Module Pacing

Each Math module presents 22 questions in 35 minutes, yielding approximately 1 minute and 35 seconds per question. Math questions have more variability in time requirements than Reading and Writing questions: a straightforward linear equation might take 20 seconds, while a complex data interpretation problem might take three minutes.

Apply the same three-phase pacing approach. In the first eighteen to twenty minutes, work through the module and flag challenging questions. In minutes twenty to thirty, continue while maintaining awareness of your time. In the final five minutes, address flagged questions and verify that every question has an answer.

One important point specific to math: student-produced response questions (grid-ins) require you to enter a numerical answer rather than select from multiple choice options. These questions do not have the same trap-answer risk as multiple choice, but they also do not offer the option to eliminate and guess. If you are running out of time on a grid-in you cannot solve, enter your best estimate rather than leaving it blank.

### Using the Built-In Timer

The Bluebook platform displays a countdown timer for each module. Use it actively. Develop the habit in practice of checking the timer regularly rather than waiting until you are under pressure. Suggested checkpoints: check the timer after every sixth question to confirm your pacing is on track. If you are ahead of pace, slow down slightly and give each question its due attention. If you are behind pace, tighten your time per question and be more decisive about flagging and moving on.

### The Relationship Between Accuracy and Speed

Many students approach SAT pacing as a purely time-management problem: finish all questions within the time limit. But the real pacing goal is not to finish; it is to maximize correct answers within the time limit. These two objectives are related but not identical, and distinguishing them leads to better strategy.

For high-scoring students targeting 700 or above on either section, near-perfect accuracy is essential. This means slowing down on questions that seem straightforward to double-check the logic, not because you lack confidence but because the SAT is specifically designed to reward careful reading. Many questions that appear to have an obvious answer contain a subtle qualifier ("most nearly," "primarily," "except") that changes the correct answer. Reading at a measured pace prevents careless errors that high scorers disproportionately regret.

For students targeting the 500 to 650 range, the priority is often different: avoid getting stuck on the hardest questions and ensure that you correctly answer all the accessible ones. The hardest questions in each module carry the same point value as the easiest ones, but they consume disproportionate time if you allow them to. Flagging hard questions aggressively and returning to them only after completing the accessible ones is the most reliable strategy for this score range.

For students targeting below 500 who are aiming to improve, focus on accuracy over completion. It is better to correctly answer eighteen out of twenty-two Math questions and leave four unanswered (guessing on those four) than to rush through all twenty-two and introduce careless errors throughout. Quality of engagement on answerable questions matters more than completion speed.

### Scratch Paper Usage Strategy

Scratch paper is provided at the testing center and you should use it liberally. Many students underuse scratch paper, attempting to do mathematical work or annotation mentally when writing it out would be faster and more reliable.

In Math, always write out multi-step calculations rather than attempting to hold intermediate values in working memory. Set up equations explicitly on paper before solving them. Sketch graphs when the Desmos calculator is slower than a quick hand sketch would be. Writing your work also creates an audit trail: if you arrive at an answer and it does not match any of the answer choices, you can scan your scratch work to find where the error occurred rather than starting entirely from scratch.

In Reading and Writing, scratch paper can be used to paraphrase the main point of a passage in your own words before tackling the comprehension questions. For grammar questions, writing out the sentence with each answer choice inserted can make the correct option immediately apparent when it is not obvious from reading. For transition questions, noting the logical relationship between the sentences (contrast, addition, causation, example) before looking at the answer choices focuses your selection.

### The Adaptive Structure and What It Means for Pacing

The Digital SAT uses an adaptive structure where your performance in Module 1 of each section determines the difficulty level of Module 2. Strong performance in Module 1 leads to a harder Module 2 that has a higher scoring ceiling; weaker performance in Module 1 leads to an easier Module 2 with a lower scoring ceiling.

This structure has a critical implication for pacing: Module 1 performance determines the maximum score you can achieve in Module 2. An error that costs you one question in Module 1 does not just cost one question; it may affect which Module 2 you receive. This does not mean you should slow to a crawl in Module 1 chasing perfection, but it does mean that Module 1 deserves focused, careful attention. Do not rush through Module 1 carelessly simply because there are more modules ahead.

### Flagging and Review

The Bluebook platform includes a flagging feature that lets you mark questions for later review. Use this feature aggressively. Any time you spend more than sixty seconds uncertain about a question, flag it and move on. The cognitive cost of continuing to stare at a question you cannot solve in the moment is significant: it consumes time, depletes mental energy, and can derail your pacing for subsequent questions.

Flagging is not giving up. It is strategic prioritization. The questions you can answer quickly and confidently are worth exactly the same points as the ones requiring extended thought. Answer the accessible questions first and return to the difficult ones with any remaining time.

## The 10-Minute Break

The ten-minute break between the Reading and Writing section and the Math section is one of the most strategically important periods of the entire testing day. Most students either waste it or actively harm themselves during it. The students who use it well arrive at the Math section re-energized and reset.

### What to Do During the Break

**Eat your snack and drink water.** As soon as you leave the testing room, get your snack from your bag and eat it. Do not wait until the final minutes of the break. Give your body as much time as possible to begin absorbing nutrients. Take several sips of water whether or not you feel thirsty.

**Move your body.** Stand up, stretch your arms and back, and walk around if the space permits. Extended sitting impairs blood flow and contributes to the physical fatigue that can affect the second half of the test. Two minutes of movement makes a measurable difference in alertness.

**Visit the restroom if needed.** Use the break to handle any restroom needs so they do not interrupt your Math modules. Even if you do not feel an urgent need, going during the break avoids the problem later.

**Perform a brief mental reset.** Spend two minutes deliberately disengaging from how you felt the Reading and Writing section went. Whether you felt great or uncertain about RW, thinking about it during the break is counterproductive. What happened in RW cannot be changed. What happens in Math is entirely ahead of you. Use a simple reset technique: close your eyes, take five slow breaths, and consciously direct your attention forward to the Math section.

**Review your math strategy briefly.** In the final three minutes of the break, briefly remind yourself of one or two key math strategies you want to apply: perhaps your approach to data analysis questions, or a reminder to use Desmos for complex equations. This is a confidence-building reminder, not a cram session.

### What Not to Do During the Break

**Do not check your phone.** This is the single most common break mistake. Your phone is powered off in your bag during the test. Leave it there during the break. Checking your phone during the break can expose you to anxiety-producing content (a stressful message, a news notification, a social media interaction) that you then carry into the Math section. It can also lead to a timing error if you become absorbed in your phone and return late to the testing room, which in some centers can result in your test being voided. Leave your phone in your bag.

**Do not discuss the test with other students.** Conversations about the test during the break are almost always anxiety-inducing. If you felt confident, hearing that another student found the test difficult can introduce uncertainty where there was none. If you felt uncertain, hearing that another student breezed through it can be demoralizing. Neither outcome helps you. Keep conversations during the break to pleasantries or silence.

**Do not attempt to recall RW answers.** Some students spend the break mentally reconstructing questions and second-guessing their answers. This is genuinely harmful. You cannot change your RW answers after the section is closed, so any time you spend analyzing them is time and mental energy wasted, and the anxiety it generates will impair your Math performance.

**Do not eat a large meal.** A snack is appropriate. A full meal during a ten-minute break is physiologically counterproductive, as the blood flow redirected to digestion will leave you sluggish and less mentally sharp during the Math section.

## Handling Unexpected Situations

No matter how thoroughly you prepare, test day can present unexpected situations. Knowing how to respond to them in advance prevents them from derailing your performance.

### Technical Issues With Your Device

The Digital SAT is delivered through the Bluebook application, which is designed with offline capability to minimize the impact of internet connectivity issues. However, technical problems with the testing device can occur. If your screen freezes, the application crashes, or your device loses power, do the following: raise your hand immediately and calmly to alert the proctor. Do not attempt to troubleshoot on your own beyond the basic action of tapping or clicking. Do not panic.

Testing centers have protocols for technical issues. Proctors are trained to address device problems, and the Bluebook system is designed to preserve your answers and session even if the application is restarted. Time that is lost due to a technical issue beyond your control is typically compensated by the testing center, though the specific procedures can vary.

The most common "technical issue" is a depleted battery. This is entirely preventable by bringing a fully charged device and a charger. If you notice your battery dropping significantly during the test, raise your hand and ask the proctor whether you can access your charger.

### A Disruptive Testing Environment

External disruptions during the test, including noise from outside the room, activity in the hallway, a disruptive fellow student, or environmental factors like extreme heat or cold, are addressed by alerting the proctor. Raise your hand and describe the disruption quietly. Proctors are responsible for maintaining the testing environment and will take action to address legitimate disruptions.

If the disruption is significant and ongoing and the proctor is unable to resolve it, document what occurred and contact the College Board after the test. If you believe a disruption significantly impaired your performance, you may be eligible to have your test results reviewed or to retake under different conditions.

### Feeling Ill During the Test

If you begin to feel genuinely ill during the test (nausea, dizziness, migraine, or other significant physical symptoms), raise your hand and notify the proctor. You have the option to stop the test and leave; understand that doing so forfeits your results for that sitting, but performing through genuine illness is rarely in your interest. If you are mildly unwell but functional, use controlled breathing and focus techniques to manage symptoms. Ginger chews or a small amount of water can help with mild nausea.

Prevention is the best approach: get proper sleep, eat well, and arrive healthy. If you know you are genuinely ill with something like a significant fever on test day, the difficult but correct decision may be to register for a future date rather than test in a compromised state.

### Running Out of Time

If you realize with two minutes remaining that you have five or more questions still to answer, shift to triage mode immediately. Skip your review of flagged questions. Quickly scan each remaining question and select the most defensible answer, even if it is a pure guess. Answer every single question before the module closes. No answer equals a guaranteed zero for that question; a guess has a non-zero probability of being correct. On four-answer multiple choice questions, a pure guess has a 25% chance of being right. Never leave a question unanswered due to time pressure.

### Time Confusion

The Bluebook platform displays the time remaining for each module clearly. However, some students experience a phenomenon where they perceive time as passing very differently than it actually is, feeling either that time is moving incredibly fast or incredibly slow. If this happens, anchor yourself to the on-screen timer. Look at it every six questions as described above. The clock does not lie, and it will keep you oriented regardless of your subjective time perception.

## Mental Focus Techniques During the Test

The SAT is a two-plus-hour cognitive endurance event. Sustaining focus, managing anxiety, and recovering from difficult moments are skills in their own right, and they can be practiced and deployed deliberately.

### Controlled Breathing for Anxiety

If you notice rising anxiety during the test, it is almost always accompanied by a shift in breathing: it becomes shallower and faster, which reduces oxygen to the brain and creates a mild physiological stress response that compounds anxiety. The antidote is deliberate, slow breathing.

When you notice anxiety rising, pause for five to ten seconds. Do not continue trying to read the question. Take one slow breath in through the nose for four counts, hold for two, and exhale through the mouth for four counts. This pattern activates the parasympathetic nervous system and reduces the physical anxiety response within seconds. You will lose five to ten seconds of test time, but you will gain significantly more through restored focus.

### The Reset Technique for Difficult Questions

When a question seems completely impenetrable, your brain may fixate and loop, attempting the same approach repeatedly in a state of increasing stress. If you have spent more than ninety seconds on a question and are no further along than when you started, flag it immediately and move on. Do not sit with the stuck feeling.

When you flag a question and move to the next one, your brain does not stop processing the flagged question. The cognitive phenomenon of incubation means that your subconscious continues working on the problem in the background while your conscious attention moves to other questions. This is not a myth or wishful thinking; it is a documented feature of human cognition. Students frequently find that when they return to a flagged question at the end of a module, an approach that was invisible before is suddenly accessible.

### The "One Question at a Time" Mindset

One of the most damaging cognitive habits during the SAT is projecting forward or backward: worrying about how many questions you have left, estimating whether you have enough time, second-guessing previous answers, or calculating what score you might need on the remaining questions to hit your target. All of this consumes cognitive bandwidth that should be applied to the question in front of you.

Develop the discipline of treating each question as the only question in the world at that moment. Close off what came before; do not speculate about what comes after. The entire test collapses into a single unit of focus: the question you are currently reading. This mindset is practiced in test prep but must be consciously maintained throughout the actual test.

### Second-Guessing and Answer Changing

Research on standardized testing consistently shows that first instincts are correct more often than the answers students switch to on review. The temptation to change an answer during review is powerful, particularly for questions that felt uncertain the first time through. But the probability that your second look produces a more correct answer is lower than most students believe.

The general rule is: change an answer only if you can identify a specific, concrete reason why your first answer was wrong. "I feel differently about this now" or "This choice looks more appealing than before" are not sufficient reasons to change. "I misread the question the first time and it actually says X, not Y" or "I made a calculation error that I can now correct" are valid reasons.

This rule protects against the anxiety-driven answer-switching that costs many students points in the review phase. If you review a flagged question and remain genuinely uncertain between two options, go with your first marked answer unless you have found a specific error.

### Handling a "Bad" Module

The adaptive structure of the Digital SAT means that if Module 1 of a section was harder than expected, or if you felt you made several errors, you might find yourself in Module 2 of that section at a different difficulty level than you hoped. This can be discouraging in the moment. The critical response is to compartmentalize.

Whatever module you receive in Module 2, the appropriate response is identical: approach each question with focus and effort, flag what you cannot answer quickly, manage your time, and answer every question. A harder Module 2 in Reading and Writing has a higher score ceiling that rewards accurate answers significantly. An easier Module 2 has a lower ceiling but is more accessible. In either case, the strategy is the same. Do not allow your perception of the module's difficulty to change your approach.

### Dealing With Comparison Anxiety in the Room

During the test, you may notice other students progressing quickly, scrolling through questions, or appearing entirely untroubled. This observation can trigger comparison anxiety: "They are moving faster than me; they must know more; I must be falling behind." This reaction is based on a false premise.

The Digital SAT is individual and adaptive. You cannot meaningfully compare your pace or apparent ease with another student's, because you are taking effectively different tests. A student who appears to fly through questions may be skipping difficult ones, leaving easy points on the table. A student who pauses and thinks carefully may be maximizing their score on each question they touch. You have no information about another student's performance from watching them. Keep your eyes on your own screen.

### Managing the Final Push

The final module of the test, Math Module 2, is where many students begin to feel the cumulative mental fatigue of the session. This is normal and expected. A few techniques help sustain performance through the final section:

Keep your posture upright. Slumping physically correlates with reduced alertness. Sit up, keep your shoulders back, and maintain the physical posture of an engaged, alert student rather than a fatigued one.

Use the break energy. The snack and movement from the break should be providing physiological support now. Trust that your preparation accounts for this final stretch.

Use the countdown as motivation rather than pressure. When fifteen minutes remain in the final module, frame the countdown as a sprint to the finish, not a race against a threatening deadline. You have trained for this distance.

Break the final module into mental segments. Rather than viewing Math Module 2 as a thirty-five-minute block, mentally divide it into thirds: the first ten minutes, the middle fifteen, and the final ten. Completing each third is a small win that sustains momentum. Students who mentally treat the final module as a single long block often experience their focus fading mid-module; students who break it into manageable segments maintain sharper attention throughout.

## After the Test: Scores, Cancellations, and Retakes

The experience does not end when you close your device. Understanding what comes next reduces post-test anxiety and helps you make smart decisions about score release and retesting.

### When Scores Are Released

SAT scores are typically released approximately two to three weeks after your test date. The College Board publishes an estimated score release timeline at the time of registration. You will receive an email notification when your scores are available, and you can access them through your College Board account online.

Scores include your total score (400-1600), your section scores for Reading and Writing (200-800) and Math (200-800), subscores by question category, and cross-test scores. The detailed score report also includes information about the difficulty of the questions you answered correctly and incorrectly, which is valuable information for targeted retesting preparation.

Do not obsessively refresh your College Board account before the announced release window. Score releases are processed in batches and not all students receive scores simultaneously; some may see their scores on the release day while others receive them the following day. This is normal and does not reflect any issue with your test.

### The Emotional Experience of Score Day

Score release day is emotionally significant for many students, and it deserves acknowledgment. Whether your scores come in above, at, or below your target, having a plan for how you will process the result helps.

Before you check your scores, establish a clear-eyed framework: scores above target are a success to be celebrated but not over-interpreted; scores at target are exactly what you prepared for; scores below target are data, not a verdict on your potential. Having this framework in place before you see the number prevents a low score from feeling catastrophic and prevents an unexpectedly high score from producing complacency about continued preparation.

It is also worth noting that immediate emotional reactions to scores are frequently not representative of the considered reaction you will have after processing for a day. Many students who initially feel devastated by a score that was five points below their goal later recognize that the score is strong and within the range for their target colleges. Give yourself twenty-four hours before making any retake decisions based on your emotional state at the moment of score release.

### Whether to Cancel Your Scores

The College Board offers score cancellation, which removes your scores from your record and prevents them from being sent to colleges. However, score cancellation decisions must typically be made before scores are released, often by a specific deadline after the test date.

The question of whether to cancel scores before you know what they are is almost always answered the same way: do not cancel unless you are highly confident you experienced a major disruption that significantly impaired your performance and can document it. Score cancellation requires predicting that your results are bad enough to be worth removing from your record, without actually seeing them. Given that the SAT allows score choice (you can choose which test dates' scores to send to colleges), there is rarely a compelling reason to cancel rather than simply not sending scores from a given sitting.

Exceptions include documented technical failures that significantly disrupted your session, clear evidence of testing irregularities, or personal circumstances that substantially affected your performance and can be formally documented. In these cases, contact the College Board promptly.

### Using Score Choice

Score choice is the College Board's policy allowing you to send only the SAT scores from test dates you choose. You are not required to send scores from every date you tested. This means a score from a difficult sitting does not automatically harm your applications; you simply choose not to send it.

Some colleges require or recommend that you submit all scores from all test dates. Research the score policies of the specific colleges you are applying to, as this varies by institution and changes periodically. Many highly selective colleges have moved toward a "consider your best" or "superscore" approach where they take the highest score from each section across all test dates you share with them.

### Deciding Whether to Retake

After scores are released, the question of whether to retake the SAT is one of the most common decisions students face. Key considerations include:

**Your score relative to your goal.** If your score is already at or above the median score for admitted students at your target colleges, the marginal benefit of retaking may be limited. If your score is significantly below your target range, retaking with a focused preparation plan is appropriate.

**Your available preparation time.** Retaking the SAT without additional preparation will likely produce a similar score. A retake is only strategically sound if you can identify specific weaknesses from your score report and devote meaningful preparation time to addressing them before the next sitting.

**Score improvement statistics.** Most students who retake the SAT with focused preparation do improve their scores. The magnitude of improvement depends on how much below your potential your first sitting was, how much preparation you invest, and how effectively you targeted your weakest areas.

**College application timeline.** Be aware of which test dates have score release timelines compatible with your application deadlines. Registering for a test date whose scores will not be available before your application deadlines achieves nothing for that application cycle.

### The Gap Between Test Day Feeling and Actual Score

One of the most consistent phenomena in SAT testing is the disconnect between how students feel they performed and what their scores actually show. Students who walk out of the testing center convinced they performed poorly frequently achieve scores well above what their in-the-moment assessment predicted. Students who feel great about a sitting occasionally encounter scores that are lower than expected.

This disconnect has several causes. The adaptive nature of the Digital SAT means that a harder Module 2 (which students receive after strong Module 1 performance) will feel harder than an easier Module 2, even though the harder module offers a higher scoring ceiling. Students who earn harder modules often perceive the test as more difficult, which they interpret as underperformance, when in fact it reflects overperformance in Module 1.

Additionally, the questions that feel hard and time-consuming are often the ones students ultimately answer correctly after significant effort. The questions that create lasting uncertainty (the ones students leave the test still unsure about) are sometimes ones they actually marked correctly. Human memory is unreliable when it comes to reconstructing test performance, which is why post-test feelings are poor predictors of actual scores.

The only reliable source of truth about your performance is the score report itself. Wait for it before drawing conclusions.

### Reviewing Your Detailed Score Report

Your score report contains more than just numbers. It identifies which question categories you struggled with, which difficulty levels challenged you, and where your strongest performance was. This information is the foundation of any retaking strategy. Do not simply note your total score and decide whether to retake; read the entire score report and use it as a diagnostic tool.

If you are planning to retake, create a preparation plan immediately based on your score report findings. Students who wait weeks before beginning retake preparation lose valuable time and often find themselves cramming in the final weeks before the next sitting.

The subscores for Reading and Writing break down performance by the four content categories: Craft and Structure, Information and Ideas, Standard English Conventions, and Expression of Ideas. The Math subscores cover the four content domains: Algebra, Advanced Math, Problem-Solving and Data Analysis, and Geometry and Trigonometry. A student who scored 650 on Math but showed weakness specifically in Problem-Solving and Data Analysis (typically the most time-consuming question type) has a clear, targeted preparation focus for a retake rather than needing to review all math content equally.

## Simulating Test Day in Practice

The best way to ensure that test day feels familiar rather than foreign is to simulate it during your preparation. Many students practice under conditions that are far more forgiving than actual test day: taking practice tests at home in pajamas, with their phone next to them, pausing to check notifications, taking longer breaks, or stopping halfway through when they get bored. These habits mean that the actual test day environment is genuinely unfamiliar territory.

### The Full Simulation Practice Test

At least once before your actual test date, complete a full SAT practice test under conditions that mirror test day as closely as possible. This means:

Start at the same time of day as your actual test. If your test begins at 8 a.m., start your simulation at 8 a.m. Your body has circadian rhythms that affect alertness, and practicing at your actual testing time trains your peak focus to occur at the right moment.

Eat the breakfast you plan to eat on test day. This is a food trial run. If you discover that your planned breakfast makes you feel sluggish or causes digestive discomfort, you still have time to adjust before the actual test.

Sit at a desk or table, not on your bed or couch. Use a laptop or tablet running Bluebook rather than a paper practice test. The interface matters; comfort with the specific digital environment reduces friction on test day.

Use timed modules with no interruptions. No pausing. No checking your phone. Power it off completely as you would on test day. No snacking during modules, only during the timed break.

Take the ten-minute break as specified: stand up, eat your break snack, move briefly, reset mentally, and return on time.

Complete all four modules in sequence without stopping. The physical and mental experience of sitting through the full test is not something you can replicate by doing two modules today and two tomorrow.

After the simulation, review your performance but also your experience: how your energy held up, whether your pacing worked, how the break felt, and what you would want to do differently. Every simulation makes the actual test day more familiar and less frightening.

### What to Adjust if the Simulation Reveals Problems

If your simulation reveals a logistics problem (your breakfast did not sit well, your charger ran out, your timing was off), address it specifically before test day. If it reveals a performance problem (you ran out of time in Math, you consistently missed a certain question type), use it as preparation data rather than as a source of discouragement. A difficult simulation is more valuable than an easy one, because it identifies exactly what needs refinement while there is still time to fix it.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. What time should I wake up on SAT test day?**

Wake up with at least ninety minutes before your departure time. If check-in opens at 7:45 a.m. and you need thirty minutes to travel, wake up at 5:45 a.m. at the latest to allow ninety minutes for your morning routine. Two hours is even better for students who feel rushed easily.

**2. What happens if I forget my admission ticket?**

Some testing centers will still admit you if you can show your registration confirmation email, your photo ID, and your photo in their system. However, policies vary by testing center and you cannot count on this. Always bring your admission ticket, both printed and accessible digitally as a backup.

**3. Can I use my phone as my testing device?**

No. The Digital SAT must be taken on a laptop, desktop, or tablet running Bluebook. Phones are not an approved testing device regardless of their technical capabilities.

**4. What if the testing room is too cold?**

Bring an extra layer. If you did not and the cold is genuinely disruptive, notify the proctor. They may be able to request an adjustment, but this is not guaranteed. Always bring a jacket or sweatshirt to any standardized test.

**5. Can I use the bathroom during the test?**

Yes, but your timer continues running while you are out. You must raise your hand, get the proctor's acknowledgment, and follow the center's restroom procedure. Use the ten-minute break for any restroom needs to minimize time lost during active testing.

**6. Is it better to skip a difficult question or guess?**

Always guess rather than skip. The SAT has no penalty for incorrect answers, so an unanswered question guarantees zero points while a guess has positive expected value. If you cannot eliminate any options, guess randomly. If you can eliminate one or two options, your expected value from guessing among the remaining options improves significantly.

**7. What should I do during the ten-minute break?**

Eat your snack, drink water, stretch or walk briefly, use the restroom if needed, and perform a mental reset. Do not check your phone, discuss the test with other students, or attempt to recall and review Reading and Writing answers.

**8. What if Bluebook crashes during my test?**

Raise your hand immediately and notify the proctor. Bluebook is designed to preserve your session and answers even through application restarts. Do not panic; follow the proctor's instructions and allow the testing center staff to resolve the technical issue.

**9. How does the adaptive structure affect how I should approach each module?**

Approach every module with full focus and effort. Module 1 performance determines which Module 2 you receive, so treat Module 1 seriously. Regardless of which Module 2 you receive (high or standard difficulty), apply the same pacing and strategy approach. Do not change your approach based on your perception of Module 2's difficulty.

**10. Should I eat during the test itself?**

No. Food and drinks are not permitted at your desk unless you have a specific accommodation. Save your snack for the break. If you have an approved food accommodation, follow the proctor's guidance on when and how to use it.

**11. What if another student is being disruptive during the test?**

Raise your hand and quietly notify the proctor. Do not attempt to address the other student yourself. The proctor is responsible for maintaining the testing environment.

**12. Can I review my answers after finishing all questions in a module?**

Yes. The flagging system and the ability to navigate back to previous questions within a module allows you to review and change answers. You can review any question in the module during the time allocated for that module. Once the module time expires or you close the module, you cannot return to it.

**13. What should I do if I think I performed poorly after the test?**

Wait for your scores before drawing conclusions. Post-test feelings of uncertainty are extremely common, even among students who perform well. The perception of difficulty during the test is not an accurate predictor of score. Review your scores when they are released and make retake decisions based on actual data rather than in-the-moment feelings.

**14. Can I cancel my scores after seeing them?**

The standard score cancellation window requires the request before scores are released. Once scores are available in your account, cancellation is generally no longer an option. If you choose not to send certain scores to colleges, score choice allows you to select which test dates' scores to share without cancelling them from your record.

**15. How many times can I retake the SAT?**

The College Board permits students to take the SAT as many times as they wish. There is no official limit on the number of test attempts. However, most college counselors recommend no more than three to four attempts before the marginal benefit of additional testing diminishes. Each retake requires a registration fee (or an approved fee waiver), adequate preparation time, and an available test date.

**16. What is superscore and how does it affect my retake strategy?**

Superscoring is a practice used by many colleges in which they take your highest Math section score from any sitting and your highest Reading and Writing section score from any sitting and combine them into a composite score. This means that if you scored 680 Math and 620 Reading and Writing on one date, and 650 Math and 700 Reading and Writing on a second date, your superscore would be 680 + 700 = 1380. Under superscoring policies, a retake has minimal downside risk because only your best performance in each section is used. Research whether your target colleges superscore before determining your retake strategy.

**17. What if I have test anxiety and it becomes overwhelming during the test?**

Apply controlled breathing immediately: four counts in through the nose, hold two, four counts out through the mouth. If necessary, flag the current question, close your eyes for ten seconds, and use the breathing technique before continuing. Severe test anxiety that is a documented condition may qualify you for an approved accommodation such as extended time or a separate testing room; speak with your school counselor about this option well before your scheduled test date.

**18. How do I access my detailed score report to plan for a retake?**

Your detailed score report is available through your College Board account once scores are released. It includes section scores, subscores by question category (for both Reading and Writing and Math), cross-test scores, and question-level performance information. Review every component of the report systematically and use the subscores to identify the specific content areas most worth prioritizing in your retake preparation.
