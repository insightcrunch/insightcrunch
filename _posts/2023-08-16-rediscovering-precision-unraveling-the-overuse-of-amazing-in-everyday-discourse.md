When every meal, meeting, and minor gesture becomes "amazing," the word loses its power — and we lose our ability to actually describe the world around us.

## The inflation of superlatives

Language economists would call this superlative inflation. A word that once signaled genuine astonishment now signals polite acknowledgment. The casualty is precision — the ability to say something and mean it exactly.

## How it happened

The mechanism is social. "Amazing" is low-cost, broadly positive, and hard to argue with. It signals enthusiasm without committing to a specific claim. In conversation and especially in professional settings, this makes it attractive.

## What we lose

When everything is amazing, nothing is. More practically: when we can't describe why something is good, we can't reliably reproduce it or improve on it.

## The alternative

Specificity. "The structure of this argument is unusually clean" says more than "amazing presentation." It also teaches the other person something.
