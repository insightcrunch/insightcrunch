# SAT Math Preparation: The Complete Section Guide

The SAT Math section is where the majority of students leave the most points on the table. Unlike the Reading and Writing section, where improvement can feel slow and subjective, Math rewards structured preparation with measurable, predictable score gains. Every question has exactly one correct answer, every topic is learnable, and the path from a diagnostic score to your target score can be mapped with precision. This guide covers every single aspect of SAT Math preparation, from the foundational concepts tested to the advanced strategies that separate a 600 from a 750.

Whether you are just beginning your preparation or looking to push past a stubborn plateau, this guide is designed to serve as the only SAT Math resource you need to understand the section inside and out. It covers the adaptive module system, every content domain and tested topic, the Desmos calculator, time management, error analysis, and detailed score-level strategies for every range from below 500 to a perfect 800.

## Understanding the SAT Math Section Structure

The SAT Math section is the second of two main sections on the Digital SAT. It comes after the Reading and Writing section and a ten-minute break. The Math section consists of two modules, each containing 22 questions and lasting 35 minutes. That gives you a total of 44 questions across 70 minutes of testing time.

Every student begins with the same Module 1. Based on your performance in Module 1, the test routes you to either a harder or an easier Module 2. This adaptive system is the single most important structural feature of the Digital SAT, and understanding how it works is critical to your preparation strategy.

Unlike the old paper-based SAT, the Digital SAT allows you to use a calculator on every single math question. The built-in Desmos graphing calculator is available throughout both modules, and you can also bring your own approved calculator. There is no separate "no-calculator" section. This means that calculator fluency is now a core test-taking skill rather than an optional advantage.

The questions within each module are generally arranged in order of increasing difficulty, though this is not absolute. The first several questions tend to be straightforward, the middle questions require more thought, and the final questions are the most challenging. This difficulty progression matters for your pacing and flagging strategy, which we will cover in detail later.

The Math section tests content that students typically encounter through the end of Algebra II or the equivalent. It does not test calculus, matrices, or advanced statistics. The content is drawn from four domains, each with specific weightage that determines how many questions come from that domain.

## How the Adaptive Module System Works

The adaptive module system is the engine that determines your Math score, and it deserves its own detailed explanation because many students misunderstand how it works.

Here is the core mechanism: Module 1 is the same for every student. It contains a mix of questions across all difficulty levels and all four content domains. The test uses your performance on Module 1 to determine which version of Module 2 you receive.

If you perform well on Module 1, meaning you answer a sufficient number of questions correctly and demonstrate strong performance on the harder questions, you are routed to the harder version of Module 2. If your Module 1 performance is below that threshold, you are routed to the easier version of Module 2.

The critical insight is this: your scoring ceiling is determined by which Module 2 you receive. If you are routed to the easier Module 2, there is a cap on how high your Math score can go, typically in the mid-600s range. Even if you answer every single question correctly in the easier Module 2, you cannot reach a 700 or above. Conversely, if you are routed to the harder Module 2, you have access to the full scoring range up to 800, but the questions are genuinely more difficult.

This has profound strategic implications. Module 1 is the gatekeeper. A careless error on an easy Module 1 question that causes you to miss the routing threshold can limit your entire Math score, regardless of how well you perform afterward. This is why the number one strategic priority for any student aiming above 650 is to maximize accuracy on Module 1, even if it means working slightly more slowly than feels comfortable.

The approximate routing threshold is not publicly disclosed by the College Board, but analysis of practice test data and student reports suggests that answering roughly 16 to 18 out of 22 questions correctly in Module 1 generally triggers the harder Module 2 routing. This is not a hard cutoff, as the scoring algorithm also considers the difficulty level of the specific questions you answered correctly, but it provides a useful benchmark.

When you are taking the test and you transition from Module 1 to Module 2, you will notice the difficulty shift. If the questions suddenly feel noticeably harder, that is a good sign. It means you have been routed to the harder module, which gives you access to higher scores. If Module 2 feels about the same difficulty or easier than Module 1, it likely means you received the easier routing, and your scoring ceiling is limited.

For preparation purposes, this means you should practice under conditions that simulate both routing scenarios. Take practice tests where you challenge yourself with harder questions (simulating the harder Module 2) and also practice the foundational questions thoroughly (ensuring Module 1 accuracy).

## The Four Content Domains

The SAT Math section draws from four content domains, each with a specific weightage that determines how many of the 44 total questions come from that domain.

Algebra accounts for approximately 13 to 15 questions, making it the most heavily tested domain. This includes linear equations, systems of equations, linear inequalities, and linear functions. If you master Algebra thoroughly, you have a strong foundation for roughly a third of all Math questions.

Advanced Math accounts for approximately 13 to 15 questions as well, making it co-equal with Algebra in terms of representation. This domain covers quadratic equations, polynomial functions, exponential expressions, rational expressions, and nonlinear systems. These questions tend to be more challenging than Algebra questions and disproportionately appear in the harder portions of each module.

Problem-Solving and Data Analysis accounts for approximately 5 to 7 questions. This domain covers ratios, percentages, proportional reasoning, statistics, probability, and data interpretation. While it has fewer questions than Algebra or Advanced Math, this domain is where many students make avoidable errors because the questions often require careful reading and interpretation rather than pure calculation.

Geometry and Trigonometry accounts for approximately 5 to 7 questions. This domain covers area, volume, lines, angles, triangles, circles, trigonometric ratios, and coordinate geometry. Despite having the fewest questions, this domain includes some of the most time-consuming problems on the test, particularly those involving multi-step geometric reasoning or coordinate geometry with circles.

Understanding this distribution is essential for prioritizing your study time. If you are aiming for a score improvement of 50 to 100 points, investing heavily in Algebra and Advanced Math gives you the best return because those domains represent the most questions.

## Domain 1: Algebra

Algebra is the backbone of the SAT Math section. The questions in this domain test your ability to work with linear relationships in multiple representations: equations, graphs, tables, and word problems. Mastery of Algebra is the single most impactful investment you can make for your SAT Math score because it provides the foundation for approximately one-third of all questions and because many Advanced Math concepts build directly on algebraic skills.

### Linear Equations in One Variable

The most fundamental skill tested is solving linear equations in one variable. These range from simple one-step equations to multi-step equations with variables on both sides, fractions, and decimals. The College Board frequently tests your ability to recognize when an equation has no solution (the variable cancels out and you are left with a false statement like 3 = 7) or infinitely many solutions (the variable cancels out and you are left with a true statement like 4 = 4).

For example, you might encounter an equation like 3(2x - 4) = 6x + k and be asked to find the value of k that makes the equation have no solution or infinitely many solutions. The approach is to distribute, collect terms, and analyze what happens when the variable terms are identical on both sides.

The fastest approach for these questions is to simplify both sides completely before attempting to solve. Distribute first, combine like terms, and then move variables to one side. Resist the temptation to skip steps mentally, as this is where careless errors creep in, particularly on questions that seem easy.

A common trap is providing an answer that is the result of a sign error. The College Board knows that students frequently make errors with negative signs, so the answer choices often include both the correct answer and the answer you would get if you made a single sign error. Always double-check sign work on equations involving subtraction and distribution of negatives.

### Creating and Interpreting Linear Models

Beyond solving equations, the SAT frequently requires you to create linear models from word problems. This means reading a scenario, identifying the independent and dependent variables, determining the slope (rate of change) and y-intercept (starting value), and writing the equation.

Consider a scenario: a phone plan charges a monthly base fee plus a per-gigabyte charge for data. If the total cost is $35 for 2 GB and $55 for 6 GB, you need to find the per-gigabyte rate (slope) and the base fee (y-intercept). The slope is (55-35)/(6-2) = 20/4 = 5, meaning $5 per GB. Plugging back in: 35 = 5(2) + b, so b = 25. The model is C = 5g + 25, where C is cost and g is gigabytes.

The SAT also tests your ability to interpret given linear models. If told that the equation P = 12,000 + 850t models a town's population, you should recognize that 12,000 is the initial population and 850 is the annual growth in people. Questions might ask what happens to the graph if the initial population were larger (the line shifts up) or if the growth rate decreased (the slope becomes less steep).

A particularly tricky variation involves interpreting the slope and intercept of regression lines in scatter plot contexts. If a line of best fit for a study of hours studied versus test score is y = 5.2x + 42, the slope of 5.2 means that for each additional hour studied, the predicted test score increases by 5.2 points. The y-intercept of 42 represents the predicted test score for a student who studied zero hours. On the SAT, you should be prepared to identify which interpretation is appropriate given the context, and to recognize when the y-intercept is meaningful versus when it is merely a mathematical artifact.

### Linear Equations in Two Variables

These questions test your understanding of the relationship between an equation and its graph. The three standard forms you must be fluent with are slope-intercept form (y = mx + b), point-slope form (y - y1 = m(x - x1)), and standard form (Ax + By = C). You should be able to convert between all three forms quickly and accurately.

The slope of a line represents the rate of change, and the y-intercept represents the initial value or starting point. The SAT frequently asks you to interpret these values in real-world contexts. For example, if a question describes a scenario where a plumber charges a flat fee plus an hourly rate, the hourly rate is the slope and the flat fee is the y-intercept.

Parallel lines have identical slopes. Perpendicular lines have slopes that are negative reciprocals of each other. These relationships are tested both algebraically (given two equations, determine if the lines are parallel or perpendicular) and graphically (given a graph, identify the equation that represents a parallel or perpendicular line).

### Systems of Two Linear Equations

Systems of equations appear frequently and can be solved using three methods: substitution, elimination, and graphing. You should be comfortable with all three and know when each is most efficient.

Substitution is best when one equation is already solved for a variable or can be easily rearranged. Elimination is best when the coefficients of one variable are identical or can be made identical with simple multiplication. Graphing (using Desmos) is best when you want to verify an answer quickly or when the algebraic approach seems cumbersome.

The College Board tests three scenarios: systems with exactly one solution (the lines intersect at a single point), systems with no solution (the lines are parallel and never intersect), and systems with infinitely many solutions (the lines are identical). You should be able to identify each scenario both algebraically and graphically.

To determine the number of solutions algebraically without solving, compare the slopes. If the slopes are different, there is exactly one solution. If the slopes are the same and the y-intercepts are different, there is no solution. If the slopes and y-intercepts are both the same, there are infinitely many solutions.

### Linear Inequalities

Linear inequalities follow the same rules as linear equations with one critical addition: when you multiply or divide both sides by a negative number, you must flip the inequality sign. This rule is tested in various ways, and forgetting to flip the sign is one of the most common errors on the SAT.

Graphing linear inequalities on the coordinate plane requires understanding when to use a solid line (for "less than or equal to" and "greater than or equal to") versus a dashed line (for strict "less than" and "greater than"), and which side of the line to shade. The Desmos calculator can be very helpful for visualizing inequality solutions.

### Word Problems Involving Linear Relationships

Many Algebra questions present real-world scenarios that you must translate into equations or systems. The key skill is identifying what the variables represent, what the slope means in context, and what the y-intercept means in context.

Common word problem types include mixture problems (mixing two solutions with different concentrations), rate problems (two people working at different speeds), cost problems (fixed cost plus variable cost per unit), and distance problems (distance equals rate times time). For each type, there is a standard setup that you should practice until it becomes automatic.

The most common mistake on word problems is misidentifying what the question is actually asking. Students often solve for x when the question asks for 2x + 3, or they solve for the variable correctly but forget to plug it back into the expression the question specifies. Always re-read the question after solving to confirm you are providing what was asked.

## Domain 2: Advanced Math

Advanced Math is where the SAT separates mid-range scorers from high scorers. The questions in this domain test your ability to work with nonlinear relationships, and they tend to appear disproportionately in the harder portions of each module. If you are aiming above 650, proficiency in this domain is non-negotiable.

### Quadratic Equations

Quadratic equations are the heart of the Advanced Math domain. You need to be proficient with three solving methods: factoring, the quadratic formula, and completing the square. You should know when each method is most efficient.

Factoring is the fastest method when the quadratic can be factored easily. Look for two numbers that multiply to give the constant term (c) and add to give the coefficient of the linear term (b) in the standard form ax^2 + bx + c. Special factoring patterns include the difference of squares (a^2 - b^2 = (a+b)(a-b)) and perfect square trinomials (a^2 + 2ab + b^2 = (a+b)^2).

The quadratic formula (x = (-b +/- sqrt(b^2 - 4ac)) / 2a) works for every quadratic equation, including those that cannot be factored. The discriminant (b^2 - 4ac) tells you about the nature of the roots: if it is positive, there are two distinct real roots; if it is zero, there is exactly one real root (a repeated root); if it is negative, there are no real roots. The SAT frequently asks about the discriminant directly, either asking you to calculate it or asking what conditions on a parameter would produce a specific number of solutions.

Completing the square is the method used to convert a quadratic from standard form (y = ax^2 + bx + c) to vertex form (y = a(x-h)^2 + k), where (h, k) is the vertex of the parabola. This conversion is tested both as a standalone skill and as a step in solving circle equations.

### Polynomial Functions

Beyond quadratics, the SAT tests your understanding of polynomial functions more broadly. You should understand the relationship between the zeros (or roots) of a polynomial and its factors. If x = r is a zero of a polynomial, then (x - r) is a factor. This relationship is tested in both directions: given the zeros, write the factored form, and given the factored form, identify the zeros.

The remainder theorem states that if a polynomial p(x) is divided by (x - r), the remainder is p(r). This means you can evaluate a polynomial at a specific value to find the remainder of the division, which is often faster than performing polynomial long division.

The end behavior of polynomial functions depends on the degree and leading coefficient. For even-degree polynomials with a positive leading coefficient, both ends of the graph point upward. For odd-degree polynomials with a positive leading coefficient, the left end points down and the right end points up. These patterns are reversed when the leading coefficient is negative.

### Exponential Functions and Growth/Decay

Exponential functions model situations where a quantity grows or decays by a constant percentage over equal time intervals. The general form is f(x) = a * b^x, where a is the initial value and b is the growth factor. If b is greater than 1, the function represents exponential growth. If b is between 0 and 1, the function represents exponential decay.

The SAT frequently tests your ability to interpret exponential functions in context. For example, if a population doubles every 5 hours, the growth factor over 5 hours is 2, and the function can be written as P(t) = P_0 * 2^(t/5). Understanding how to set up and interpret these functions is more important than performing complex calculations.

Compound interest problems are a specific application of exponential growth. The formula A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt) appears in various forms, and you should understand what each variable represents: P is the principal, r is the annual interest rate, n is the number of compounding periods per time unit, and t is the number of time units.

A common SAT question pattern involves interpreting exponential models. For example, if a population model is P(t) = 500(1.03)^t, you should recognize that 500 is the initial population and 1.03 represents a 3% growth rate per time period. If the question changes the model to P(t) = 500(1.03)^(t/12), the growth is now 3% over every 12 time units, which means the effective rate per single time unit is much smaller. Understanding how changes to the exponent affect the rate of growth is essential for the harder questions in this domain.

The SAT also tests your ability to recognize when a scenario involves exponential versus linear growth. The key distinction is that linear growth adds a constant amount per time period while exponential growth multiplies by a constant factor per time period. A bank account that earns $50 per month is linear. A bank account that earns 2% per month is exponential. Many word problems on the SAT are designed to test whether you can identify which model is appropriate.

### Radical Equations and Expressions

Radical equations contain a variable under a radical sign. To solve them, isolate the radical on one side and then square both sides (or raise both sides to the appropriate power). The critical step that many students forget is checking for extraneous solutions. Squaring both sides of an equation can introduce solutions that do not satisfy the original equation, so you must substitute every solution back into the original to verify.

For example, to solve sqrt(2x + 3) = x - 1, you would square both sides to get 2x + 3 = (x-1)^2 = x^2 - 2x + 1. Rearranging gives x^2 - 4x - 2 = 0. Using the quadratic formula yields two solutions. You must check both in the original equation because one of them may produce a negative value under the radical or may not satisfy the equation.

The SAT also tests simplification of radical expressions. You should be comfortable simplifying expressions like sqrt(50) = 5*sqrt(2), rationalizing denominators (multiplying numerator and denominator by the conjugate or the radical), and working with fractional exponents (x^(1/2) = sqrt(x), x^(2/3) = (cube root of x)^2).

### Rational Expressions and Equations

Rational expressions involve fractions with polynomials in the numerator and denominator. The SAT tests your ability to simplify, add, subtract, multiply, and divide rational expressions. The key skills are factoring the numerator and denominator to identify common factors that can be canceled, finding common denominators to add or subtract, and cross-multiplying to solve rational equations.

When solving rational equations, you must check for extraneous solutions. A value that makes the denominator zero is not a valid solution, even if it satisfies the equation algebraically. The SAT tests this concept directly by including extraneous solutions among the answer choices.

### Function Transformations

Understanding how algebraic changes to a function affect its graph is a core Advanced Math skill. The standard transformations include vertical translation (adding a constant outside the function), horizontal translation (adding a constant inside the function argument), vertical stretch/compression (multiplying the function by a constant), horizontal stretch/compression (multiplying the input by a constant), and reflection (multiplying by negative one, either inside or outside the function).

The key insight that many students miss is that horizontal transformations work in the opposite direction from what seems intuitive. Adding a positive number inside the function (replacing x with x - h where h is positive) shifts the graph to the right, not to the left. Multiplying x by a number greater than 1 compresses the graph horizontally rather than stretching it.

### Composition of Functions

Function composition, where you evaluate one function inside another, is tested on the SAT in both notation (f(g(x))) and applied contexts. The approach is straightforward: work from the inside out. First evaluate the inner function, then use that result as the input for the outer function.

## Domain 3: Problem-Solving and Data Analysis

This domain tests your ability to work with quantitative data in real-world contexts. While it has fewer questions than Algebra or Advanced Math, the questions in this domain often require more careful reading and interpretation, and careless errors are common.

### Ratios and Proportional Relationships

Ratio and proportion questions test your ability to set up and solve proportional relationships. The fundamental skill is cross-multiplication: if a/b = c/d, then ad = bc. Beyond this basic setup, you should be comfortable with unit rates (how much per one unit), scaling (if the ratio is 3:5 and the total is 80, how much goes to each part), and converting between different units using proportional reasoning.

The SAT frequently embeds ratio questions in word problems that require you to identify the correct relationship before setting up the proportion. A common trap is setting up the proportion with the numbers in the wrong positions, leading to an answer that is the reciprocal of the correct answer.

### Percentages

Percentage questions test three core calculations: finding a percentage of a number, finding what percentage one number is of another, and finding the original number when given the percentage and the result. Beyond these basics, the SAT heavily tests percent increase and percent decrease.

The percent change formula is: percent change = (new value - original value) / original value * 100. The critical detail is that the denominator is always the original value, not the new value. Many students confuse this, and the College Board exploits this confusion by including the incorrectly calculated answer among the choices.

Successive percentage changes are a frequent source of errors. If a price increases by 20% and then decreases by 20%, the final price is not equal to the original price. It is actually 4% less than the original. This is because the 20% decrease is applied to the increased price, not the original. To handle successive changes, multiply the growth/decay factors: 1.20 * 0.80 = 0.96, which represents a 4% decrease overall.

### Statistics: Measures of Center and Spread

The SAT tests your understanding of mean, median, and mode, with particular emphasis on when each is appropriate and how outliers affect them.

The mean (average) is sensitive to outliers. A single very large or very small value can pull the mean significantly in that direction. The median (middle value when data is ordered) is resistant to outliers and is a better measure of center for skewed distributions.

Standard deviation is tested conceptually rather than computationally. You will never need to calculate a standard deviation on the SAT. Instead, you need to understand that standard deviation measures how spread out the data is from the mean. A larger standard deviation means the data is more spread out; a smaller standard deviation means the data is more clustered around the mean. When comparing two datasets, you should be able to identify which has a larger standard deviation by visually inspecting the spread.

### Probability

Basic probability questions ask you to find the likelihood of an event occurring. Probability is always between 0 and 1 (or equivalently, between 0% and 100%), where 0 means the event is impossible and 1 means the event is certain.

Conditional probability questions ask for the probability of one event given that another event has already occurred. These often involve two-way frequency tables, where you restrict your attention to a specific row or column and calculate the probability within that restricted group.

The SAT tests the concept of independence conceptually: two events are independent if the occurrence of one does not affect the probability of the other. Mathematically, A and B are independent if P(A given B) = P(A).

A worked example illustrates conditional probability with two-way tables. Suppose a table shows survey results of 200 students categorized by grade (10th or 11th) and whether they prefer online or in-person classes. If 60 of the 120 tenth-graders prefer online and 30 of the 80 eleventh-graders prefer online, then the probability that a randomly selected student prefers online, given that they are a tenth-grader, is 60/120 = 0.50. But the probability that a randomly selected student prefers online, given that they are an eleventh-grader, is 30/80 = 0.375. Since these conditional probabilities differ, grade and class preference are not independent in this dataset.

The key skill with two-way tables is identifying the correct denominator. For a conditional probability, the denominator is always the total for the condition (the row total or column total), not the grand total. This distinction is the source of many errors.

### Making Inferences From Studies

Understanding the limits of conclusions you can draw from different study types is a newer emphasis area on the SAT. There are three key principles to remember.

Random sampling allows generalizations from the sample to the population. If a study randomly selects participants from a city, the results can be generalized to that city. If participants are self-selected (they volunteer), the results may not generalize.

Random assignment allows causal conclusions. If a study randomly assigns participants to treatment and control groups, differences in outcomes can be attributed to the treatment. If there is no random assignment, you can only identify an association, not a cause-and-effect relationship.

The scope of inference is limited by both the sampling and the assignment. A well-designed experiment on randomly selected college students can establish causal relationships within the college student population, but not necessarily for the general population.

The SAT tests these principles by presenting study descriptions and asking which conclusion is valid. The trap answers typically overstate what the study can conclude (claiming causation from an observational study, or generalizing beyond the sampled population).

### Data Interpretation

You will encounter various types of data displays: bar graphs, line graphs, histograms, scatter plots, dot plots, box plots, and tables. For each type, you should be able to read values accurately, identify trends, make comparisons, and draw appropriate conclusions.

Scatter plots with lines of best fit require you to interpret the slope (the rate of change of the dependent variable per unit change in the independent variable) and the y-intercept (the predicted value of the dependent variable when the independent variable is zero) in context. You should also understand the concept of residuals (the difference between the actual value and the predicted value) at a conceptual level.

Two-way frequency tables are among the most commonly tested data displays. You should be able to calculate joint frequencies, marginal frequencies, conditional frequencies, and relative frequencies from a two-way table. Many students struggle with these because the questions require careful attention to which row, column, or total serves as the denominator.

### Data Collection and Study Design

The SAT tests basic concepts of study design: random sampling ensures that the sample is representative of the population, random assignment allows researchers to establish cause-and-effect relationships, and the scope of inference is limited by the sampling method. If a study only surveys people in one city, the results can only be generalized to that city. If a study uses random assignment, it can establish causation; if it only uses observation, it can only establish association.

Margin of error is tested at a conceptual level. You should understand that a larger sample size generally produces a smaller margin of error, and that the margin of error tells you the range within which the true population parameter is likely to fall.

## Domain 4: Geometry and Trigonometry

Geometry and Trigonometry has the fewest questions on the SAT, but these questions can be among the most time-consuming. Students who are weak in this domain often spend disproportionate time on geometry questions, which hurts their performance on easier questions elsewhere.

### Lines and Angles

Complementary angles sum to 90 degrees. Supplementary angles sum to 180 degrees. Vertical angles are equal. When a transversal crosses parallel lines, it creates corresponding angles (equal), alternate interior angles (equal), alternate exterior angles (equal), and co-interior angles (supplementary). These relationships form the basis for many multi-step geometry problems.

### Triangles

The angles of a triangle always sum to 180 degrees. The SAT tests several triangle properties beyond this basic fact.

The Pythagorean theorem (a^2 + b^2 = c^2) applies to right triangles, where c is the hypotenuse. You should memorize the common Pythagorean triples: 3-4-5, 5-12-13, 8-15-17, and 7-24-25, as well as their multiples (6-8-10, 9-12-15, etc.).

Special right triangles appear frequently. In a 45-45-90 triangle, the sides are in the ratio 1:1:sqrt(2). In a 30-60-90 triangle, the sides are in the ratio 1:sqrt(3):2. These ratios allow you to find missing sides without using the Pythagorean theorem, which is often faster.

Similar triangles have proportional corresponding sides. If two triangles are similar, you can set up a proportion using corresponding sides to find an unknown length. The SAT tests similarity both in standard geometric contexts and in coordinate geometry settings.

The triangle inequality theorem states that the sum of any two sides must be greater than the third side. This theorem is occasionally tested directly but more often appears as a constraint in problem-solving contexts.

### Circles

Circle properties are a significant area of testing. You should know the formulas for circumference (C = 2*pi*r), area (A = pi*r^2), arc length (L = (theta/360)*2*pi*r for degrees or L = r*theta for radians), and sector area (A = (theta/360)*pi*r^2 for degrees or A = (1/2)*r^2*theta for radians).

The relationship between central angles and inscribed angles is tested: an inscribed angle is half the central angle that subtends the same arc. A tangent line to a circle is perpendicular to the radius at the point of tangency.

Circle equations in the coordinate plane come in two forms. Standard form is (x-h)^2 + (y-k)^2 = r^2, where (h,k) is the center and r is the radius. General form is x^2 + y^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0. Converting from general form to standard form requires completing the square for both x and y terms. This is one of the most challenging question types on the SAT and typically appears in the harder portions of each module.

Here is the step-by-step process for converting from general to standard form. Given x^2 + y^2 - 6x + 4y - 12 = 0, first group the x terms and y terms: (x^2 - 6x) + (y^2 + 4y) = 12. Next, complete the square for each group. For the x group: take half of -6 (which is -3) and square it (which is 9), adding 9 to both sides. For the y group: take half of 4 (which is 2) and square it (which is 4), adding 4 to both sides. The result is (x - 3)^2 + (y + 2)^2 = 25. The center is (3, -2) and the radius is 5. Practice this process until it is completely automatic, because it appears on nearly every SAT.

A related question type asks you to find the equation of a circle given its center and a point it passes through, or to determine whether a given point lies inside, on, or outside a circle. To check if a point lies on a circle, substitute the coordinates into the equation. If the left side equals the right side, the point is on the circle. If the left side is less than the right side, the point is inside. If greater, the point is outside.

### Area, Perimeter, and Multi-Step Geometry Problems

The SAT frequently combines multiple geometry concepts in a single problem. You might need to find the area of a shaded region, which requires computing the area of a larger shape and subtracting the area of a smaller shape cut from it. Or you might need to use properties of similar triangles to find a missing length before computing an area.

These multi-step problems are where students lose the most time, because each step introduces the possibility of error and the solution path is not always immediately obvious. The best approach is to identify what the question ultimately asks for, then work backward to determine what intermediate values you need. Label everything on the figure (or draw your own figure if one is not provided) and note all given information before attempting to calculate.

For three-dimensional figures, the SAT tests volume calculations for cylinders (V = pi*r^2*h), cones (V = (1/3)*pi*r^2*h), spheres (V = (4/3)*pi*r^3), and rectangular prisms (V = lwh). A common question type gives you the volume and one or two dimensions and asks you to find the remaining dimension. Another common type asks how doubling one dimension affects the total volume, which requires understanding the multiplicative relationships within the formulas.

### Volume and Surface Area

The SAT provides the formulas for volume of common 3D shapes on the reference sheet available during the test. These include the volume of a rectangular prism, cylinder, sphere, cone, and pyramid. While you do not need to memorize these formulas, you do need to be comfortable using them, particularly in multi-step problems where you might need to equate volumes, find dimensions given a volume, or calculate how a change in one dimension affects the total volume.

### Trigonometric Ratios

The SAT tests the basic trigonometric ratios: sine = opposite/hypotenuse, cosine = adjacent/hypotenuse, tangent = opposite/adjacent (SOH-CAH-TOA). These ratios apply to right triangles and allow you to find missing sides or angles.

The complementary angle relationship is important: sin(x) = cos(90 - x) and cos(x) = sin(90 - x). This means that the sine of an acute angle equals the cosine of its complement. The SAT tests this relationship directly.

You should also understand radian measure and be able to convert between degrees and radians. The key relationship is that 180 degrees equals pi radians. To convert from degrees to radians, multiply by pi/180. To convert from radians to degrees, multiply by 180/pi. Common radian values you should know without calculation include: 30 degrees = pi/6, 45 degrees = pi/4, 60 degrees = pi/3, 90 degrees = pi/2, and 360 degrees = 2*pi.

The unit circle is tested at a basic level. You should know the sine and cosine values for the standard angles (0, 30, 45, 60, and 90 degrees) and understand that the coordinate of a point on the unit circle at angle theta from the positive x-axis is (cos(theta), sin(theta)). The SAT does not require you to evaluate trig functions at arbitrary angles, but you should be comfortable with the standard reference angles and their values.

A practical tip for trigonometry questions: when you see a right triangle with an angle and side given, immediately label all three sides using SOH-CAH-TOA relationships. Even if the question only asks for one missing side, having all labels in place prevents confusion and makes it easy to set up the correct ratio on the first attempt. For example, if you are given a right triangle with an angle of 35 degrees and the side adjacent to that angle is 12, you can immediately write: adjacent = 12, opposite = 12*tan(35), hypotenuse = 12/cos(35). Then you select whichever calculation the question requires.

While the SAT does not go deep into trigonometry beyond basic ratios and unit circle awareness, you should be comfortable with these foundational concepts.

## The Desmos Graphing Calculator: When to Use It and When Not To

The built-in Desmos graphing calculator is one of the most powerful tools available to you during the SAT Math section. It is accessible on every question in both modules, and learning to use it effectively can save significant time and prevent calculation errors. However, it is not always the fastest approach, and knowing when to use Desmos versus when to work by hand is a critical test-taking skill.

### When Desmos Is the Fastest Approach

Desmos excels at graphing. If a question asks you to identify the equation of a line or curve given a graph, or if you need to find the intersection of two functions, graphing both functions and visually identifying the intersection point is often faster than solving algebraically. To find an intersection, type both equations and click on the intersection point that Desmos identifies.

Desmos is extremely useful for solving systems of equations, especially nonlinear systems. Type both equations, look for their intersections, and read off the coordinates. This is particularly valuable for systems involving a linear equation and a quadratic equation, where algebraic substitution can be tedious.

For quadratic equations, Desmos can identify the vertex, x-intercepts (zeros), and y-intercept instantly. If a question asks for the minimum or maximum value of a quadratic function, graphing it in Desmos and reading the vertex is often the fastest approach.

Desmos can verify your answers. If you solve an equation algebraically and want to confirm your answer, you can plug your solution back in using Desmos to check that both sides are equal. This takes only a few seconds and can prevent costly errors.

When you are stuck on a question and cannot see the algebraic path forward, Desmos often provides a visual approach. Graphing the functions or expressions involved can reveal relationships that are not obvious from the algebra alone.

### When Hand Calculation Is Faster

For simple arithmetic and basic equation solving, pulling up Desmos is slower than doing the calculation in your head or on scratch paper. If a question asks you to solve 2x + 7 = 15, just solve it. Do not graph it.

For percentage calculations, unit conversions, and basic proportion problems, Desmos does not add value. These are arithmetic-heavy questions where a standard calculator (if you brought one) or mental math is more efficient.

For geometry questions involving area, volume, or angle calculations, the graphing calculator is rarely helpful. These questions typically require applying formulas and performing arithmetic, which is faster by hand.

### Specific Desmos Techniques to Practice

You should practice these specific techniques before test day so that they are automatic during the test. Type equations using the standard Desmos syntax. Use the zoom and window adjustment to see the relevant portion of the graph. Use the table feature to generate input-output pairs for a function. Use the slider feature to explore how changing a parameter affects the graph. Use the intersection tool by clicking on the point where two graphs cross.

### Advanced Desmos Techniques for Hard Questions

Beyond the basics, there are several advanced Desmos techniques that can turn difficult questions into straightforward ones.

For questions that ask how many solutions a system has, graph both equations and count the intersection points. This is often faster than algebraic analysis, especially for nonlinear systems where one equation is quadratic and the other is linear.

For optimization questions (finding the maximum or minimum value), graph the function and use Desmos to identify the peak or valley. For quadratic functions, the vertex gives you the maximum or minimum. For more complex functions, visually scanning the graph is sufficient.

For questions involving parameters (such as "for what value of k does the system have exactly one solution"), use the slider feature. Type the equation with k as a variable, create a slider for k, and adjust the slider until the desired condition is met (one intersection, no intersection, tangent to a line, etc.). This technique is remarkably powerful and can solve questions that would require complex algebra in seconds.

For verifying grid-in answers, type the original equation and substitute your answer. For example, if you solved for x = 7 in an equation, type the left side and the right side as separate expressions and check that they produce the same value when x = 7. This verification takes about 10 seconds and prevents costly errors on student-produced response questions.

For questions involving absolute value equations or inequalities, graphing both sides and looking at the intersection points is often the clearest approach. Absolute value questions can have zero, one, or two solutions depending on the setup, and visualizing the V-shape of the absolute value function makes the number of solutions immediately apparent.

### Desmos Practice Before Test Day

You should spend at least one full practice session (30 to 45 minutes) exclusively practicing with the Desmos calculator before your test date. Access the online version of Desmos (which is the same interface available in Bluebook) and practice the following exercises: graph a system of two linear equations and find the intersection, graph a quadratic and identify its vertex and zeros, graph a circle equation and identify its center and radius, use sliders to explore the effect of a parameter on an equation, and use the table feature to generate values for a function. The more familiar you are with the interface, the faster you will be on test day.

## Question Types: Multiple Choice vs Student-Produced Response

The SAT Math section contains two types of questions: multiple-choice questions with four answer choices and student-produced response questions (also called grid-ins) where you type your own answer.

Approximately 75% of questions are multiple choice and 25% are student-produced response. The distribution is roughly the same in both modules.

### Multiple-Choice Strategy

For multiple-choice questions, you always have four options, and there is exactly one correct answer. The primary strategy is to solve the problem, identify your answer, and select it. However, when you are unsure of the algebraic approach, you can work backwards from the answer choices. This is particularly effective for questions where the answer choices are simple numbers, as you can test each choice to see which one satisfies the given conditions.

Process of elimination is valuable when you can rule out obviously wrong answers. For example, if a question asks for a positive value and two of the four choices are negative, you can immediately eliminate those two and focus on the remaining options.

Estimation is another powerful tool. If you can approximate the answer, you can often eliminate answer choices that are too large or too small, narrowing your options to one or two plausible candidates.

### Student-Produced Response Strategy

For student-produced response questions, you type your answer as a number, fraction, or decimal. There are no answer choices to guide you, which means you cannot work backwards or use process of elimination.

Important rules for grid-in responses: you can enter fractions (like 3/7) or decimals (like 0.4286), and you do not need to simplify fractions. If the answer is a decimal that does not terminate, you should enter as many decimal places as the response field allows or use the fraction form. Negative numbers can be entered by typing the negative sign. Mixed numbers should be entered as improper fractions or decimals (type 7/3 rather than 2 1/3, because the system may interpret 2 1/3 as 21/3).

A critical grid-in tip: if a question can have multiple correct answers (for example, "find a value of x that satisfies..."), any correct value will receive credit. In these cases, choose the simplest correct value.

There is no penalty for guessing on either question type. You should always enter an answer, even if you are unsure.

## Time Management Strategy for Math Modules

You have 35 minutes for 22 questions in each module, which works out to approximately 1 minute and 35 seconds per question on average. However, not all questions take the same amount of time. Easy questions might take 30 seconds, while hard questions might take 3 minutes or more. Your time management strategy should account for this variation.

### The Two-Pass Approach

The most effective time management strategy is a two-pass approach. On the first pass, work through the module from beginning to end, solving every question you can solve confidently within a reasonable time frame (roughly 90 seconds to 2 minutes). If a question does not click within that time frame, flag it and move on. Do not leave it blank; enter your best guess before flagging, so that you have something entered in case you run out of time during the review phase.

On the second pass, return to the flagged questions and work on them with whatever time remains. Prioritize questions that you think you can solve with a bit more thought over questions that seem completely unfamiliar.

### Pacing Benchmarks

Use these benchmarks to gauge whether you are on pace during the module. After 10 minutes, you should have completed approximately 6 to 7 questions. After 20 minutes, you should have completed approximately 13 to 14 questions. After 30 minutes, you should have completed approximately 19 to 20 questions, leaving 5 minutes for review.

If you find yourself significantly behind these benchmarks, consider guessing on the hardest remaining questions and focusing your time on questions where you have the best chance of getting the right answer.

### Module 1 vs Module 2 Pacing

Your pacing strategy should differ slightly between Module 1 and Module 2 because of the adaptive system. In Module 1, accuracy is paramount. It is better to work slightly slowly and get 19 questions right than to rush and get 17 right, because that difference could determine your routing. In Module 2, you can be more aggressive with your pacing, especially if you received the harder Module 2 and know that some questions are genuinely difficult.

## Common Trap Answer Patterns

The College Board uses several recurring trap answer patterns in the Math section. Recognizing these patterns can help you avoid common mistakes.

The sign error trap places an answer choice that results from a single sign mistake. If the correct answer is -3, you will often find +3 among the choices. Always check signs carefully, especially after distributing negatives or subtracting.

The partial solution trap provides an intermediate value rather than the final answer. If the question asks for 3x + 2 and you solve for x = 4, the trap answer is 4 while the correct answer is 14. Always re-read the question to confirm what is being asked.

The reciprocal trap provides the inverse of the correct answer. If the correct answer to a rate problem is 3/5, you might find 5/3 among the choices. This is common in ratio and proportion questions where students set up the fraction upside down.

The misread axis trap appears in data interpretation questions. The answer choices include values read from the wrong axis, wrong scale, or wrong data series. Always label what you are reading from a graph before selecting an answer.

The extraneous solution trap includes solutions that satisfy the algebraic equation but violate a constraint in the original problem (such as making a denominator zero or producing a negative under a square root). Always check solutions in the original equation, not just the simplified version.

## The Flagging and Review Strategy

The Digital SAT's flagging feature allows you to mark questions for review. Used strategically, this feature can significantly improve your score by ensuring you spend your time where it matters most.

Flag any question that takes more than 90 seconds without progress. Enter your best guess, flag it, and move on. This prevents the common trap of spending 4 to 5 minutes on a single hard question while leaving easy points on the table at the end of the module.

Flag any question where you selected an answer but feel uncertain. During your review pass, these questions deserve a second look. Sometimes a fresh perspective reveals an approach you missed the first time.

Do not flag questions you are confident about, even if they were difficult. Reviewing questions you already answered correctly wastes valuable time that could be spent on questions you actually need to reconsider.

During your review pass, check flagged questions in order of perceived difficulty, starting with the ones you think you are most likely to solve. This maximizes the probability of picking up additional points with your remaining time.

## Error Analysis Framework

After every practice test, you should analyze your errors using a structured framework. This is the single most valuable thing you can do between practice tests, and it is far more important than simply taking another practice test.

Categorize every incorrect answer into one of five error types.

Content gap errors occur when you did not know the concept, formula, or method needed to solve the question. The remedy is targeted study of that specific topic.

Procedural errors occur when you knew the approach but made a calculation or algebraic mistake. The remedy is more careful work and double-checking procedures.

Misread errors occur when you misunderstood the question, missed a key word, or solved for the wrong thing. The remedy is training yourself to re-read the question before selecting your answer.

Time pressure errors occur when you ran out of time or rushed through a question you could have solved with more time. The remedy is improving your pacing strategy and decision-making about when to skip.

Trap answer errors occur when you fell for a deliberately designed wrong answer. The remedy is learning the specific trap patterns (described above) and developing the habit of verifying your answer against the original question.

Track your errors over multiple practice tests. If you consistently make procedural errors in Advanced Math, that tells you something different than if you consistently have content gaps in Geometry. The pattern reveals where to focus your study time for maximum improvement.

### How to Conduct an Effective Error Review

The error review process should take approximately 30 to 45 minutes after each practice test. Here is the step-by-step process that produces the best results.

First, go through every question you answered incorrectly. For each wrong answer, write down the question topic (linear equations, quadratics, probability, etc.), the error type (content gap, procedural, misread, time pressure, or trap answer), and a one-sentence explanation of what went wrong. Do not simply note that you got it wrong. Be specific: "I forgot to flip the inequality sign when dividing by a negative" or "I solved for x but the question asked for 2x - 1" or "I ran out of time and guessed."

Second, go through every question you answered correctly but flagged as uncertain. These near-misses are almost as valuable as actual errors because they represent areas of fragile understanding. If you guessed correctly on a probability question, that question is still a warning sign that your probability skills need work.

Third, look for patterns across questions. Are your errors concentrated in one domain? Are most of them the same error type? Are they clustered at the end of the module (suggesting time pressure) or distributed throughout (suggesting content issues)?

Fourth, create an action plan. For each error pattern you identified, write a specific corrective action. "Study circle equations for 30 minutes tomorrow" is a good action. "Get better at math" is not a useful action. The more specific your plan, the more likely you are to follow through.

Finally, keep a running list of your most frequent error types across multiple tests. After three or four practice tests, you will have a clear picture of your two or three biggest weakness areas. These are the areas that deserve the most study time going forward, because eliminating a recurring error pattern produces a predictable and repeatable score increase.

## Score-Level Strategy: Below 500

If your diagnostic Math score is below 500, your primary task is building foundational fluency. At this level, the majority of your errors are likely content gaps, meaning there are topics you either have not learned or have forgotten.

Focus exclusively on Algebra and basic arithmetic. Before touching any other domain, make sure you can confidently solve linear equations in one variable, graph linear equations, solve simple systems, and work with basic percentages and ratios. These skills account for a large number of questions and represent the fastest path to score improvement.

Build arithmetic fluency. Many students scoring below 500 lose time and make errors on basic calculations: fraction operations, decimal conversions, order of operations, and negative number arithmetic. Spending time shoring up these foundational skills pays dividends across every question type.

Use the Desmos calculator for every calculation. At this score level, calculator dependence is not a weakness; it is a strategy. The calculator prevents arithmetic errors and frees your cognitive resources for understanding the problem setup.

Practice with easy and medium questions only. Do not attempt hard questions yet. Your goal is to answer every easy question correctly and most medium questions correctly. The points gained from mastering easy questions are far more valuable than the points you might gain from occasionally getting a hard question right.

Set a realistic timeline. Moving from below 500 to the 550-600 range typically takes 8 to 12 weeks of consistent daily practice (30 to 60 minutes per day). The improvement is achievable but requires sustained effort.

## Score-Level Strategy: 500 to 600

If your diagnostic Math score is in the 500 to 600 range, you have the basics but need to fill specific content gaps and reduce careless errors.

Identify your specific weak topics using practice test error analysis. At this level, your errors are typically a mix of content gaps (especially in Advanced Math) and procedural mistakes. Targeted study of your weakest 3 to 5 topics will produce the most improvement per hour of study.

Master the most heavily tested Algebra concepts. Systems of equations, slope and y-intercept interpretation, and linear word problems are all high-frequency, learnable topics. Ensure you can solve these question types consistently before moving to less frequently tested topics.

Begin building Advanced Math skills. Quadratic equations (factoring, quadratic formula, discriminant), exponential growth/decay, and function notation are the highest-priority topics in this domain for students in the 500-600 range.

Start developing Desmos proficiency beyond basic arithmetic. Learn to graph equations, find intersections, and use the calculator to verify solutions. These skills become increasingly valuable as you encounter harder questions.

Reduce careless errors through deliberate practice. For every practice set, track how many questions you got wrong despite knowing the correct approach. If this number is consistently above zero, you have a process problem rather than a knowledge problem. Slow down slightly, double-check key calculations, and re-read the question before selecting your answer.

The typical timeline for moving from the 500-600 range to the 600-650 range is 6 to 10 weeks of focused daily practice.

## Score-Level Strategy: 600 to 700

If your diagnostic Math score is in the 600 to 700 range, you have solid fundamentals and need to master the harder question types, optimize your Module 1 performance, and eliminate the subtle errors that are holding you back.

At this level, Module 1 accuracy becomes critical. You should be aiming for 19+ correct out of 22 in Module 1 to ensure you are routed to the harder Module 2 and have access to the full scoring range. This means treating every Module 1 question with maximum care, especially the first 15 questions which tend to be easier and should be near-automatic for a 600+ scorer.

Master the harder Advanced Math topics. Completing the square, function transformations, polynomial factor/zero relationships, and rational expressions are the topics that most frequently separate 600-level scorers from 700-level scorers. These topics require deeper conceptual understanding rather than just procedural fluency.

Develop fluency with Geometry and Trigonometry. Circle equations, coordinate geometry, trigonometric ratios, and special right triangles are the areas where 600-level scorers most often lose points. These topics are learnable but require dedicated practice because they come up less frequently and students tend to deprioritize them.

Refine your error analysis to focus on trap answers and misread errors. At the 600-700 level, most of your errors are not content gaps but rather strategic mistakes: falling for designed trap answers, solving for the wrong variable, or misinterpreting a graph. These errors are eliminated through awareness and deliberate checking habits rather than additional content study.

The typical timeline for moving from the 600-700 range to the 700+ range is 4 to 8 weeks of focused daily practice, with the emphasis shifting from content learning to strategic refinement.

## Score-Level Strategy: 700 to 800

If your diagnostic Math score is in the 700 to 800 range, you are already in elite territory and the path to a perfect or near-perfect score requires eliminating the last few error patterns with surgical precision.

At this level, every question you get wrong is precious diagnostic data. You should be able to identify exactly why you missed each question and implement a specific fix. The most common error types for 700+ scorers are: careless mistakes on easy questions (often caused by overconfidence and rushing), trap answers on medium questions where the first instinct leads to the wrong choice, and content gaps on the hardest question types (often in obscure Geometry topics or unusual Advanced Math formulations).

Practice with the hardest available questions. Focus on questions from the harder Module 2 of official practice tests. These questions test concepts in unusual ways, combine multiple topics, and require multi-step reasoning. Exposure to the full range of difficulty is essential for maintaining your score under test conditions.

Perfect your Module 1 execution. At the 700+ level, you should aim for 22/22 or 21/22 on Module 1. This is achievable with careful, deliberate work. The time investment is worthwhile because a strong Module 1 ensures you are routed to the harder Module 2 with maximum scoring potential.

Develop a verification habit for every question. After selecting your answer, take 5 to 10 seconds to verify it makes sense. For algebra questions, substitute your answer back into the original equation. For geometry questions, check that your answer is reasonable given the figure. For word problems, confirm that your answer is in the correct units and within a plausible range.

Work on mental math speed for common calculations. Being able to quickly compute percentages, evaluate simple expressions, and estimate square roots saves cumulative time across the module that can be reallocated to the hardest questions.

The timeline for moving from the 700 range to the 750-800 range can be as short as 2 to 4 weeks of targeted practice, but it requires intense focus on eliminating specific errors rather than broad content review.

## The Complete Preparation Plan: From Diagnostic to Mastery

Regardless of your starting score, the preparation process follows a consistent sequence of phases.

### Phase 1: Diagnostic (Days 1-3)

Take a full-length official practice test under timed, realistic conditions. This is your baseline. Score it, review every question (including the ones you got right), and categorize your errors using the error analysis framework. Identify your starting score, your target score, and the specific topics and error types that need attention.

### Phase 2: Foundation Building (Weeks 1-4)

Focus on your highest-priority content gaps. Study the underlying concepts using instructional resources, then practice with targeted question sets. Start with the topics that appear most frequently (Algebra, then Advanced Math) before moving to less frequent domains. During this phase, do not take full practice tests. Focus on learning and practicing at the topic level.

### Phase 3: Integration and Practice Tests (Weeks 5-8)

Begin taking timed practice tests at regular intervals (one every 1 to 2 weeks). After each test, perform a thorough error analysis and adjust your study focus based on the results. During the days between practice tests, continue targeted practice on your weakest topics. Start incorporating Desmos practice into your routine if you have not already.

### Phase 4: Refinement and Simulation (Weeks 9-12)

At this stage, your content knowledge should be solid and the focus shifts to strategy, pacing, and error elimination. Take practice tests under the most realistic conditions possible: timed, on a laptop, in a quiet room, with no distractions. Analyze your performance with emphasis on the types of errors you are still making and whether those errors are preventable with better habits. Practice your Module 1 strategy of maximum accuracy. Practice your flagging and review strategy.

### Phase 5: Test Week

In the final week before the test, do not take any more full practice tests. Instead, do light review of key formulas, concepts, and strategies. Practice a few questions each day to stay sharp, but keep the volume low. Focus on rest, sleep, nutrition, and mental preparation. Your performance on test day depends more on being well-rested and mentally sharp than on cramming additional content.

### Building a Study Schedule That Works

The most effective SAT Math preparation is consistent rather than intensive. Studying for 45 minutes per day, six days per week, produces better results than cramming for 5 hours on a single weekend day. This is because spaced repetition (reviewing material at intervals) produces stronger long-term retention than massed practice (studying everything at once).

Here is a sample weekly schedule for a student in the active preparation phase:

Monday through Thursday: 45-minute focused study sessions. Each session should begin with 10 minutes of review (revisiting a topic from the previous week using flashcards or a quick problem set), followed by 25 minutes of new topic study (reading concept explanations and working through examples), followed by 10 minutes of timed practice (answering 5 to 7 questions under timed conditions on the topic you just studied).

Friday: 30-minute mixed practice session. Answer 15 to 20 questions drawn from all topics you have studied so far. Time yourself. This simulates the experience of encountering different question types in sequence, as you will on the actual test.

Saturday: Full practice test (on alternating weeks) or 60-minute deep practice session on your weakest topics. When you take a practice test, reserve at least 30 to 45 minutes afterward for error analysis. When you do a deep practice session, focus exclusively on the topics where your error rate is highest.

Sunday: Rest. Take a complete day off from SAT preparation. Mental rest is part of the learning process, and burnout is a real risk during multi-week preparation.

### Tracking Your Progress

Keep a simple progress log that records three things after each study session: what topics you studied, how many questions you attempted, and how many you answered correctly. Over time, this log reveals your trajectory. You should see the accuracy on each topic increasing as you progress through your preparation.

After each practice test, record your total Math score, your accuracy on each content domain, and the number and type of errors you made. Plot your practice test scores over time to visualize your improvement trajectory. This data is motivating when you can see progress and diagnostic when you can identify areas that are not improving.

If your practice test scores plateau for more than two consecutive tests, it usually means one of three things: you are making the same types of errors repeatedly and need to address a specific habit rather than study more content, you have mastered the easy and medium questions and need to deliberately practice harder questions, or you are experiencing test fatigue and need to adjust your pacing strategy. Your error analysis will tell you which of these applies.

**How does the adaptive module system work for Math?**
All students take the same Module 1. Based on your Module 1 performance, you are routed to either a harder or easier Module 2. The harder Module 2 allows access to higher scores (up to 800), while the easier Module 2 caps your score at approximately the mid-600s.

Enter your best guess, flag the question for review, and move on. Return to it during your review pass at the end of the module. Spending too long on a single question is one of the most common pacing mistakes.

**Can I improve my SAT Math score without a tutor or prep course?**
Absolutely. The official College Board resources (Bluebook practice tests and the Question Bank) combined with Khan Academy's free SAT prep provide all the content and practice you need. The key is structured, consistent self-study with error analysis after every practice session.

**What is the most common mistake students make on SAT Math?**
The most common preventable mistake is carelessness on easy questions, often caused by rushing. Students who slow down slightly on the first half of each module and double-check their work on straightforward questions consistently score higher than students who rush through easy questions to save time for hard ones.

**How does SAT Math differ from school math?**
SAT Math tests the same content as school math through Algebra II, but the questions are designed to test understanding and reasoning rather than memorized procedures. The SAT frequently presents familiar concepts in unfamiliar ways, which is why practice with official questions is essential.

**What should I do the night before the SAT?**
Do not study intensively. Review a few key formulas or concepts briefly, then focus on relaxation. Prepare everything you need for the next day (ID, device, calculator, snacks). Go to bed early and aim for 7 to 8 hours of sleep. Your Math performance depends more on being well-rested and focused than on last-minute cramming.
