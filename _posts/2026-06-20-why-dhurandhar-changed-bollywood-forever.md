The most important thing that changed when the Dhurandhar franchise became the commercial and cultural phenomenon it became is not any specific record or any specific critical recognition. It is the vocabulary. The vocabulary that Hindi commercial cinema uses to describe what is possible changed, and changed permanently, because the franchise demonstrated at the commercial blockbuster scale that things previously considered commercially impossible are possible. That demonstration is not reversible. You cannot un-demonstrate something that has been demonstrated. The franchise has given Hindi commercial cinema a new set of reference points against which ambition is measured, and those reference points will shape what is attempted, what is funded, and what is received by audiences for the foreseeable future.

## Bollywood Before Dhurandhar: The Landscape the Franchise Entered

Understanding what the franchise changed requires understanding what it changed from. The Hindi commercial cinema landscape that the franchise entered in 2025 had specific characteristics that shaped the creative and commercial conventions the franchise challenged.

The dominant commercial model in Hindi cinema before the franchise was organized around a set of assumptions that had proved commercially reliable across the previous decade. Major films needed major stars. A rating needed to be avoided in favor of U/A to maximize demographic reach. Runtimes needed to stay under two and a half hours to maximize daily show counts and reduce audience commitment thresholds. The narrative register needed to be emotionally direct enough for audiences across the diversity of the Hindi-speaking market to receive it without inference. And the genre framework needed to be recognizable enough that audiences understood what kind of entertainment to expect before entering the theater.

These assumptions were not arbitrary: they reflected genuine commercial knowledge about what had worked. Uri had worked within them, delivering a nationalist military thriller that honored the runtime, the emotional directness, and the U/A certificate while generating extraordinary commercial performance. Pathaan had worked within them, delivering a YRF spy thriller with star-return narrative that honored the commercial formula while generating extraordinary opening numbers. Jawan had worked within them at enormous scale. The commercial model was functioning, and the conventional wisdom of the industry was that the model would continue to function.

What the conventional wisdom did not anticipate was a film that violated every parameter of the model and generated better commercial performance than any film that honored them. The franchise's A certificate, its nearly four-hour runtime, its emotionally demanding register, its genre that required the audience to understand spy tradecraft rather than simply to enjoy spy action: each of these should have been, by the conventional model's logic, a commercial liability. Together, they should have been commercially disqualifying. Instead, they produced the most commercially successful franchise in Hindi cinema history. The model was wrong, and the franchise proved it was wrong at a scale that could not be dismissed as an anomaly.

## The Commercial Changes: What the Records Established

The commercial changes the franchise produced are the most immediately visible and the most quantifiable, and they are addressed in detail in [the records analysis](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/05/21/dhurandhar-franchise-records-broken/) and through [the complete box office data](https://reportmedic.org/tools/bollywood-box-office-explorer.html). This section identifies the specific commercial changes that have already begun reshaping industry practice.

The A-certificate ceiling has been removed. Before the franchise, the conventional commercial wisdom was that A-rated films could not reach blockbuster commercial territory because the adult-only restriction eliminated the family viewing segment that drives peak-weekend commercial performance. The franchise's commercial performance has permanently disproved this claim. The A certificate is no longer a commercial disqualification for ambitious filmmakers who want to tell stories that adult-only content standards serve: it is a certification choice that the commercial evidence now supports rather than militates against when the content quality justifies the classification.

The runtime convention has been challenged. The franchise's nearly four-hour Part 1 and comparable Part 2 generated better commercial performance than films whose runtimes were calibrated to the conventional under-two-and-a-half-hour standard. This does not mean that long films will always outperform shorter ones: runtime is not itself a commercial variable. What it means is that runtime is not a commercial ceiling: a film whose creative vision genuinely requires extended duration no longer faces the commercial assumption that duration is a liability.

The repeat-viewership model has been validated. The franchise's extraordinary holdover performance demonstrated that an audience exists in Hindi commercial cinema for films that reward repeat engagement: the audience that returned to the franchise in its second, third, and fourth weeks was not returning out of unfulfilled first-viewing curiosity but out of genuine desire to engage more deeply with material that had rewarded the first viewing. This validation of the repeat-viewership model changes the commercial calculations for ambitious filmmakers who want to make films that are richer on second viewing than on first: the commercial evidence now supports such films rather than suggesting that films should be designed to deliver their full value in a single viewing.

## The Creative Changes: What Became Possible

The creative changes the franchise produced are less immediately measurable than the commercial ones but more durable in their effects on what Hindi commercial cinema believes it can attempt.

The psychological spy thriller is now a commercially viable Hindi commercial genre at the blockbuster scale. Before the franchise, Hindi spy cinema operated primarily in the action-entertainment register: the hero is capable, the mission is clear, the action is spectacular, the moral stakes are simple. [The franchise's relationship to the Bollywood spy tradition](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/05/01/dhurandhar-vs-bollywood-spy-thrillers/) examines this at length. The franchise has demonstrated that the psychological register, in which the spy's inner life is the primary dramatic subject rather than the operational effectiveness, is commercially viable at the scale that the action-entertainment register had previously occupied. This is not a small creative opening: it is the demonstration that an entire register of spy storytelling, previously considered commercially impossible in Hindi cinema, is actually commercially possible.

The two-film narrative arc with genuine structural evolution between installments is now a commercially demonstrated possibility. Before the franchise, franchise sequels in Hindi cinema typically delivered more of what the first film had established: more action, more characters, more scale. The franchise demonstrated that a sequel can be a genuinely different kind of film from its predecessor, organized around different genre conventions and a different emotional register, while sustaining and amplifying the commercial performance. This demonstration has expanded the structural options available to ambitious franchise architects who want to build narrative arcs that evolve genuinely rather than simply escalating.

The moral complexity of national security subjects is now available to Hindi commercial cinema without the assumption that complexity requires commercial sacrifice. Before the franchise, the dominant model for Hindi nationalist cinema was the model of moral clarity: India's cause is just, the enemy is clear, the victory is earned and deserved. The franchise has demonstrated that a film can be nationalist in its endorsement of the strategic objective while being honest about the human cost that pursuing that objective requires, and that this honest complexity can generate stronger commercial performance than moral simplicity. [The controversies analysis](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/06/03/dhurandhar-controversies-explained/) examines the contested dimensions of this moral complexity; what matters for the impact analysis is that the complexity was commercially viable, which changes the creative space available to filmmakers who want to engage seriously with national security subjects.

## The Performance Changes: What Actors Now Know

The franchise's impact on acting practice in Hindi commercial cinema is among its most specific and most practically important cultural contributions. [The performance analysis](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/06/13/ranveer-singh-career-best-performance-dhurandhar/) examines what Singh did in the franchise; this article examines what the franchise's performance demonstrated for the broader acting culture.

The franchise has established that the complete suppression of star quality in service of character inhabitation is commercially viable in Hindi commercial cinema at the blockbuster scale. This is the most important single demonstration the franchise makes for the acting culture: that the star power that generates the commercial conditions for ambitious filmmaking can be completely subordinated to the character's requirements in the actual film, and that this subordination is not a commercial sacrifice but a commercial asset. The audience does not attend the franchise to see Ranveer Singh. They attend to see Hamza Ali Mazari, and the fact that Hamza Ali Mazari was created by Ranveer Singh becoming unavailable as Ranveer Singh is the performance's most historically significant contribution to what acting in Hindi commercial cinema can mean.

The dual-performance architecture, in which the actor simultaneously communicates the character and the character performing the cover, has been established as achievable in Hindi commercial cinema at the two-film scale. This architecture has no precise precedent at this scale in Hindi commercial cinema: it requires a level of sustained performance discipline, maintained across more than seven hours of screen time, that the craft had not previously been required to demonstrate in this specific context. Singh's demonstration that it is achievable changes what directors and producers can reasonably ask of major stars in similarly demanding creative contexts.

## The World-Building Changes: What Settings Now Mean

The franchise's contribution to Hindi commercial cinema's world-building practice is the establishment of what sustained world investment looks like and what it produces commercially and artistically. Before the franchise, Hindi commercial films typically used their settings as backdrops: efficiently established locations that provided visual variety without requiring the audience to genuinely inhabit them. [The Karachi world-building analysis](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/05/11/karachi-underworld-lyari-dhurandhar/) examines what the franchise did differently; this article examines what that difference demonstrates for the industry.

The franchise demonstrated that genuine world-building, the construction of a setting as a community rather than as a backdrop, is commercially viable in Hindi commercial cinema. The repeat viewership that drove the franchise's extraordinary holdover performance is substantially driven by the audience's desire to return to the Lyari world rather than simply to see the plot again, and the Lyari world was constructed through the kind of sustained, specific, patient investment in community texture that efficient commercial production typically cannot accommodate. The franchise's willingness to make this investment, and the commercial evidence that the investment paid dividends, changes the production calculus for filmmakers and producers who want to build worlds rather than simply populate settings.

## Where the Changes Will Take Longest

The franchise's impact is not uniform: some changes are already visible in the industry's creative and commercial choices, while others will take longer to manifest fully.

The changes to financial modeling will take longest. The conventional commercial models that Hindi film financiers use to project a film's commercial potential are built on historical data, and historical data is slow to update for genuinely unprecedented performances. The franchise's specific commercial profile, particularly the A-certificate performance and the holdover ratios, is still being integrated into the models that investors and distributors use to evaluate commercial risk. Until the models fully integrate the franchise's performance, filmmakers who propose projects with the franchise's commercial profile will face resistance from financial models that have not yet caught up with what the franchise demonstrated.

The changes to genre convention will take longest in the action dimension. The franchise's action philosophy, in which violence is staged as costly rather than as spectacular, challenges the dominant model of Hindi commercial action cinema in ways that the conventional action film audience will resist. The audience that goes to Pathaan or War for the pleasure of spectacular physical action is not the audience that the franchise was reaching with its uglier and more consequence-oriented action, and the action genre will not shift toward the franchise's model simply because the franchise succeeded commercially: the audience segment that drives action film commercial performance has different preferences from the segment that drove the franchise's commercial performance.

The changes to narrative patience will take longest in the production and development process. The franchise's willingness to spend screen time that narrative efficiency would cut, to build a world rather than a plot, to take as long as the story requires rather than as long as the commercial convention permits, is the kind of creative choice that is most resistant to industry adoption because it runs counter to the production economics that control development decisions. Producers and distributors who fund film development are most sensitive to runtime and pacing because these are the variables most directly connected to the commercial model's efficiency assumptions. Changing these assumptions requires more than commercial proof: it requires a production culture shift in which the value of patience is understood as a creative mechanism rather than as a concession to artistic indulgence.

## The Bigger Argument: What Changed and What It Means

The franchise's most lasting impact on Hindi commercial cinema is not any specific creative or commercial innovation. It is the permanent expansion of what the industry believes is possible, and the contraction of what the industry believes is impossible. Before the franchise, specific things were believed to be commercially impossible in Hindi commercial cinema: A-rated blockbusters, four-hour spy narratives, psychologically complex national security subjects, sustained world-building investment, complete star-quality suppression. Each of these is now possible in a way it was not before, not because the franchise made them possible but because the franchise proved they were possible. The proof is permanent. It lives in the commercial record, in the critical literature, and in the cultural memory of an industry that watched an unprecedented commercial performance and has to account for what produced it.

What the industry does with this accounting is the question that the franchise's impact analysis ultimately cannot answer, because the answer depends on decisions that have not yet been made and on creative projects that have not yet been attempted. The franchise has opened a creative space. Whether that space is occupied by works that honor what opened it, or by works that extract its surface characteristics while abandoning the creative commitment that made those characteristics meaningful, is the question that will determine whether the franchise's impact on Bollywood is lasting or merely temporary.

The analysis that follows from this uncertainty is not that the franchise's impact is contingent: the commercial proof of concept is permanent and the vocabulary change is permanent. What is contingent is the industry's response to that proof, which will be shaped by the creative and commercial choices of specific filmmakers and producers across the next decade of Hindi commercial cinema. The franchise has made the best available case for what serious, patient, ambitious Hindi commercial cinema can achieve. The industry will make the cases for everything else.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Q: What is the single most important change the franchise produced?

The single most important change is the removal of the A-certificate ceiling as a commercial assumption. Every other change the franchise produced is dependent on someone being willing to make a commercially ambitious film that accepts adult-only certification in service of content that requires it. Before the franchise, this willingness was commercially irrational: the A certificate restricted the audience in ways the commercial model predicted would limit commercial performance. After the franchise, the willingness is commercially supported: the commercial evidence demonstrates that an A-rated film of sufficient quality can generate blockbuster performance from the adult demographic alone. This single change is the prerequisite for every other creative change the franchise enables.

### Q: How quickly will the industry internalize the franchise's lessons?

The industry will internalize the franchise's commercial lessons faster than its creative ones. Commercial lessons, specifically the evidence that A-rated content and extended runtimes are commercially viable, will be integrated into development decisions within the next few project cycles: producers and distributors will become willing to greenlight projects with these characteristics because the commercial evidence supports the risk. Creative lessons, specifically the understanding that patient world-building, psychological complexity, and performance restraint are the mechanisms behind the commercial success rather than incidental accompanying features, will take longer: they require the kind of creative conviction that cannot be mandated by commercial success but must be developed by individual filmmakers who understand what the franchise did and why it worked.

### Q: Which Bollywood filmmakers are most likely to benefit from the creative space the franchise opened?

The filmmakers most likely to benefit are the ones who were already developing creative ambitions in the direction the franchise validated: filmmakers who were interested in psychological depth, in world-building, in moral complexity, and in the kind of performance-centered storytelling that the franchise demonstrated was commercially viable. The franchise has not created these filmmakers: they existed before the franchise and their ambitions were constrained by the commercial assumptions the franchise has now challenged. What the franchise has done is remove the commercial argument against funding their projects, which is the most practically important obstacle that ambitious filmmakers in the commercial cinema context face. The specific names are less important than the creative type: any filmmaker whose ambitions were previously considered commercially prohibitive by the conventional model now has a case to make that the conventional model was wrong.

### Q: Will the franchise's impact be visible in the next five years of Hindi commercial cinema?

The franchise's impact will be visible in the next five years in specific and limited ways. A-rated projects with serious ambitions will receive more development consideration than they previously did. Franchise proposals that involve genuine structural evolution between installments will face less resistance. Performance projects that require star-quality suppression will find producers more willing to accept the commercial risk. These are real changes and they are already beginning to manifest in the development decisions that are visible from the outside of the industry. What will not be visible in five years is the full cultural shift that the franchise represents: the genuine internalization of the understanding that quality and commercial scale are compatible rather than in trade-off. That shift takes a generation rather than five years, because it requires the development of a generation of filmmakers, producers, and investors who learned from the franchise what their predecessors had to be shown by it.

### Q: Is the franchise's impact limited to Hindi cinema or does it extend to the pan-Indian film industry?

The franchise's impact extends to the pan-Indian film industry in specific and limited ways. The specific creative demonstration, that Hindi commercial cinema can tell a psychologically complex spy story at the blockbuster scale, is more culturally specific: the spy film tradition in South Indian cinema has its own conventions and its own commercial logic, and the franchise's specific contribution is to the Hindi-language tradition. What travels pan-Indian is the commercial proof of concept. What stays in the Hindi-language tradition is the creative proof of concept.

### Q: What would the franchise need to produce in the next decade to make its impact permanent?

The franchise's commercial and cultural impact is already permanent in the specific sense that the commercial records cannot be unset and the vocabulary change cannot be reversed. What would make the impact most durably felt is a body of subsequent work, by Dhar or by other filmmakers inspired by the franchise, that demonstrates that the franchise's specific qualities are reproducible rather than unique. A single extraordinary work can change the vocabulary of possibility without changing the practice: it takes a body of work to demonstrate that the vocabulary change has been internalized as creative practice. The franchise has opened the space. The work that occupies the space, which is the work that remains to be done, will determine whether the franchise's impact is that of a landmark or a revolution. The comparison to Baahubali's impact on South Indian cinema is the most instructive available international comparison for understanding the scale and nature of the franchise's impact on Bollywood. The franchise demonstrates something comparable but different for Hindi cinema: not that the scale of Hindi commercial cinema can expand, but that the ambition of Hindi commercial cinema can expand without sacrificing commercial scale. ### Q: How did the franchise change the industry's understanding of what repeat viewership means?

Before the franchise, repeat viewership in Hindi commercial cinema was primarily associated with mass entertainers whose most engaged fans returned to experience the euphoric theatrical events the films produced: the action sequence that generates a standing ovation, the item number that demands repeat exposure, the dialogue delivery that produces collective audience response. This kind of repeat viewership is driven by the desire to re-experience a peak moment and is typically concentrated in the film's opening weeks when the theatrical event quality is freshest. The franchise produced a different kind of repeat viewership: audiences returning not to re-experience peak moments but to engage more deeply with material that rewarded sustained attention. The repeat viewer of the franchise is not returning to relive the most exciting sequence but to notice what they missed the first time, to track the performance's micro-level work, to hear the dialogue's second layer of meaning, to see the visual language doing things the first viewing consumed too much attention to register. This kind of repeat viewership is more durable and more commercially valuable per viewer than the event-repeat model, because it sustains commercial performance into the third and fourth weeks rather than exhausting itself in the first. The franchise's holdover ratios are the commercial evidence for this different model of repeat engagement.

### Q: What does the franchise's success suggest about the Indian multiplex audience specifically?

The franchise's success suggests that the multiplex audience is larger, more sophisticated, and more commercially capable of sustaining ambitious cinema than the industry's conventional model assumed. The multiplex audience has been modeled primarily as a premium-format extension of the mass entertainment audience: people who want the same entertainment as the mass market but in a better environment. The franchise's performance suggests that the multiplex audience also contains a substantial segment that wants something the mass entertainment model does not provide: the specific quality of sustained, patient, psychologically demanding cinema that the franchise offers. This segment was always present in the multiplex audience but had not been given a commercial product calibrated to its specific preferences at the franchise scale. The franchise calibrated to that preference and found the segment large enough to sustain blockbuster commercial performance. The discovery of this segment is commercially significant: it means the multiplex market is larger than the conventional model had accounted for, and the uncaptured segment is one that specifically rewards quality and complexity.

The franchise's marketing approach for both films departed from the conventions of Hindi commercial film marketing in ways that reflected and reinforced the franchise's creative philosophy. Conventional Hindi commercial film marketing is designed to communicate the entertainment value proposition clearly and immediately: the trailers show the action sequences, the songs, the star's most charismatic moments, the emotional climaxes. The franchise's marketing communicated something different: the quality and the seriousness of the creative enterprise, the world that the film had built, and the kind of engagement the film was designed to produce. The trailers showed the atmosphere and the psychological texture rather than the peak moments. The promotional interviews focused on the creative process and the specific craft investments rather than on the star's personal life or the film's commercial expectations. The approach signaled to the specific audience the franchise was designed for that this was a different kind of film, and that signal was commercially productive: it converted the multiplex audience segment that wanted exactly what the franchise was offering into advance-booking certainty rather than first-week curiosity.

The franchise has raised the threshold of operational authenticity that a Hindi spy film needs to meet to be received as serious rather than as entertainment with spy aesthetics. This does not mean the conventional spy film is no longer commercially viable: different audiences want different things, and the mass entertainment spy film audience is not the same audience the franchise was reaching. But the segment of the spy film audience that has engaged deeply with the franchise has been permanently upgraded in its expectations for what serious treatment of intelligence work looks like, and future Hindi spy films that want to reach this specific segment will need to engage with those upgraded expectations rather than with the pre-franchise baseline.

### Q: How has the franchise affected how Indian film critics operate?

The franchise's impact on Indian film criticism is among its most practically significant cultural contributions.
