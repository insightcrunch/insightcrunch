Ranveer Singh's public image has been built on maximalism. For more than a decade, the actor has been Bollywood's most visibly energetic presence, with a performance register that prioritises physical presence, vocal amplitude, and emotional largeness. His earlier roles, from the brash romantic lead of Band Baaja Baaraat to the historical spectacle of Bajirao Mastani to the musical intensity of Gully Boy, have shared a common DNA: they ask Singh to fill the frame, to dominate rooms, to deliver emotional cues at the top of his available range. This register has earned him critical and commercial success across a wide tonal spread, but it has also become the trap of expectation. Audiences arrive at a Ranveer Singh release expecting maximalism, and the industry has reinforced the expectation by casting him in roles that reward it.

Dhurandhar breaks the trap. Across two installments totalling roughly six hours of screen time, Ranveer Singh delivers the most disciplined work of his career, in a role that asks him to subtract rather than to add. Hamza Ali Mazari is not Bajirao, not Gully Boy's Murad, not Padmaavat's Alauddin Khilji. The character is a man who must, for a decade of story time, not be noticed. His survival depends on his ability to reduce his presence to the level of the ambient neighbourhood in which his cover embeds him. The role requires an actor capable of performing absence rather than presence, and the achievement of Ranveer Singh's output in Dhurandhar is that he answers this requirement with craft choices that run against every instinct his earlier roles have built.

This article argues that Ranveer Singh's piece in Dhurandhar is his career-best performance, not because it is his loudest or his most technically showy, but because it demonstrates a subtractive discipline that his earlier roles never required him to develop. The analysis traces the career arc that made the Hamza role possible, examines the specific craft choices that make the technique distinctive, compares The lead's choices to the choices his peers have made in comparable roles, and engages honestly with the places where the work's ambition meets its limits. What emerges is a portrait of an actor whose range has been significantly underestimated, and whose willingness to risk everything his earlier career taught audiences to expect is what makes Dhurandhar the pivot point of his labour to date.

The [filmmaking context that made Singh's output possible](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/04/24/aditya-dhar-filmmaking-style-analysis/) is worth acknowledging at the outset. Aditya Dhar's direction, script, and restraint philosophy created the conditions in which a subtractive Ranveer Singh could flourish, and the pairing of player and director is one of the most productive collaborations in recent Hindi commercial cinema. But the direction is not sufficient to produce the effort the finished piece delivers; Singh had to choose the discipline, sustain it throughout an extended production, and trust that the subtractive approach would read on screen despite its departure from every audience expectation his earlier career had established. He did, and it does.

## The Career Arc: What Each Role Built

Understanding why Ranveer Singh's work as The protagonist is a departure requires understanding what his prior roles had built. His career divides roughly into three phases, each of which developed craft capacities that the Dhurandhar role eventually drew on, even as it required the actor to set aside the maximalist register that those earlier phases trained him to deploy.

The first phase, from Band Baaja Baaraat through Ram-Leela, established his romantic lead credentials. These parts asked him to be charming, present, and physically engaged with his scene partners, and Singh developed a physical register that reads as ease in the frame. He learned how to hold rooms without dominating them, how to negotiate romantic chemistry with a range of female leads, how to deploy a smile as dramatic punctuation rather than as ambient pleasantry. The first-phase roles established him as a reliable commercial lead, and they did so without requiring the deeper craft disciplines that his later roles would demand.

The second phase, from Bajirao Mastani through Padmaavat, expanded his range toward historical and spectacular material. These roles asked him to inhabit figures whose size exceeded the contemporary frame, to deliver long speeches with period-register rhetorical flourish, and to carry ensemble films in which his character was the visual and emotional centre. The lead developed a rhetorical register in these roles, a capacity for elevated speech, a physical commitment to armed combat and dance choreography, and the vocal discipline that period material requires. The second-phase roles established him as a serious actor whose range included theatrical and historical material that commercial Bollywood has often delegated to supporting rather than protagonist performers.

The third phase, from Gully Boy through Jayeshbhai Jordaar, explored the contemporary socially engaged register. These roles asked him to play figures whose struggles were rooted in specific urban Indian realities, and they required him to develop a mode of ordinariness that his earlier roles had not demanded. The Gully Boy performance in particular was significant for demonstrating that he could strip his maximalist instincts back far enough to play a character whose power came from accumulation rather than spectacle. What the third phase established was that his range extended below his default mode, into a territory where less is more, and the finding was the craft foundation that the Hamza role eventually tested at scale.

What the career arc reveals is that Singh had, by the time Dhurandhar began production, developed the full range of technique capacities that the Hamza role would require: the ease in the frame from phase one, the vocal and physical discipline from phase two, and the subtractive instinct from phase three. What was uncertain was whether he could sustain the subtractive register throughout a production of Dhurandhar's scale. His previous subtractive delivery had been contained within single films of conventional length; the role would require him to maintain the register across approximately six hours of screen time, over dozens of ensemble scenes, and against the gravitational pull of his own earlier maximalism. The sustained execution is what makes the Dhurandhar the craft choice career-defining.

The actor's earlier roles also established patterns that Hamza requires him to unlearn. The maximalist star learns to command attention as the baseline mode of occupying scenes; the undercover operative must learn to diffuse attention as the baseline mode. The spectacular historical figure learns to speak at volume as the default mode; the long-cover agent must speak at the ambient volume of the neighbourhood. The socially engaged contemporary lead learns to inhabit one psychological space with full presence; the undercover agent must inhabit two psychological spaces simultaneously, with the cover identity dominating the surface while the real identity persists underneath. Each of these adjustments is technically demanding, and the accumulation of adjustments across the role is what separates it from Singh's earlier artistry.

What the Dhurandhar role provides is critical value instead of commercial value, and the distinction matters for assessing what the role means in the context of his career overall.

## What Singh Strips Away

The most useful way to describe what The actor does in Dhurandhar is to enumerate what he removes from his default range. Each removal is a deliberate craft choice, and the accumulation of removals is the mechanism through which the subtractive discipline operates.

He removes vocal amplitude. Hamza speaks quietly, often just above the ambient volume of the room he occupies, and his voice rarely rises above the register of ordinary conversation even in dramatic moments that a conventional lead role would deliver at elevated volume. The vocal work is one of the most immediately audible departures from The lead's previous roles, and attentive viewers notice it in the opening sequences of the first installment without being able to name what has changed. What has changed is that the actor has deliberately moved his voice down, and the downward shift is sustained across the entire paired production.

He removes physical expansiveness. Hamza occupies the frame with minimum physical presence: his gestures are small, his posture is neutral, his movement through rooms is unobtrusive. This is the opposite of the large gestural mode that Singh's earlier roles established, and the physical discipline is one of the most difficult sustained choices an actor can make. Muscle memory, rehearsal habits, and unconscious instincts all pull toward larger physical registers, and maintaining the smaller register requires continuous conscious attention. The lead sustains it across approximately six hours of screen time, which is a technical achievement that most actors at his career stage would not attempt.

He removes smile as default punctuation. His earlier roles often relied on his smile as a dramatic tool, a way of signalling warmth, relaxation, or charm. Hamza smiles only when his cover specifically requires it, and the absence of the default smile across most of the runtime is one of the quietest but most significant craft choices in the performance. Attentive viewers notice that Hamza's face rarely carries the warmth that Ranveer's earlier characters' faces routinely carried, and the absence communicates the cover's psychological toll without requiring any line of dialogue to announce it.

He removes rhetorical flourish. His earlier historical roles deployed elevated speech patterns, rhetorical structures, and theatrical delivery rhythms. Hamza speaks in the ordinary register of the neighbourhood his cover inhabits, with short sentences, colloquial vocabulary, and conversational rhythms that refuse any theatrical elevation. The rhetorical restraint is consistent with Dhar's writing, which gives Hamza few long monologues, but Ranveer takes the restraint further than the writing strictly requires: even where his lines could be delivered with rhetorical weight, he delivers them flat, and the flatness is where the character lives.

He removes eye performance. The actor's earlier roles often relied on his eyes as a dramatic tool, with significant work done by sustained direct gaze, expressive blinks, and emotional cues delivered through the face. Hamza's eyes are more guarded, more frequently deflected, less willing to carry the emotional role that the earlier roles routinely assigned to them. The guarded eye mode is one of the most psychologically accurate choices in the turn: a man whose survival depends on not revealing himself would naturally develop a guarded eye, and the guard is maintained across hundreds of scenes with a consistency that surprises attentive viewers.

He removes tempo variation. His earlier roles moved through different emotional tempos across a film's runtime, with high-energy sequences alternating against slower dramatic passages. Hamza operates at a narrower tempo range: his baseline is slower than a conventional star's baseline, and his peaks are lower than a conventional principal's peaks. The compressed tempo range is an unusual choice for a commercial Bollywood lead, and it is one of the reasons the rendering reads as distinctive within the genre it occupies. The compression communicates the control Hamza must exercise to maintain his disguise, and the control is the dramatic work the performance is doing.

What remains after these removals is a character built on minor gestures, small vocal adjustments, and accumulated textural detail rather than on broad performance strokes. The character Singh constructs lives in the granular level of the craft, and the granular accumulation is what produces the sustained dramatic effect the portrayal achieves. This is a craft approach that requires the actor to trust that the viewer will register what has been removed, and the trust is vindicated by the audience response the acting has generated.

## The Dual Work: A Man Playing a Character

The deeper craft demand of the Hamza role is that The lead is not playing one figure; he is playing a man playing a character. Jaskirat Singh Rangi is performing Hamza Ali Mazari across the entirety of both installments, and the performance must communicate both the identity being performed and the person performing it, simultaneously, for hours of screen time.

This is a distinct craft challenge from the challenge of playing a character straightforwardly. A straight character performance asks the actor to inhabit a single psychological space and operate within it. A dual work asks the actor to inhabit two psychological spaces simultaneously, with the outer space visible to other characters in the scene and the inner space visible only to the viewer. The viewer must see Hamza as the Lyari community sees him, fluently embedded and unremarkable, and must simultaneously see Jaskirat performing Hamza, with the portrayal legible through small cracks in the surface.

The actor accomplishes the dual performance through calibration rather than through overt signalling. He does not use winks at the audience, voice-over asides, or visible breaking of the cover identity to remind the viewer that Jaskirat is underneath. Instead, he calibrates Hamza's surface so that the surface is slightly effortful in a way a fluent native would not be, and the slight effortfulness is the signature of the performance underneath. A genuine Lyari native speaking Urdu would speak with a certain unreflective ease; Hamza speaks with a near-ease that carries the micro-signature of conscious construction, and the micro-signature is visible to the viewer even as it remains invisible to the Lyari characters within the scene.

The calibration is most visible in the scenes where Hamza is least stressed. In high-pressure moments, any actor could credibly perform the effortfulness of a cover under strain; the dual role is only impressive when it persists in ordinary domestic moments where no external pressure should be producing any visible strain. Singh's Hamza carries the micro-signature of effort even when drinking tea with his adopted Lyari family, even when joking with neighbours in an ordinary street encounter, even when performing the rituals of a community celebration. The signature never rests, because the performance never rests, because the cover never rests. This is the craft foundation of the dual performance, and it is maintained throughout hundreds of scenes with a discipline that is almost unprecedented in Hindi commercial cinema.

A second aspect of the dual acting involves the slippages. Ranveer's work contains a small number of moments where Jaskirat surfaces through The protagonist, and these slippages are the performance's richest craft payoffs. In a moment of grief, Hamza's Urdu carries a Punjabi rhythm that he does not usually let through. In a moment of strategic calculation, his posture tightens in a way that the ambient Hamza register would not show. In a moment of unexpected recognition, his eyes meet someone's eyes with a directness that the guarded Hamza register would normally avoid. These slippages are small, brief, and never commented on within the scene. They are the performance's signatures to the viewer that Jaskirat is still underneath, and that the cover's psychological cost is continuous and accumulating.

The dual work is sustained throughout both installments with a consistency that suggests Singh and Dhar planned it from the script stage rather than discovering it in the performance. The micro-signatures are too precisely placed to be improvisational, and the slippages are too well-timed to the dramatic structure to be accidental. What the finished piece delivers is a portrayal designed in writing, calibrated in rehearsal, and executed over the span of a production schedule that allowed both actor and director to verify that the dual work was registering as intended. The planning is as much a craft achievement as the execution, and both deserve recognition in any serious evaluation of the rendering.

A third aspect of the dual performance involves the interaction with the audience's knowledge. First-time viewers of the opening installment do not know Hamza is actually Jaskirat until the film reveals the cover structure, and the revelation reconfigures everything the viewer has seen up to that point. Returning viewers watch the opening scenes with full knowledge of the alias, and the slightly effortful The role register now reads as visible construction rather than as ambient characterisation. His role is calibrated to reward both modes of viewing: the first-time viewer experiences Hamza as Hamza, the returning viewer experiences This figure as Jaskirat performing Hamza, and the performance supports both readings without collapsing into either. This is durability of the sort that cinematic performances rarely achieve, and it is one of the reasons the Dhurandhar role will likely be studied for years, and one of the reasons its craft lessons will carry forward into future commercial productions.

## The Body That Tells Two Stories

The physical artistry of Singh's craft deserves its own analysis because it operates at a level of granularity that most commercial Bollywood performances do not attempt. The protagonist's body tells two stories simultaneously: the story of the Lyari man he appears to be, and the story of the undercover operative maintaining the appearance. Both stories are visible to attentive viewers, and the physical technique that makes the dual visibility possible is distinctive in the Hindi film tradition.

Consider the walk. Singh's Hamza moves through Lyari with the slightly measured gait of a man who has rehearsed the neighbourhood long enough to know its rhythms but who retains a residual caution that a true local would not. The gait is subtly different from Singh's walks in his earlier roles: the earlier walks were confident, frame-filling, attention-gathering; the Hamza walk is low-profile, edge-of-frame, attention-deflecting. The Lyari community within the scene reads this as ordinary neighbourhood walking; the viewer reads it as conscious composition. The walk is doing dual narrative effort, and it is doing the output continuously across hundreds of scenes.

Consider the hands. Singh's earlier roles used his hands as dramatic tools: gestures that punctuated speech, touches that communicated intimacy, expansive physical articulation that filled the frame. The protagonist's hands are quieter. They rest on his lap when he sits, they move minimally when he speaks, they deliver touches only when the cover's social register requires them. The hand discipline is one of the most visibly sustained craft choices in the interpretation, and it communicates the character's internal control without requiring any line of dialogue to announce it. An actor less disciplined than Singh has become across this role would let the hands drift back toward the earlier register; Ranveer does not, and the containment is the physical signature of the performance.

Consider the posture. Singh's past roles often featured upright, chest-forward posture that commanded rooms. Hamza's posture is softer: a slight lean, shoulders neither forward nor back, a body configuration that does not announce itself. The posture is consistent with the undercover logic of the lead role: a body that announces itself is a body that will be noticed, and Hamza cannot afford to be noticed. The lead maintains the soft posture throughout scenes involving formal institutional encounters, intimate family moments, tense operational exchanges, and ordinary street interactions. The consistency is a testament to Singh's sustained physical awareness, and it is one of the elements of the acting that takes repeat viewing to fully appreciate.

Consider the facial micro-adjustments. Hamza's face is not expressionless, but the expressions it carries are narrower in range than the expressions Singh's earlier characters routinely carried. Small adjustments, a slight tightening around the eyes, a minor downturn at the mouth, a brief widening of the gaze, do the work that larger facial expressions would do in a conventional acting. The reduction in facial range is coupled with an increase in facial precision: the small adjustments land exactly where they need to land, with a timing that rewards close viewing. This is the facial equivalent of the vocal minimum The actor maintains throughout the interpretation, and the two registers reinforce each other to produce the overall impression of a man whose body is carrying a constant interior load while presenting a calm exterior.

The physical work across the two installments also registers the passage of time. Hamza in the opening chapter is a man who has been under cover for over a decade; by the sequel, the accumulated weight of that decade is visible in small physical signs, a slightly more tired carriage, a few more deliberate pauses before moving, a minor adjustment in the way he enters and exits rooms. Ranveer calibrates these signs so they are visible to attentive viewers without being foregrounded as explicit character choices, and the calibration is another element of the performance's durability. A viewer watching both installments in sequence registers the physical aging even without conscious attention to it, and the registration is what makes the role's accumulated psychological cost feel earned in place of asserted.

Comparing Singh's physical approach here to the physical approach in his earlier roles reveals the scope of the art advance. Bajirao and Alauddin were bodies that filled space; Hamza is a body that minimises its space footprint. Murad in Gully Boy was a body that expanded across the film's runtime, as the protagonist's confidence grew; Hamza is a body that maintains constant discipline across the runtime, because the cover cannot afford growth. The contrast is not between better and worse physical work but between different physical modes, and Singh's range has turned out to be wider than the earlier roles implied.

## The Emotional Climaxes

A performance built on subtraction faces a particular challenge at the moments when the dramatic logic calls for emotional release. The viewer has been trained over hours of runtime to expect Hamza to maintain his minimum register, and any explicit emotional expression risks feeling either underpowered, because it remains within the minimum register, or overpowered, because it breaks the range and feels tonally disconnected from everything that preceded it. The Dhurandhar work contains several emotional climaxes, and Singh's handling of them is one of the performance's most technically impressive achievements.

The mehendi sequence in the opening installment is the earliest and most tightly constructed of these climaxes. The scene gives Hamza essentially no dialogue that could carry emotional weight; the weight must come from the face and body. Singh's choice is to play the scene at Hamza's full minimum tone, with no explicit grief expression, and to let the accumulated weight of the previous hours of runtime do the emotional work. The viewer knows what Hamza is feeling because the viewer has watched him build the relationship with the deceased across the runtime; Hamza does not need to perform the grief, because the grief is already present in the viewer's understanding of Hamza. This is emotional work done through negative space, and it is one of the most distinctive technique choices in recent Hindi commercial performance.

The revelation scene in the second installment is a different kind of climax. Hamza's cover begins to fracture, and Singh faces the challenge of showing the fracture without collapsing the performance into conventional emotional display. His choice is to let small physical cracks register, a breath held a beat too long, a hand gesture that tightens before it completes, an eye contact that lingers a moment beyond what the cover would normally permit, while maintaining the baseline minimum register for the majority of the scene. The cracks are the emotional release, but they are delivered at a scale that matches the role's overall logic rather than breaking it. Other actors might have played the revelation at full emotional volume; Singh plays it at micro-volume, and the micro-volume is more devastating than full volume would have been.

The Rehman death sequence in the opening installment is another climax worth examining. Rehman has been one of the relationships that The protagonist has built most carefully spanning the runtime, and his death is partly the result of the operation Hamza has been conducting. Ranveer faces the challenge of showing Hamza's complex response, relief at operational success, grief at a relationship's end, guilt at his own role in the death, without letting any single emotion dominate the scene. His choice is to distribute the emotional load within small gestures and sustained facial adjustments, refusing the conventional lead-character grief close-up that the genre would normally deliver. The distribution makes the scene work as emotional climax while preserving the performance's subtractive logic, and the combination is what makes it possible for the performance to honour both the dramatic demand and the craft discipline.

The Major Iqbal confrontation in the second installment is the climactic scene of the entire paired project, and The actor's handling of it is one of the interpretation's most-debated craft decisions. The scene requires Hamza to confront the institutional antagonist directly, which is a register the subtractive Hamza acting has otherwise avoided. Singh's choice is to allow Hamza to expand slightly in this scene, speaking a touch more directly, occupying a bit more physical space in the frame, letting emotional cues land more visibly than they have elsewhere. Some reviewers have argued that this expansion compromises the subtractive discipline of the earlier runtime; others have argued that the expansion is a considered authorial choice that lets the climax deliver the conventional dramatic payoff the genre requires. The debate is productive, and different viewers will resolve it differently, but the expansion is small enough and well-timed enough that it does not break the performance's overall logic.

The final family reconciliation sequence in the second installment is perhaps the most emotionally demanding moment in the paired production, and it showcases the performance's full range. Hamza is reunited with members of his original family, the family that has not seen Jaskirat for over a decade, and the scene asks Singh to carry both identities simultaneously with full emotional presence. The scene contains longer held close-ups than most of the runtime permits, and Singh fills the close-ups with a stillness that reads as overflow rather than as vacancy. The stillness is the emotional climax the restrained performance has been building toward, and it lands because the hours of discipline that preceded it have earned the viewer's full emotional investment. A work that had been building toward conventional emotional display would have released the investment earlier and less powerfully; the subtractive performance saves everything for this moment, and the moment delivers.

## The Bigger Argument

The actor's output in Dhurandhar is not just a good performance by a good actor. It is a redefinition of what audiences and critics can expect from the Hindi commercial lead role. The expectation that has dominated the industry's star system is that the commercial protagonist is a presence that fills rooms, a voice that dominates scenes, a body that carries spectacle. He has demonstrated that the commercial lead can also be a presence that minimises itself, a voice that speaks at ambient volume, a body that carries subtextual rather than spectacular weight. This demonstration changes what the next generation of commercial Hindi films can aspire to, and the change is one of the more significant industry impacts of the Dhurandhar project.

The argument extends to development practices. If Ranveer can sustain a subtractive performance throughout a six-hour paired production, then other market-level leads can be asked to sustain comparable performances in projects at smaller scales. The industry's default assumption that market leads will anchor films through maximalist presence may be questioned by the next generation of producers and directors who have seen what Singh has accomplished. The questioning will not happen universally, and many mainstream projects will continue to require maximalist presence from their leads, because many mainstream scripts will continue to be written for maximalist leads. But the window for alternatives has widened, and filmmakers who want to technique within the subtractive mode now have industry evidence that audiences will follow a subtractive lead across extended runtimes.

The argument also extends to actor preparation. Singh's capacity to sustain the cover key over hundreds of scenes is not accidental; it is the result of preparation disciplines that most mainstream leads have not traditionally invested in at this scale. Linguistic work, cultural immersion, physical calibration, and sustained attention to micro-level the art decisions are all visible in the finished piece. If his peers recognise the preparation as the mechanism through which the rendering succeeds, the industry's preparation norms may raise over the span of the next several years. Some of The lead's peers will match the investment; others will decline to match it; the industry's casting practices will adjust accordingly, with the most demanding roles going to the performers willing to make the preparation commitment.

The argument also has implications for the relationship between lead and director. Dhar's direction created the conditions in which Singh's subtractive labour was possible; without the writing's restraint philosophy, the direction's patience, and the production's willingness to allow rehearsal time for calibration, the delivery could not have happened at the level it achieved. The director-actor collaboration is one of the more productive such pairings in recent Hindi cinema, and the portrayal's success is partly a function of the collaboration. Future commercial Hindi projects that attempt comparable portrayal registers will need to replicate the collaborative conditions, and the replication is not trivial: it requires producers willing to invest in development time, script writers willing to resist announcement conventions, and technical teams willing to support the subtle craft choices the actor is making.

The turn's career implications for The player are also worth addressing. He has demonstrated that his range extends significantly below his maximalist register, which expands the kinds of roles that producers and directors will consider offering him. His future career now contains two possible trajectories: continued maximalist effort in commercial action and historical material, which his established commercial relationships will continue to support, and expanded minimalist effort in serious dramatic and literary material, which the Dhurandhar acting has now qualified him for. He will likely operate in both registers through the next decade, and the two-tone career is one of the more promising possibilities that the Dhurandhar work has created for him.
