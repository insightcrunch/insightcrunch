## Understanding Unreliable Narration as Literary Technique

Unreliable narration operates through systematic gap between what narrators say and what careful readers understand about characters, events, and meanings, creating interpretive complexity that engages readers as active participants in constructing understanding from partial and potentially distorted information.

The technique requires readers to evaluate narrator credibility through analysis of internal contradictions, selective attention, emotional bias, and cultural assumptions that shape their perspectives while maintaining enough sympathy to understand psychological and social forces affecting their viewpoints.

Effective unreliable narration provides sufficient information for readers to recognize narrator limitations while avoiding simple dismissal of their perspectives, creating interpretive tension that illuminates both individual psychology and broader social conditions that influence perception and judgment.

The literary value of unreliable narration lies in its ability to explore complex moral and psychological questions through multiple layers of meaning while demonstrating how individual perspective affects understanding of truth, relationships, and social conditions.

## Nick Carraway: Social Aspiration as Distorting Lens

Nick Carraway's unreliability in The Great Gatsby stems from social aspiration that creates romantic admiration for wealth and status while preventing accurate assessment of moral character and social relationships that expose the corruption underlying American Dream mythology.

His famous assertion that he is "one of the few honest people that I have ever known" reveals fundamental self-deception about his own moral character while establishing unreliability through obvious contradiction between claimed honesty and demonstrated behavior throughout novel.

The gap between Nick's self-presentation and actual behavior creates ironic distance that enables Fitzgerald to critique both narrator and society through systematic exposure of moral compromises that Nick cannot acknowledge about himself.
