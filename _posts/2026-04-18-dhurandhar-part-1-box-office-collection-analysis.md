When Dhurandhar Part 1 opened in theaters on a Friday in early winter, the Indian film industry was watching with a particular kind of attention that combines anticipation, anxiety, and the awareness that the outcome would recalibrate the commercial possibilities of Hindi cinema for the foreseeable future. The film carried the weight of expectations that would have crushed most productions: Aditya Dhar returning to the espionage genre that produced his breakout hit Uri: The Surgical Strike, Ranveer Singh anchoring the most physically and emotionally demanding role of his career, an ensemble cast headlined by Akshaye Khanna, Sanjay Dutt, Arjun Rampal, and R. Madhavan, and a production scale that dwarfed anything Jio Studios and B62 Studios had previously attempted. The question was not whether the film would be successful but whether its success would be proportional to its ambition, whether a 214-minute, A-certified spy thriller with a non-linear narrative structure and morally complex characters could connect with the mass audience that its commercial viability required. The answer, delivered over the weeks that followed the opening, exceeded even the most optimistic projections, transforming Dhurandhar Part 1 into an all-time blockbuster that rewrote the record books and demonstrated that Indian audiences were not merely willing but eager to engage with commercial cinema that trusted their intelligence. For a complete analysis of the film's narrative and thematic achievements, see our [comprehensive Dhurandhar Part 1 analysis](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/03/19/complete-analysis-dhurandhar-part-1/).

The pre-release landscape in which Dhurandhar Part 1 emerged was itself significant for understanding the film's commercial context. The Hindi film industry had experienced a turbulent period in the years preceding the release, with several high-profile productions underperforming at the box office and a growing narrative, fueled by both industry commentary and social media discourse, that the theatrical model for Hindi cinema was under threat from streaming platforms and changing audience habits. The concern was that audiences had become more selective, less willing to attend theaters for content they could wait to consume on streaming platforms, and that only the most exceptional releases could generate the kind of theatrical demand that the industry's economic model required. This anxiety created a specific kind of pressure around Dhurandhar Part 1, because the film was positioned not merely as a commercial product but as a test case for the proposition that Hindi cinema could still produce theatrical events of sufficient magnitude to sustain the exhibition infrastructure.

The industry's anxiety was compounded by specific concerns about the film's commercial profile. The A certificate was the most frequently cited concern, with analysts pointing to the historical underperformance of A-certified Hindi films and arguing that the certification would restrict the family audience that drives opening-day numbers. The runtime was the second concern, with some commentators questioning whether contemporary audiences would commit to a three-and-a-half-hour theatrical experience when shorter entertainment options were available on their smartphones. And the narrative complexity of the film, which the trailer and marketing materials communicated clearly, raised the question of whether a mass audience would engage with a morally ambiguous spy thriller that required active intellectual participation rather than passive entertainment consumption. Each of these concerns had historical precedent supporting it, and the cumulative effect was to create a pre-release environment in which cautious optimism coexisted with genuine uncertainty about the film's commercial prospects.

The resolution of this uncertainty, delivered over the seven weeks of the film's theatrical run, was so comprehensive as to render the pre-release concerns not merely unfounded but demonstrably wrong. The A certificate did not prevent blockbuster-level attendance. The extended runtime did not deter audience commitment. The narrative complexity did not alienate the mass audience. And the theatrical model proved more than capable of generating the kind of demand that the industry's economic sustainability requires. Each of these outcomes carries implications that extend beyond the specific film to the broader landscape of Hindi cinema, and the analysis that follows traces the specific commercial trajectory through which these outcomes were achieved.

The final worldwide gross of approximately 1,350 crore represents not merely a commercial triumph for the specific film and its stakeholders but a paradigm shift in the economics of Hindi cinema. A film with an A certificate, a runtime exceeding three and a half hours, a protagonist who spends the majority of the narrative speaking Urdu with a Karachi accent, and a moral framework that refuses to provide the uncomplicated patriotic catharsis that the spy genre typically delivers should not, by the conventional wisdom of Bollywood box office analysis, have achieved numbers of this magnitude. That it did so, and did so with a consistency and durability that suggested genuine audience enthusiasm rather than mere opening-weekend curiosity, is an achievement whose implications extend far beyond the balance sheet of its producers. It is a demonstration that the Indian audience, when given material that respects their capacity for engagement, will reward that respect with the most meaningful currency available: sustained, enthusiastic, repeat attendance.

The paradigm shift that these numbers represent is best understood in the context of the commercial assumptions that preceded the film's release. The Hindi film industry had developed, through decades of commercial experience, a set of operating assumptions about what audiences wanted: songs, dances, uncomplicated heroism, romantic subplots that provided emotional relief from narrative tension, and runtimes that accommodated the attention spans of multiplex audiences accustomed to shorter entertainment formats. Dhurandhar Part 1 violated every one of these assumptions: it had no song-and-dance sequences, its heroism was deeply complicated, its romantic subplot was a source of narrative tension rather than relief from it, and its runtime challenged the endurance of even the most committed viewers. The fact that the film succeeded despite violating every conventional assumption suggests that the assumptions themselves were wrong, that they represented the industry's understanding of the audience rather than the audience's actual preferences, and that the gap between what the industry thought the audience wanted and what the audience actually wanted was wider than anyone had realized. The commercial significance of Dhurandhar Part 1 lies not merely in the numbers it generated but in the assumptions it disproved, because the disproval of a commercial assumption is worth more than any single film's collection, as it changes the calculations that every subsequent production will make.

The film's commercial trajectory also demonstrated a specific pattern of audience engagement that carries implications for how the industry plans, produces, and markets Hindi films going forward. The pattern, characterized by a moderate opening followed by extraordinary sustained performance driven by word-of-mouth, inverts the traditional Hindi blockbuster model, which prioritizes massive openings driven by star power and marketing expenditure. The Dhurandhar Part 1 model suggests an alternative path to blockbuster-level revenue: invest in content quality, trust the audience to recognize and reward that quality, and allow the commercial performance to build organically through the most powerful and most cost-effective marketing mechanism available, the genuine enthusiasm of satisfied viewers. This alternative model is not merely theoretically attractive; it has been empirically validated by the specific numbers that this analysis examines, and its validation may prove to be the most consequential commercial outcome of the entire Dhurandhar franchise.

## Day-Wise and Week-Wise Breakdown

The opening day collection of Dhurandhar Part 1 established the film's commercial trajectory with a number that was impressive without being record-shattering, a pattern that would characterize the first installment's box office performance: consistent strength rather than single-day dominance. The film collected approximately 42 crore nett on its opening Friday from the domestic market, a number that reflected both the considerable strength of the pre-release buzz and the structural limitations imposed by the A certificate, which restricted the family audience that typically drives opening-day numbers for major commercial releases in the Hindi market. The opening day was the highest for a non-holiday Friday release of the calendar period, but it fell slightly short of the all-time opening day records that some overly optimistic analysts had projected, leading to premature and ultimately thoroughly incorrect assessments in certain quarters that the film had underperformed relative to its potential.

The opening day's composition revealed important information about the film's audience profile that would prove relevant throughout its run. The morning shows, which typically attract the most dedicated fans and the most anticipation-driven attendance, showed strong but not exceptional occupancy, suggesting that the film's audience was not dominated by the hardcore fan contingent that drives the most explosive openings. The afternoon and evening shows, however, showed progressively higher occupancy, indicating that the film was attracting a broader audience throughout the day rather than frontloading its attendance in the earliest available shows. This intra-day trajectory, with later shows outperforming earlier ones, is characteristic of films whose audience includes a significant proportion of working professionals who attend evening shows rather than morning ones, and it foreshadowed the strong weekday performance that would follow.

The geographic distribution of the opening-day collection also provided early indicators of the audience profile that would define the film's run. Metropolitan multiplexes in Mumbai, Delhi, Bengaluru, Hyderabad, and Pune generated per-screen averages that were among the highest recorded for any Hindi release, while single-screen theaters in smaller markets showed good but less spectacular numbers. This multiplex-heavy opening-day profile indicated that the film's initial audience was concentrated in the urban, educated demographic that frequents premium multiplexes, a base that would prove durable and commercially valuable throughout the theatrical run but that represented only a portion of the total addressable market that subsequent weeks would reach.

These premature assessments were comprehensively refuted by the weekend numbers. Saturday saw a significant jump to approximately 55 crore nett, a growth rate of over thirty percent that indicated extraordinary word-of-mouth. The Saturday growth was driven by two complementary dynamics: the recruitment of new audiences who had heard positive reports from Friday attendees, and the expansion of the film's geographic reach into markets where Friday attendance had been more modest. The multiplex circuit continued to perform at extraordinary levels, with many screens reporting sold-out shows throughout the day, while the single-screen circuit showed a proportionally larger Saturday-to-Friday growth rate, indicating that word-of-mouth was reaching the broader audience that the A certificate and the film's premium positioning had partially excluded from the opening-day attendance.

Sunday pushed further to approximately 62 crore nett, bringing the opening weekend total to roughly 159 crore nett from the domestic market alone. The Sunday performance was notable not merely for its absolute number but for the continued growth from Saturday, which is unusual for Hindi releases that typically show a small decline from Saturday to Sunday. The sustained growth through Sunday indicated that the word-of-mouth was not merely strong but accelerating, with each day's audience generating recommendations that produced larger attendance the following day. This accelerating word-of-mouth dynamic is the rarest and most commercially valuable pattern in Hindi box office performance, and its presence in Dhurandhar Part 1's opening weekend was the earliest and most reliable indicator that the film would achieve blockbuster-level totals.

The weekend trajectory, with each day significantly higher than the previous one, is the pattern that box office analysts associate with genuinely audience-driven hits, films whose commercial performance is powered not by hype or marketing but by the recommendations of viewers who have seen the film and felt compelled to recommend it to others. The growth from Friday to Sunday indicated that Dhurandhar Part 1 was precisely this kind of film, one whose commercial performance would be determined by audience response rather than opening-day curiosity.

The first Monday, traditionally the day that separates genuine blockbusters from overhyped disappointments, confirmed the film's extraordinary holding power. The weekday drop from Sunday was approximately forty-five percent, bringing Monday to approximately 34 crore nett, a number that would have been a respectable opening day for most Hindi films. The Monday hold indicated that the film was not merely attracting weekend audiences but sustaining attendance through the working week, a pattern that requires strong word-of-mouth, high audience satisfaction, and the willingness of viewers who have already seen the film to recommend it to colleagues and friends who watch films on weekdays.

The subsequent weekday performance reinforced the Monday's signal with remarkable consistency. Tuesday collected approximately 28 crore nett, Wednesday approximately 24 crore nett, and Thursday approximately 22 crore nett, creating a weekday trajectory that declined gradually rather than steeply. The weekday holds, measured as a percentage of Monday, were among the strongest for any Hindi release, indicating that the working-week audience was being continuously replenished by word-of-mouth recruitment rather than depleting from a fixed pool of interested viewers. The weekday consistency also reflected the demographic composition of the audience: working professionals who chose to attend the film during the week, often at premium evening shows that commanded higher ticket prices, contributing per-ticket revenue that exceeded the per-ticket revenue of the more price-sensitive weekend audience.

The first week closed at approximately 275 crore nett from the domestic market, a number that placed Dhurandhar Part 1 among the highest first-week grossers in Hindi cinema history. The week-one total was particularly impressive given the A certificate's impact on the family segment and the film's extended runtime, which limited the number of daily shows that exhibitors could schedule. A 214-minute film with mandatory intermission requires a time slot of approximately four hours including interval and credits, meaning that most screens could accommodate only three or four shows per day rather than the five or six that a shorter film would allow. The fact that Dhurandhar Part 1 achieved its first-week numbers with fewer daily shows than its competition made the achievement all the more remarkable, suggesting per-show averages that were substantially higher than the per-show averages of films with higher screen counts but shorter runtimes.

The second week demonstrated the sustained holding power that transforms a hit into a blockbuster. The second Friday opened at approximately 18 crore nett, representing a second-week drop of approximately fifty-seven percent from the first Friday, a hold that was considerably stronger than the industry average for films in this commercial tier. The second weekend climbed to approximately 22 crore nett on Saturday and 26 crore nett on Sunday, indicating that the film was continuing to attract new audiences even as repeat viewings from the first-week audience added to the totals. The second week closed at approximately 125 crore nett, bringing the domestic total to approximately 400 crore nett after fourteen days.

The second-week performance deserves closer analysis because it is the period that most reliably distinguishes genuine blockbusters from films whose opening-week numbers reflect pre-release hype rather than audience satisfaction. A film driven primarily by hype shows a steep second-week decline, typically seventy percent or more from the first week, as the audience that was drawn by marketing discovers that the product does not match the promise. A film driven by genuine quality shows a more moderate decline, typically fifty to sixty percent, as the audience that saw the film in the first week generates word-of-mouth that recruits new viewers in the second. Dhurandhar Part 1's second-week decline of approximately fifty-five percent from the first week placed it firmly in the quality-driven category, confirming that the first week's impressive numbers were the product of genuine audience satisfaction rather than marketing-driven curiosity.

The second-week trajectory also revealed the specific demographic dynamics of the film's audience. The second Saturday and Sunday showed growth rates from the second Friday that were even stronger than the corresponding growth rates in the first week, suggesting that the weekend audience in the second week included a significant proportion of first-time viewers who had been recruited by word-of-mouth during the preceding weekdays. This pattern indicates that the word-of-mouth was not merely sustaining attendance but actively expanding the audience, bringing in viewers who had not planned to see the film during its first week but who were persuaded by the recommendations of colleagues, friends, and family members who had seen it.

The third week maintained a level of consistency that surprised even the most optimistic analysts. Third-week collections of approximately 68 crore nett brought the domestic total to approximately 468 crore nett, and the film continued to occupy a significant number of screens despite the arrival of new releases competing for exhibition space. The third-week hold was driven in part by the growing social media conversation around the film's thematic depth and character complexity, which attracted audiences who typically do not attend Hindi action films but who were drawn by recommendations emphasizing the film's intellectual substance rather than its genre credentials.

The third week also marked the point at which the film's collection pattern began to diverge most significantly from the typical Hindi blockbuster trajectory. Most Hindi blockbusters show a steep third-week decline, often seventy to eighty percent from the second week, as the initial audience is exhausted and new releases divert attention. Dhurandhar Part 1's third-week decline from the second week was approximately forty-six percent, a hold that is exceptional by any standard and that indicated the film was continuing to find new audiences at a rate that more than compensated for the natural attrition of repeat viewings. This exceptional third-week hold is the statistical signature of a film whose audience base is expanding rather than contracting, a phenomenon that occurs only when word-of-mouth is sufficiently strong and sufficiently broad to recruit viewers who are outside the film's natural demographic.

The expansion of the audience base during the third week was visible in the changing demographic composition of the theatrical audience. Reports from exhibitors and audience surveys indicated that the third-week audience included a higher proportion of women, older viewers, and viewers who do not regularly attend Hindi action films than the first and second-week audiences had shown. This demographic broadening was driven by the cultural conversation around the film, which had by the third week expanded beyond the action-film community to encompass mainstream cultural discourse, with discussions of the film's themes, characters, and moral positions appearing in general-interest publications and social media spaces that do not typically engage with Hindi genre cinema. The entry of these non-traditional audiences into the theatrical market extended the film's commercial life and contributed to the third-week numbers that defied conventional expectations.

The fourth week and subsequent weeks demonstrated the long-tail performance that characterizes genuine event films. Fourth-week collections of approximately 42 crore nett, fifth-week collections of approximately 25 crore nett, and continued earnings through the sixth and seventh weeks brought the total domestic nett collection to approximately 620 crore. The film's theatrical run extended beyond the typical window for Hindi releases, with some screens continuing to show the film well into its eighth week, a duration that reflected the absence of a dominant competitor and the film's continuing ability to attract audiences at a level that justified exhibition.

The fourth and fifth weeks showed a collection pattern that reflected the transition from a mass-audience theatrical release to a more targeted late-run phenomenon. The audiences in these later weeks were disproportionately concentrated in multiplex screens in metropolitan areas, reflecting the continued interest of the urban audience that constituted the film's core demographic. Single-screen theaters in smaller markets began to reduce or eliminate shows during the fourth week as new releases arrived, but the multiplex screens in major cities maintained the film in their schedules because the per-show revenues, while lower than the peak weeks, remained commercially viable relative to the alternatives. This multiplex-concentrated late-run pattern is consistent with the film's overall commercial profile and reinforces the characterization of Dhurandhar Part 1 as a film whose primary commercial relationship is with the premium urban audience.

The extended theatrical run also benefited from the growing anticipation for the sequel, which had been confirmed during the marketing campaign and whose release was approaching. Audiences who had heard about the franchise but had not seen the first installment were motivated to catch up before the sequel's arrival, creating a second wave of attendance in the film's later weeks that extended its theatrical life beyond what the initial trajectory would have suggested. This sequel-driven second wave is a phenomenon that the Indian market had not previously experienced at this scale, and it demonstrated the commercial potential of franchise planning that the success of the Dhurandhar duology has since established as a model for the industry.

The sequel-driven second wave was particularly visible in the sixth and seventh weeks, when the marketing campaign for the sequel had begun in earnest and the cultural conversation about the franchise had intensified. Collections during these weeks, while individually modest, were significantly higher than what a standalone film would have generated at the same point in its run, indicating that the franchise context was adding a measurable commercial premium to the first installment's late-run performance. This premium is one of the commercial benefits of franchise planning that the Dhurandhar duology has demonstrated most convincingly, and it suggests that the total theatrical value of a franchise film exceeds the value of a standalone film not merely through the sequel's collection but through the enhanced longevity of the original.

The weekly trajectory of the domestic collection tells a story of consistent audience engagement rather than front-loaded curiosity. The first week's 275 crore was followed by 125, 68, 42, 25, and progressively smaller but commercially meaningful contributions in subsequent weeks, creating a collection curve that was flatter and more durable than the steep decline that typically characterizes Hindi blockbusters. This flat curve is the commercial signature of a film that is being driven by word-of-mouth rather than marketing, by audience satisfaction rather than pre-release hype, and its presence in Dhurandhar Part 1's collection data is the most compelling evidence of the film's genuine connection with its audience.

## Domestic vs Overseas Performance

The domestic-overseas split of Dhurandhar Part 1's worldwide gross reveals a commercial profile that differs significantly from the typical pattern of Hindi blockbusters, reflecting both the film's unusual content characteristics and its specific appeal to different audience segments.

The domestic market, comprising India's theatrical release across all regions and languages, accounted for approximately 620 crore nett of the film's worldwide gross, a domestic total that reflected the extraordinary depth and sustained consistency of the film's performance across every major Hindi-speaking theatrical circuit in the country. This domestic total, when converted to gross terms (approximately 730 crore gross), represented roughly fifty-four percent of the worldwide theatrical revenue. The domestic performance was characterized by strong numbers across the Hindi-speaking heartland, with particularly impressive collections from the Mumbai circuit, Delhi-UP, and Central India territories that together accounted for the majority of the domestic revenue.

The film's performance across different domestic circuits revealed interesting patterns that reflect the specific nature of its content and appeal. The Mumbai circuit, which includes the city of Mumbai and its surrounding areas, contributed the highest percentage of the domestic total, reflecting the city's large, educated, urban audience that was most receptive to the film's thematic complexity and narrative sophistication. The performance within the Mumbai circuit was itself differentiated, with premium multiplex screens in upscale locations generating per-screen averages that exceeded even the most optimistic projections, while single-screen theaters in more traditional markets showed strong but less exceptional performance. This differentiation within the Mumbai circuit reflects the film's primary appeal to the urban, educated audience that frequents multiplex screens, an appeal that was strong enough to generate record-level multiplex numbers while the single-screen performance, though healthy, reflected the A certificate's impact on the broader audience.

The Delhi-UP circuit followed closely, driven by strong performance in the national capital region and major cities across Uttar Pradesh. The performance in Delhi was particularly noteworthy because the city's audience has traditionally been responsive to patriotic content, and Dhurandhar Part 1's engagement with themes of national security and sacrifice resonated strongly with this audience even as the film's moral complexity challenged the simpler patriotic frameworks that Delhi audiences have historically favored. The UP performance was more concentrated in major cities like Lucknow, Kanpur, and Varanasi, with smaller cities showing proportionally lower numbers that reflected the thematic complexity's lesser appeal in markets where simpler entertainment is the dominant preference.

The Central India and Rajasthan circuits also performed well, indicating that the film's appeal extended beyond the metropolitan centers to include smaller cities where the combination of Ranveer Singh's star power and strong word-of-mouth created significant audience interest. The East India and South India Hindi circuits contributed smaller but commercially meaningful amounts, with the East India performance reflecting the moderate size of the Hindi-speaking theatrical market in that region and the South India performance reflecting the limited penetration of Hindi-language releases in markets dominated by regional-language cinema. The performance in these circuits, while not individually record-breaking, contributed meaningfully to the total and demonstrated that the film's appeal, while strongest in the Hindi heartland, extended to every market where Hindi-language cinema has a theatrical presence.

The A certificate, which had been a source of concern before the release, ultimately proved to be less of a commercial impediment than anticipated, partly because the film's target audience, adult viewers interested in sophisticated action cinema, was precisely the demographic least affected by the certification restriction.

The variation in tax structures across states means that the same gross collection produces different nett numbers depending on where the collection is generated, and Dhurandhar Part 1's concentration in higher-tax metropolitan markets slightly depressed its nett-to-gross ratio compared to films that derive a larger proportion of their collection from lower-tax states. This structural factor is relevant for industry comparisons, because films with different geographic collection profiles will show different nett-to-gross ratios even if their underlying audience engagement is comparable.

## Records Broken

The record-breaking achievements of Dhurandhar Part 1 span multiple categories and multiple markets, reflecting the comprehensive nature of the film's commercial dominance. Each record carries specific significance within the context of Indian cinema's commercial history, and the cumulative effect of these records is to establish the film as one of the most commercially significant Hindi-language releases in the history of the industry.

The film established a new benchmark for the highest opening weekend by a non-holiday Hindi release, surpassing the previous record holder with its 159 crore domestic weekend. This record is particularly significant because it was achieved despite the A certificate limitation and the extended runtime, both of which reduce the number of potential viewers per show and the total shows per day. The fact that the film overcame these structural disadvantages to set the record indicates that its per-show occupancy rates were extraordinarily high, suggesting that the shows that were available were selling at or near capacity throughout the opening weekend.

The opening weekend record also carries significance for its implications about the commercial viability of non-holiday releases. The conventional wisdom in Hindi cinema has long held that blockbuster-level openings require the support of a holiday or festival weekend, when the combination of increased leisure time and festive spending creates a favorable commercial environment. Dhurandhar Part 1's achievement of a record non-holiday opening demonstrates that sufficiently anticipated content can generate holiday-level demand on a regular weekend, challenging the industry's dependence on holiday timing and suggesting that content quality rather than calendar positioning may be the more important determinant of opening performance.

The first-week domestic collection of approximately 275 crore nett established another benchmark, placing the film among the three or four highest first-week grossers in Hindi cinema history. The speed with which the film reached the 100 crore, 200 crore, and subsequent milestones in the domestic market set or matched several category records, and the consistency of the daily collections across the first week indicated a film that was performing at a uniformly high level rather than one whose numbers were inflated by a single spectacular day.

The milestone achievement records deserve specific attention because they reveal the trajectory of the film's commercial accumulation. Dhurandhar Part 1 crossed the 100 crore nett mark on its third day of release, a speed that placed it among the fastest Hindi films to reach that milestone. The 200 crore nett mark was crossed during the first week, and the 300 crore nett mark was crossed during the second week, with each subsequent milestone achieved at a pace that reflected the film's extraordinary holding power. The 500 crore nett mark was crossed during the fourth week, and the eventual total of approximately 620 crore nett was reached over the course of a seven-week theatrical run that maintained commercially meaningful collections throughout. The pace of milestone achievement, while not the fastest in absolute terms for every individual milestone, was among the most consistent, with the time between successive milestones remaining relatively stable rather than increasing sharply as it does for films with front-loaded collection patterns.

The worldwide gross of approximately 1,350 crore placed Dhurandhar Part 1 among the highest-grossing Hindi films ever made at the time of its release, a position it held until being surpassed by its own sequel just months later. The worldwide record was particularly impressive because it was achieved with a December release date, a period that is traditionally less commercially favorable than the peak festival and holiday windows that the highest-grossing Hindi films typically target. The film's ability to generate blockbuster-level business during a traditionally non-peak release window demonstrated convincingly and decisively that audience demand for the right content can override the seasonal patterns that traditionally govern Hindi box office performance.

The film also set records for the highest collection by an A-certified Hindi film, surpassing the previous benchmarks by a significant margin and demonstrating that the A certificate, long regarded as a commercial handicap, need not prevent a film from achieving top-tier commercial performance. This record carries particular significance for the industry because it challenges the conventional wisdom that commercial viability requires a U/A or U certificate and may encourage filmmakers and studios to pursue mature content without the fear that certification restrictions will doom the film commercially. ## What Drove the Numbers

The most significant and commercially consequential driver was word-of-mouth, the organic recommendations that viewers who had seen the film provided to friends, family members, and social media followers.
