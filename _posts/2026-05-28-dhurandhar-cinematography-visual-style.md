The camera in Dhurandhar does not follow the story. It tells a second one. This distinction is the foundation of any serious analysis of the franchise's visual language, because the conventional description of cinematography as "serving the story" misses what the franchise actually does with its visual apparatus. The camera in most commercial films confirms what the dialogue and performance have already communicated: it is a recording instrument that chooses favorable angles and flattering light and efficient cuts. The camera in Dhurandhar argues. It takes positions. It frames Hamza in ways that tell the audience about his psychological state before any dialogue has been spoken, uses color temperature as a geographic and emotional classifier, and moves with a specific logic of stability and instability that tracks the cover's condition in real time. The visual style is the franchise's second screenplay, and reading it alongside the first produces a richer understanding of what the franchise is doing than either screenplay can provide alone.

This article argues that the franchise's visual language operates on three simultaneous levels that must be read together rather than separately. The geographic level is where color and light distinguish the franchise's two primary spaces, Lyari and Delhi, with a specificity that makes every frame a locational statement as much as a narrative one. The psychological level is where camera movement and framing track the cover's condition, mapping the gap between what Hamza performs and what he experiences in purely visual terms, without requiring dialogue to communicate the difference. The moral level is where the franchise's specific visual choices about how to stage violence, how to frame institutional power, and how to position the audience relative to the action constitute an argument about the relationship between the state and the individual that runs parallel to the verbal argument the screenplay makes through dialogue. Understanding all three levels simultaneously is what distinguishes a visual analysis of the franchise from a technical inventory of its cinematographic choices.

The analysis that follows is not a technical manual. Technical details appear when they illuminate storytelling effects rather than when they demonstrate knowledge of filmmaking vocabulary. [The franchise's complete Part 1 analysis](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/03/19/complete-analysis-dhurandhar-part-1/) addresses the narrative dimension of what the camera depicts. This article addresses the visual argument the camera makes about what the narrative means, which is a different analytical task requiring a different set of analytical tools.

The claim that the franchise's visual style constitutes a second screenplay requires one more dimension of defense before the analysis can proceed to its specific arguments. The conventional understanding of visual style in commercial cinema treats visual choices as supplements to the primary communication system of dialogue and plot: the visuals make the dialogue and plot more engaging, more atmospheric, more polished. The franchise's visual style does more than this. In specific sequences, which this article will identify and analyze, the visual communication is primary rather than supplementary: the camera is saying something that the dialogue is not saying, is making an argument that the verbal text cannot make because the cover's logic forbids Hamza from saying what he means. The visual language is not a supplement to the story. It is the story's honest voice, doing in the visual domain what [the score does in the sonic domain](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/05/04/dhurandhar-soundtrack-background-score-analysis/): expressing what the surface of the cover cannot express. To miss the visual language is to miss half the franchise's storytelling.

## The Color Grammar: Geography as Psychology

The most immediately legible element of the franchise's visual language is its color palette, because the palette works as a geographic classifier before it works as anything else. Lyari is amber and ochre and the warm dirty gold of sun-baked concrete, the saturated earthy tones of a neighborhood where the built environment has absorbed decades of sun and human activity. Delhi is cool and desaturated, the institutional grey of offices and corridors and controlled environments where temperature and light are managed rather than received. Every frame in either location is colored by these palettes, and the palettes' consistency is so complete across both films that the audience learns to read color temperature as a locational signal before the dialogue has confirmed the setting.

This geographic reading of color is the palette's primary function, but it is not its only one. The warm amber of Lyari is also the color of warmth as an emotional quality: the color of a place that feels inhabited rather than administered, of a community that has generated its own visual culture through the accumulation of human presence rather than through institutional design. The cool grey of Delhi is also the color of institutional distance: the color of a place where decisions are made about people who are not in the room, where the human cost of those decisions is translated into strategic language before being communicated upward through hierarchies that are organized to absorb the cost without registering it as such. The palette is doing geography and psychology simultaneously, and the two dimensions reinforce each other: Lyari's warmth is both the warmth of a specific place and the warmth of the human relationships Hamza has built there, and Delhi's coolness is both the coolness of a specific institutional environment and the emotional temperature of the institutional relationship between Hamza and the operation's architects.

The franchise's most analytically revealing use of the color palette is in the sequences that occupy the boundary between the two geographies: the brief scenes that occur in transit between Lyari and Delhi, in airports and vehicles and transitional spaces that do not belong fully to either color world. These transitional sequences are shot in a palette that splits the difference between the two poles, neither warm nor cool but a specific quality of neutrality that carries its own emotional register. The neutrality is not merely compositional. It is the visual expression of what Hamza occupies in these transitional moments: the space between identities, neither Hamza nor Jaskirat, neither embedded operative nor institutional asset, but the specific kind of nowhere that the cover requires him to inhabit whenever he crosses between the two worlds it requires him to maintain.

The color story evolves between Part 1 and Part 2 in ways that the analysis must acknowledge because they track the cover's evolution rather than simply repeating the established grammar. In Part 1, the contrast between Lyari's amber warmth and Delhi's institutional grey is presented as the franchise's fundamental visual opposition: clear, consistent, almost diagrammatic in its legibility. In Part 2, the contrast begins to blur in specific sequences. The Lyari sequences in Part 2 occasionally carry a slightly cooler undertone beneath the amber, a quality of desaturation that was absent from Part 1's Lyari photography. The Delhi sequences carry slightly warmer undertones in certain scenes involving Bansal, a quality that reflects the emotional temperature of those specific interactions rather than the institutional temperature of the setting. The blurring is subtle and does not collapse the opposition. But it registers the decade's accumulated impact on both the world and the man who has been living in it.

## Camera Movement and the Cover's Condition

If the color palette is the franchise's geographic and emotional classifier, the camera movement is its real-time psychological instrument. The specific relationship between camera stability and the cover's operational condition is the franchise's most consistently deployed visual storytelling mechanism, and it is the one that rewards analytical attention most generously because it operates at a level of subtlety that many viewers register emotionally without identifying analytically.

The franchise's camera is locked down when the cover is functioning as designed. The locked camera in the Lyari sequences does not signal static drama: it signals managed performance. When Hamza is in full cover, handling social interactions with the fluency that years of practice have built, the camera observes from a stable, fixed position that communicates the same quality of professional control that the performance itself projects. The stability is not a default position. It is a chosen position, selected because it places the audience in the position of an observer who is watching a controlled performance rather than experiencing an uncontrolled situation. The audience watches the cover succeed, and the camera's stability is the visual confirmation of the success.

The franchise's camera becomes handheld when the cover is under pressure. The shift is not dramatic in the conventional sense of a sudden technical change: it is incremental, a gradual introduction of camera movement into sequences where the preceding shot was stable, a progressive increase in the camera's micro-instability as the scene's pressure escalates. The most sustained use of the handheld-under-pressure approach is in the warehouse fight sequence analyzed in [the action sequences ranking](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/05/14/dhurandhar-action-sequences-ranked-analyzed/), but the technique appears throughout both films in non-action contexts: in the scenes where a social interaction takes an unexpected turn, in the sequences where the operational environment shifts in ways that require Hamza to improvise rather than execute. The camera's instability tracks the cover's instability, and the audience who learns this grammar reads each increment of camera movement as an increment of operational risk.

The most analytically important use of camera movement in the franchise is the specific instance where the camera movement is wrong relative to the scene's visible content. These are the sequences where the camera becomes agitated during what appears to be a successful social interaction, or where the camera becomes unusually still during what appears to be a moment of crisis. The wrongness is the franchise's most sophisticated visual storytelling technique: when the camera is agitated during apparent success, it is telling the audience that the success is more precarious than it appears. When the camera is still during apparent crisis, it is telling the audience that Hamza has entered a mode of extreme professional control that is more frightening than agitation would be. The gap between what the visible content communicates and what the camera movement communicates is the franchise's visual equivalent of the gap between what Hamza performs and what he experiences, and reading the gap is the most analytically rewarding dimension of the franchise's visual language.

The Steadicam sequences, which appear in both films at specific and deliberate moments, deserve their own analysis because they occupy a specific position in the franchise's movement grammar between the locked camera's stability and the handheld's instability. Steadicam produces a movement that is smooth but not fixed, that follows action with the fluid tracking quality of a human observer who is moving through space rather than watching from a fixed position. The franchise uses Steadicam primarily in the Lyari sequences that show Hamza moving through the neighborhood as a community member rather than as an operative under pressure: the daily-life sequences, the market walks, the neighborhood gatherings. In these sequences, the Steadicam movement communicates belonging rather than pressure: the camera moves through the neighborhood with the ease of someone who knows it, which is the visual expression of what the cover's decade of construction has produced.

The Steadicam Lyari sequences are the franchise's visual argument that the cover has succeeded in its most demanding objective: making Hamza genuinely at home in a world that is not his. This is a claim the dialogue cannot make directly without forcing Hamza to acknowledge something about his own psychological state that the cover's logic forbids him to acknowledge. The camera makes the claim instead, through the specific quality of the movement that says: this person knows where they are going and belongs in the space they are moving through. The visual argument is available to the audience throughout the franchise's middle sections, when the verbal argument is deliberately withheld, which is why the Steadicam sequences are among the most emotionally unsettling in the franchise: the camera is showing you something that should be a triumph of the cover's construction and is simultaneously a measure of the original self's displacement.

One further dimension of the camera movement analysis deserves explicit treatment: the relationship between camera movement and the score's leitmotif system. [The soundtrack analysis](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/05/04/dhurandhar-soundtrack-background-score-analysis/) establishes that the score uses the Jaskirat motif's presence or absence to communicate the original identity's proximity to the cover's surface. The camera movement grammar operates in tandem with this musical grammar: when the Jaskirat motif sounds, the camera typically shifts from the movement register appropriate to the cover's confident operation toward a slightly different register that communicates disruption or involuntary access. The two systems are not identical, because they are serving the same information through different sensory channels. But they are designed to be consistent at the psychological level, creating a unified audiovisual argument that the audience receives as a single experience.

## Framing and Power: What the Composition Communicates

The franchise's shot composition is a continuous argument about power, a visual system that uses the relationship between figures in the frame to communicate who controls a scene before a single line of dialogue establishes the dynamic verbally. The analysis of this system requires close attention to specific compositional choices rather than to general principles, because the franchise's visual intelligence is visible most clearly in the specific rather than the typical.

The two-shot is the franchise's primary instrument for communicating the current state of any relationship. In the Hamza-Rehman scenes, the two-shot composition changes across the franchise's timeline in ways that track the relationship's evolution. Early Part 1 two-shots place Hamza and Rehman at approximately equal heights in the frame with a small spatial separation that reads as professional rather than personal: the composition of two men who are doing business together. Later Part 1 two-shots close the spatial gap and equalize the height relationship, which is the visual expression of intimacy: the composition of two men who know each other. Part 2 two-shots introduce specific compositional asymmetries, slight differences in height or position that communicate the specific tension of a relationship in which the emotional reality has changed while the social surface has not. The two-shot is not a fixed composition in the franchise. It is a dynamic one, and tracking its evolution across the franchise's timeline produces a visual biography of the Hamza-Rehman relationship that runs parallel to the verbal biography the dialogue provides.

The close-up in the franchise operates with a specific logic that distinguishes it from the conventional use of close-ups in Bollywood action cinema. The conventional Bollywood close-up is an emphasis instrument: it is used when the director wants the audience to pay maximum attention to a face, typically at moments of emotional climax or dramatic revelation. The franchise uses close-ups this way in some sequences, but it also uses them in a second, more analytically interesting mode: as instruments of examination rather than of emphasis. In these examination close-ups, the camera moves to a very close position and holds there while the character is in the midst of performing normality, while Hamza is having a conversation or eating a meal or participating in a social gathering. The close-up examination does not catch a moment of emotional intensity. It catches a face that is performing something other than what it is experiencing, and the specific quality of the performance, the micro-expressions that Ranveer Singh builds into the cover's surface, is only visible at close range and with the sustained attention that the held close-up provides.

The wide shot is used in the franchise with a geographic and psychological precision that corresponds to its use of the close-up. Wide shots in the Lyari sequences function as community-presence shots: the camera pulls back to show Hamza within the neighborhood's spatial fabric, surrounded by the built environment and the human activity that constitute the world he inhabits. The wide shot in this context communicates belonging and embeddedness: Hamza is part of the scene rather than the subject of it. Wide shots in the Delhi sequences function differently: they pull back to show Hamza within the institutional architecture of the RAW environment, surrounded by the controlled spaces and the specific human arrangements that constitute the world he reports to. In the Delhi wide shots, the pull-back communicates not belonging but exposure: Hamza is a specific person in an institutional space that is organized around purposes larger than and indifferent to his individual experience. The same technical choice communicates opposite emotional states depending on which geography it is deployed in, and this flexibility is the franchise's most sophisticated use of the wide shot's conventional language.

The over-the-shoulder shot, which is the franchise's primary instrument for depicting conversations between two characters, is used with a directionality that rewards analysis. When the camera is positioned over Hamza's shoulder, the audience sees the other character from Hamza's perspective, which is the cover's perspective: the perspective of someone managing an interaction. When the camera is positioned over the other character's shoulder, the audience sees Hamza from outside the cover, which is the audience's privileged perspective: the perspective that can see Hamza as Hamza rather than as Hamza performing Hamza. The franchise uses this directionality deliberately, favoring the over-the-other-character's-shoulder position in the sequences where it wants the audience to observe the cover rather than to inhabit it, and favoring the over-Hamza's-shoulder position in the sequences where it wants the audience to experience the social interaction from within the cover's operational logic. The shift in shoulder position is the franchise's visual grammar for the shift between experiencing the cover and examining it.

The franchise's deepest compositional achievement is in the sequences that occupy the space between examination and experience: the sequences where the camera is positioned to show both Hamza's face and his interlocutor's face in the same frame, at approximately equal size and prominence, with neither positioned as the observer and neither as the observed. These equal-prominence two-shots are the franchise's visual equivalent of the franchise's moral ambiguity: they place the audience in the position of a witness to an interaction between two people who are each fully present and neither of whom the visual grammar allows the audience to privilege or dismiss. In these frames, the Lyari characters are not the backdrop to Hamza's operational drama. They are co-inhabitants of a visual space that belongs equally to all of them, and the visual equality is the franchise's most direct statement about the moral weight that the operation's eventual betrayal of these characters carries.

## Lighting as Storytelling: The Moral Texture of Illumination

The franchise's lighting philosophy is among its most analytically underexamined visual dimensions, partly because lighting's effect on the viewer is often absorbed without being consciously registered. The specific decisions about what to illuminate and what to leave in shadow, about the quality and direction of available light versus controlled light, and about the specific emotional registers that different lighting conditions produce, are among the franchise's most consistently deployed storytelling instruments.

The domestic Lyari sequences are lit with a commitment to available and practical lighting that is unusual for a major commercial Hindi film production. Rehman's household, Jameel's neighborhood interactions, the community gatherings that Hamza attends as part of his cover life: all of these are lit primarily with the practical light sources that the spaces themselves provide, the warm incandescent bulbs of domestic interiors, the specific quality of late-afternoon sun through high windows, the ambient glow of tea-stall neon on a nighttime street. The commitment to practical lighting is not merely a stylistic choice. It is a characterization choice: domestic spaces look like domestic spaces when lit by domestic light sources rather than by cinematographic lighting that sculpts the space for visual drama. The franchise's Lyari domestics look like homes, and they look like homes partly because they are lit like homes.

The operational sequences are lit with a different philosophy that reflects the different quality of the situations they depict. The sequences in which Hamza is actively working, navigating a social interaction with operational stakes, managing a cover test or a near-exposure situation, are lit with a controlled quality that introduces specific shadow and contrast that the domestic sequences do not contain. The controlled lighting of the operational sequences is not merely atmospheric. It is the visual equivalent of the cover's managed performance: both the lighting and the performance are doing the work of constructing a specific impression rather than simply existing. The franchise is lighting the cover as cover, with the sculpted quality that distinguishes intentional presentation from natural existence.

The night scenes in both films are the franchise's most dramatically lit and most analytically revealing lighting choices. Night in Lyari is lit with the specific quality of urban nighttime in a dense South Asian neighborhood: patches of light from commercial establishments and domestic windows, ambient glow from street-level activity, the specific darkness of the spaces between light sources that is deeper than the diffuse nighttime of more brightly lit urban environments. This specific nighttime quality is the visual expression of the neighborhood's ambient unpredictability: in Lyari at night, you can see where the light is and you cannot fully see what the dark contains, and this visual structure mirrors the operational situation of a deep-cover agent who knows his immediate environment well but cannot fully monitor everything that the operational ecology conceals.

The franchise's use of shadow deserves specific analytical attention because shadow is used not just as an absence of light but as an active storytelling instrument. In several of the Hamza-Rehman meeting sequences, Dhar positions the characters so that shadow falls across specific parts of the frame in ways that visually separate the characters from their own faces, or that obscure portions of the room that the narrative has not yet given the audience reason to examine. This use of shadow as selective disclosure is the cinematographic equivalent of the cover's selective disclosure: the frame shows you what it wants you to see and conceals what it is not yet ready to reveal, which is the visual grammar of a story organized around the management of what is known and what is hidden.

The interplay between natural and artificial light in the Karachi exterior sequences produces one of the franchise's most beautiful and most analytically significant visual qualities: the specific character of South Asian urban daylight in a dense, low-rise neighborhood, where the sun's direct illumination is periodically interrupted by the shadows of adjacent buildings and where the reflected light from painted walls and metallic surfaces creates a complex ambient environment that is simultaneously warm and variable. This quality of light is specific to a certain kind of urban space in a certain part of the world, and the franchise's attention to it is part of the same commitment to geographic specificity that characterizes [its production design](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/05/11/karachi-underworld-lyari-dhurandhar/) more broadly. The light is Karachi's light, which is a level of visual specificity that most productions set in Pakistan do not approach.

## The Action Cinematography: Beauty and Its Refusal

The franchise's approach to photographing its action sequences is the dimension of its visual language that most directly connects to its philosophical claims about what kind of experience its violence should be. [The action sequences analysis](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/05/14/dhurandhar-action-sequences-ranked-analyzed/) examines the sequences themselves in detail; the cinematography analysis examines the specific visual choices that produce their specific effects.

The warehouse fight in Part 1, which is the franchise's most analytically discussed action sequence, is shot with a camera strategy that actively resists the conventional action cinematography's organizing principle of the beautiful shot. Conventional action cinematography is organized around the principle that each shot should maximize the visual impression of the action it depicts: the camera should be positioned to show the stunt work at its most impressive, the editing should be timed to land each significant blow with maximum aesthetic impact, and the overall visual experience should produce something that could be described as choreography. The franchise's warehouse fight is shot as if the camera is in the room rather than filming it: the camera is inside the spatial chaos of the confrontation rather than positioned above or outside it, and it moves with a quality of being buffeted rather than being directed. The audience does not watch this fight. They survive it.

The specific technical means by which this survival-rather-than-spectacle effect is achieved involves a combination of lens choice and camera placement that produces a slight visual distortion at the fight's most intense moments. The franchise uses lenses in the action sequences that introduce a subtle barrel distortion at the frame's edges, making the walls of the warehouse seem slightly closer than they actually are and the space slightly more compressed. This distortion is not consciously registered by most viewers: it is felt rather than seen, producing the specific quality of spatial constriction that the fight sequence is designed to generate. The distortion is the visual expression of Hamza's psychological experience of the fight, which is not the open-space experience of a trained fighter operating in a controlled environment but the constricted experience of someone fighting for survival in a space that is actively working against him.

## Visual Quotations: What the Camera Has Seen Before

The franchise's visual language is not invented from nothing. It is in conversation with a specific set of cinematic references, and identifying those references illuminates what the franchise is doing visually by locating it within a broader tradition of how serious cinema has approached similar visual problems.

The most discussed visual reference in the franchise's Karachi sequences is the Sicario aesthetic: the specific quality of visual geography that Denis Villeneuve and Roger Deakins established for the American-Mexican borderland in the 2015 film. The Sicario visual language is organized around the principle that criminal geography is best shown at a distance that reveals its structural dimensions rather than at a proximity that emphasizes its human details: the aerial shots that show the organization of the landscape, the long-lens observations that compress the space between watcher and watched, the specific quality of surveillance distance that makes the criminal ecology legible as a system rather than as a collection of individual actors. The franchise's Karachi panoramic sequences use similar visual strategies, and the shared vocabulary is not coincidental: both films are making visual arguments about how a specific kind of dangerous place should be shown, and the argument they share is that showing it from the structural perspective of the institution that is trying to understand it is more honest than showing it from the dramatic perspective of an individual within it.

The surveillance sequences in the franchise reference the visual grammar of the le Carre adaptations, particularly Tomas Alfredson's Tinker Tailor Soldier Spy, in which surveillance is shot as observation: patient, distant, visually restrained in ways that communicate the specific temporal quality of intelligence work as waiting rather than acting. The franchise's handler-communication sequences and its Hamza-watching-Rehman sequences share this quality of patient observation, the camera holding in positions of watchfulness rather than seeking the angles that would maximize dramatic impact.

The violence in the franchise shares visual kinship with the Indian cinema violence of Anurag Kashyap's work, particularly Gangs of Wasseypur, in its refusal to aestheticize the physical reality of bodies in conflict. Kashyap's violence is shot with a quality of documentary proximity: the camera is close enough that the physical reality of what is happening is undeniable, and the editing is timed to the action rather than to the conventional dramatic beat, which means that violence ends when it ends rather than when the rhythm would be most satisfying. The franchise's violence has this same quality of physical honesty, and the shared visual kinship is the clearest evidence of a conscious commitment to the kind of cinema that takes violence seriously rather than using it as spectacle.

The franchise's domestic Lyari sequences share visual kinship with a different and less frequently cited tradition: the kitchen-sink realism of British social cinema from the late 1950s and early 1960s, and its Indian equivalent in the parallel cinema movement of the 1970s and 1980s. The specific quality of the franchise's domestic cinematography, with its commitment to practical lighting, its preference for holding on faces in ordinary domestic contexts rather than cutting away to the next plot point, and its treatment of ordinary daily activity as cinematographically worthy subject matter, is inherited from this tradition of using the camera to argue that ordinary domestic life is worth the same quality of visual attention that commercial cinema reserves for extraordinary events. The franchise's Lyari domestics are arguing that the community Hamza has inhabited is worth this quality of attention, and the visual argument is as important as the narrative argument for establishing the community's moral weight.

The franchise's most distinctive visual quotation, the one that has generated the most commentary in film criticism, is the specific quality of the surveillance drone footage that appears in several of both films' Delhi sequences. The institutional perspective of surveillance technology, the flat, top-down view that reduces human movement to geometrical patterns, is the franchise's visual argument about how institutions see the people they deploy: not as individuals with faces and histories but as patterns of movement that can be assessed for operational utility. The drone footage is the visual grammar of institutional abstraction, and its placement in the sequences involving RAW's institutional decision-making communicates the specific way that institutions process the human cost of the operations they authorize: by translating human individuals into operational variables that the surveillance grammar renders anonymous.

The franchise also deploys visual quotations from the global tradition of crime film that are less explicitly coded but equally deliberate. The specific quality of the Lyari rooftop sequences, with their vertical geography and their use of height to communicate both surveillance advantage and vulnerability, shares visual grammar with sequences in the Brazilian film City of God that use the favela's rooftop landscape in similar ways. The specific quality of the market fight sequences, with their use of the crowd as both witness and concealment, has visual kinship with similar sequences in Korean and Hong Kong action cinema that use dense urban crowds as choreographic environments rather than as mere background. The franchise is in dialogue with a global tradition of crime and espionage cinema, and the dialogue is conducted through visual grammar rather than through verbal homage.

## Where the Franchise Falls Short Visually

The cinematography analysis must acknowledge where the franchise's visual ambitions exceed their consistent execution, because the gaps between aspiration and achievement reveal something about the specific pressures that commercial production exerts on artistic intention.

The most visible inconsistency is in the Part 2 action sequences, several of which are shot with a visual grammar that is more aligned with conventional Bollywood action cinematography than with the franchise's own established approach. The harbor chase sequence, while technically ambitious and visually spectacular, is shot with the kind of aerial-plus-ground alternation that produces beautiful images rather than the raw physical immediacy that the franchise's best action sequences generate. The visual beauty of the harbor chase is not a failure in isolation: it is a very well-made piece of action cinema. But it sits slightly outside the franchise's visual argument about violence, which insists on ugliness and physical cost rather than on beauty and physical capability, and the inconsistency is detectable to an audience that has absorbed the franchise's visual grammar in Part 1 and the early Part 2 sequences.

The Delhi institutional sequences in Part 2 are occasionally over-lit in ways that make the institutional spaces feel more dramatically staged than the franchise's own visual argument about institutional distance would require. Institutional spaces should feel controlled, but controlled light in service of institutional documentation is different from controlled light in service of dramatic emphasis, and Part 2 occasionally crosses the line between the two in ways that make the Delhi sequences feel slightly more like a drama about institutions than like a documentation of how institutions actually look and feel. This is a minor inconsistency, and it is visible primarily to viewers who are attending to the franchise's visual argument at the analytical level. But it represents a small retreat from the visual honesty that the franchise's best sequences maintain.

The third visual inconsistency is in the treatment of daylight in several of the Karachi exterior sequences in Part 2, which are occasionally shot with the specific quality of golden-hour light that is more associated with commercial photography than with the documentary proximity that the franchise's best Lyari sequences achieve. The golden-hour Karachi is beautiful in a way that the franchise's own aesthetic argument works against: it aestheticizes the neighborhood in ways that compromise the raw authenticity that the franchise's visual language is designed to produce. These moments are brief and infrequent, but they are the visual equivalent of the franchise's occasional concessions to commercial imperatives at the expense of artistic consistency.

## The Bigger Argument: A Visual Screenplay

The franchise's visual language is, at its best, a complete argument about the story it is telling that runs parallel to and sometimes ahead of the verbal argument the screenplay makes. The claim that the visual style constitutes a second screenplay is not merely a metaphor. It is a precise description of how the franchise's best sequences function: the camera's decisions about what to show, how to show it, and how to move through it are storytelling decisions with the same analytical weight as dialogue and plot, and reading them produces a second layer of meaning that the verbal layer alone cannot supply.

The most complete expression of this second screenplay is the sequence described in [the soundtrack analysis](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/05/04/dhurandhar-soundtrack-background-score-analysis/) as the balcony scene: Hamza stepping out after a dinner, the camera holding on his face for several seconds, the score sounding the longing phrase beneath the Lyari ambient texture. The visual analysis of this sequence adds a dimension that the soundtrack analysis alone cannot capture: the specific quality of the shot's composition, which places Hamza at the edge of the frame rather than at its center, with more space on the side toward Lyari than on the side toward the apartment interior. The composition is a visual argument: the space in the frame that leads toward Lyari is larger than the space that leads back to the self that the apartment represents, which is the camera's way of showing that the balance between identities has shifted. Hamza is no longer a person who inhabits Lyari. He is a person who is inhabited by it.

This kind of compositional argument, where the frame's visual organization communicates something about the character's psychological state that neither the dialogue nor the performance states explicitly, is what the franchise's visual language achieves at its most sophisticated. It is a level of visual storytelling that most commercial Bollywood films do not attempt and that the franchise demonstrates is both achievable and commercially viable. The [franchise's impact on Bollywood filmmaking](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/06/20/why-dhurandhar-changed-bollywood-forever/) will include, among its longer-term effects, a generation of filmmakers who have seen what it looks like when a commercial Hindi film uses its visual apparatus as a storytelling instrument rather than as a recording device.

The visual argument the franchise makes is ultimately an argument about what cinema can be when it takes seriously the responsibility to communicate through every available channel simultaneously. Dialogue is one channel. Performance is another. Sound and score are a third. And the visual language, the color and movement and framing and lighting and composition, is a fourth, and in the franchise's best sequences it is the most precise of all four, doing in the frame what none of the others can do as efficiently or as completely. The records the franchise broke commercially are the evidence that audiences are prepared to receive this kind of visual storytelling. The visual analysis is the guide to understanding what that storytelling actually does, and why the [complete franchise](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/06/17/dhurandhar-franchise-complete-guide/) rewards repeated viewing in ways that films organized around verbal rather than visual storytelling typically cannot match. You can watch it again and hear things you missed. But you can also watch it again and see things you missed, and the seeing is often more precise than the hearing when the film is working at the level this one does.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Q: Who was the cinematographer for Dhurandhar?

Dhurandhar's cinematography is the product of a close collaboration between Aditya Dhar and his director of photography, whose specific visual approach reflects the kind of integrated pre-production development between director and DP that produces a genuinely unified visual language rather than a talented DP executing a director's verbal instructions. The visual philosophy described throughout this article, the color grammar distinguishing Lyari from Delhi, the camera movement tracking the cover's condition, the specific lighting approach for domestic versus operational sequences, reflects a visual intelligence that was developed collaboratively across the pre-production period rather than arrived at through individual creative decisions. Dhar has credited his DP with significant creative ownership of the specific visual choices that produce the franchise's most analytically interesting sequences.

### Q: What makes the Dhurandhar color palette different from other Bollywood spy films?

The franchise's color palette is not merely warmer than most Bollywood spy films, though it is that. The lesson internalization would produce films that ask, for every cinematographic choice: what is this communicating, and is what it communicates consistent with what the story requires at this specific moment? This is the question the franchise's camera appears to have been asked for every sequence in both films, and the answer to that question, multiplied across the thousands of shots that constitute two three-and-a-half-hour films, is the franchise's visual achievement. The future of Bollywood cinematography is the future of how many filmmakers learn to ask this question consistently and answer it with the same quality of analytical commitment that the franchise demonstrates.

### Q: How does the franchise's visual language handle the sequences where Hamza communicates with his RAW handlers?

The handler-communication sequences are among the franchise's most precisely calibrated cinematographic achievements because they must convey simultaneous and contradictory emotional realities: the relief of dropping the cover, the habit of professional restraint that persists even in supposedly safe contexts, and the specific quality of a relationship that is defined by institutional hierarchy even when conducted in physically informal settings. The visual grammar for these sequences uses a specific neutralizing of the color temperature: neither the warm amber of the full-cover Lyari sequences nor the cool grey of the formal RAW institutional environments, but a specific quality of visual in-between that is the visual expression of the psychological in-between these communication windows represent. Hamza in a handler-communication sequence is neither Hamza nor Jaskirat but the specific third thing that exists in the space between them: the operational asset communicating through a secure channel, a role that has its own specific visual register distinct from either identity.

The editing rhythm in these sequences is also distinctive: slower than the action sequences, faster than the deep domestic Lyari sequences, with a specific quality of controlled pacing that reflects the communication discipline that secure handler contact requires. The franchise does not shoot these sequences as emotional releases, as they might be in a film that treated the cover as pure performance and the handler contact as the moment when the performance is dropped. The franchise treats them as a different kind of performance: the performance of operational professionalism in a context where that performance is still required, which means the visual grammar does not relax into the domestic register even though the narrative context would support relaxation. The camera maintains its watchfulness because the character maintains his, and the visual grammar and the performance grammar are mutually reinforcing.

### Q: What is the most visually precise single shot in the entire franchise?

The most visually precise single shot, evaluated by the criterion of how much analytical information is compressed into a single frame, is the over-the-shoulder shot in the final meeting between Hamza and Rehman before the operation's execution phase begins. The shot is positioned over Hamza's shoulder in the conventional two-person conversation angle, but the specific composition within this conventional setup is extraordinary in its precision: Rehman's face fills the right portion of the frame, while the left edge of the frame includes a portion of the space behind Hamza that shows, in soft focus, the specific Lyari environment that the operation has been building toward this moment in for a decade. The composition holds both Rehman as the specific person Hamza is facing and Lyari as the world Hamza will be instrumental in disrupting, simultaneously and in the same frame. The audience cannot process both in the same instant: the eye moves between the sharp focus of Rehman's face and the soft focus of the Lyari background, and in that movement between focuses the franchise's entire moral argument is compressed into a single visual experience. It is the most precise shot in the franchise because it accomplishes the most in the most compact form, and it is the shot that most completely demonstrates what the franchise means when it claims that its visual style is a second screenplay.

### Q: How does the franchise's visual approach to Lyari's street scenes establish authenticity?

The street-level visual treatment of Lyari across both films is the franchise's most sustained commitment to location specificity in its cinematography. Most Bollywood productions set in foreign or semi-foreign environments use their locations as backgrounds for foreground action, treating the street as a visual context that establishes geography before the narrative moves to more controlled interior spaces. The franchise reverses this priority in specific sequences: the street is the subject rather than the background, and the camera's attentiveness to the street's specific visual quality, the density of pedestrian traffic, the specific rhythm of commercial activity, the particular visual relationship between the built environment and its human inhabitants, is the cinematographic expression of the franchise's commitment to treating Lyari as a world rather than as a setting. The street-level visual authenticity is not decorative. It is the visual argument that the world Hamza inhabits is real enough to make a genuine identity claim on the person who inhabits it, which is the precondition for the franchise's moral argument about what the operation ultimately costs.

### Q: What is the one visual technique from Dhurandhar that every aspiring Indian filmmaker should study?

The visual technique most worth studying is the one most difficult to execute without its specific creative preconditions: the held examination close-up deployed at a moment of ordinary rather than extraordinary narrative content. The technique is simple to describe and difficult to deploy correctly because it requires all three of the following simultaneously: a shot held past the conventional commercial editing rhythm, a face capable of sustaining examination without either collapsing into visible performance or retreating into blankness, and a narrative moment that is rich enough in psychological subtext to justify the examination without being so dramatically explosive that the examination feels understated. The franchise achieves all three consistently in its best sequences, and studying where it succeeds and where it falls slightly short of the technique's full potential is the most direct available education in how visual storytelling at this level actually works. It is not the most technically impressive technique in the franchise's vocabulary. It is the most analytically productive to study precisely because its requirements are so clear and its execution challenges are so illuminating.

### Q: How does the franchise's editing use music and visual transitions together?

The franchise's transitions between sequences, from Lyari to Delhi and back, from action to aftermath, from cover to handler communication, are edited with a specific attention to the relationship between the visual cut and the score's simultaneous state that produces transitions with two simultaneous tracks of meaning rather than one. A cut from a warm Lyari sequence to a cool Delhi sequence while the score is in the process of transitioning from the Lyari ambient texture to the RAW motif produces a moment of audiovisual overlap in which the audience is leaving one world sonically and entering another visually, which is the audiovisual expression of the specific quality of transition that Hamza himself experiences: not a clean switch between identities but a process of transition in which neither world fully releases its claim before the other asserts its own. The franchise's best transitions are not cuts. They are arrivals that began before the cut happened, which is the visual-editorial expression of what the decade of deep-cover work has produced: a person for whom the transition between worlds is never clean because neither world ever fully lets go.

### Q: What is the clearest proof that the franchise's cinematography is intentional rather than incidental?

The clearest proof is the consistency of the visual grammar across two separately shot films produced approximately two years apart, with all the production variation that entails: different shooting days, different lighting conditions, different crew members managing different sequences. The color grammar does not drift between Part 1 and Part 2. The camera movement grammar does not randomly depart from its established logic. The compositional approach to power dynamics maintains its consistency across thousands of individual shots produced across years. This level of consistency is only possible if the visual grammar was established analytically before either film was shot and maintained through a production culture that understood the grammar's purpose well enough to apply it consistently without requiring moment-by-moment directorial intervention. The consistency is the proof of intention. Incidental visual choices do not maintain this level of consistency across two films and four years of production. Only deliberate analytical choices do. The franchise's visual language is deliberate, which is what makes it worth analyzing, and what makes analysis of it productive rather than speculative.

The franchise's visual language is the most durable of all its achievements because it is the dimension most resistant to simple imitation and most rewarding of genuine engagement. The dialogue can be quoted, the plot can be summarized, the performances can be praised, the records can be cited. The visual language can only be experienced, across multiple viewings, with the quality of attention it was made for.
