Bollywood dialogue traditionally announces itself. It arrives at the emotional destination in advance and then describes the journey: the character says what they feel, what they want, what they are about to do, what the scene means. The confession is made explicit, the threat is stated directly, the revelation is named at the moment it occurs. This tradition of emotional announcement reflects commercial cinema's preference for clarity: mass audiences in diverse linguistic contexts need lines that communicate intent without requiring inference. The result is a dialogue culture where the gap between what people say and what they mean is typically small, and where silence is used primarily as a pause before further announcement rather than as a communicative act in its own right.

![Dhurandhar Dialogue Writing Analyzed - Insight Crunch](/assets/images/blog/blog-38.webp)

Aditya Dhar's writing in the Dhurandhar pair refuses this convention. The most devastating lines in these films are the ones in which a character says less than they mean, and the viewer must close the gap. When Hamza speaks with his Lyari neighbours, the surface chatter hides strategic calculation; when Sushant Bansal briefs the Delhi room on an operation, the clipped briefing hides moral exhaustion. Restraint carries the weight that announcement carries in conventional Bollywood text. The lines that earn theatre-wide cheers and the lines that demand a second viewing are often the same lines, operating at two registers simultaneously.

This article argues that Dhar's lines represent a structural departure from Bollywood convention instead of a tonal adjustment. He does not simply write quieter lines; he builds the entire script around the premise that what is withheld is heavier than what is said. The analysis that follows examines four craft dimensions through which this premise operates, traces where Dhar's approach reaches its limits, and connects the screenplay choices to the duology's larger argument about identity, loyalty, and the cost of a life spent lying to everyone who loves you. The [craft of the screenplay connects directly to the filmmaking vision Dhar brings across his career](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/04/24/aditya-dhar-filmmaking-style-analysis/), and his dialogue choices here reflect a decade of thinking about how restraint reads on screen.

## The Art of Restraint: What Is Not Said

Restraint in the Dhurandhar pair operates through several mechanisms, each deployed for a distinct dramatic purpose. The first is substitution: when a scene requires an emotional exchange, Dhar often writes the exchange as an operational briefing. Sushant Bansal and Hamza meet in the Delhi safe-house sequences not to discuss the toll the cover is taking, not to acknowledge that Hamza has been under for nearly a decade, not to name the personal collapse that both men can see in the other. Instead, they discuss logistics: operational detail, communication protocols, the information the institutional record requires Bansal to document. The logistics conversation is the substitution for the authentic conversation, and the manner in which the logistics are discussed, the pauses before certain operational steps, the way Bansal's voice drops when Hamza reports on the health of a particular Karachi asset, carries the real exchange underneath the procedural one.

A careful viewer watching a Bansal-Hamza sequence for the first time registers a clipped professional meeting. A second viewing reveals an act of care performed through form: Bansal is checking on Hamza, and Hamza is reassuring Bansal, and neither man can say this aloud because the institutional frame will not permit it and because saying it would make the cover, the project, the entire operation feel provisional not necessary. Dhar trusts the audience member to make this inference. He does not cut to a flashback in which Bansal tells his wife he worries about Hamza. He does not insert a voice-over in which Hamza reflects on missing his family. The substitution carries the emotional load, and the subtext is the text, once the viewer has learned to read it.

The second mechanism is deflection. Characters redirect conversations that threaten to expose them by answering questions with adjacent questions, picking up on a minor detail in the interlocutor's sentence and running with it, changing registers from intimate to professional mid-exchange. Hamza is the duology's master deflector, as he must be; his entire cover depends on steering every dangerous conversation away from the seam that would reveal it. When Rehman Dakait's lieutenants probe Hamza's biography in the first act's dinner sequence, Hamza does not evade directly. He laughs at a joke someone made earlier, pours another glass, picks up a different topic that he knows Rehman's brother finds interesting, and the probe dissolves into conviviality. The scene feels spontaneous; it is architectural.

The third mechanism is silence itself, used not as a pause between utterances but as a communicative act that carries meaning. This mechanism is rarer in both films than substitution or deflection, but it appears in scenes where Hamza's cover is under the kind of pressure that produces verbal slippage. Dhar writes these moments as long, uncomfortable silences in which the camera holds on a face, a hand, a cup, and the audience learns to read the silence as the character's refusal to say what the scene demands. The mehendi sequence, where Hamza's adopted Lyari family is celebrating and Hamza is also mourning a death he cannot acknowledge, is built almost entirely on withheld speech. Ranveer Singh's face performs what the screenplay refuses to write.

The restraint philosophy stands in sharpest contrast with the duology's own text when the Dhurandhar films deliberately choose announcement over suppression for dramatic effect. Major Iqbal's interrogation sequences in Part 2 are loud, declarative, almost operatic in their menace. Dhar writes these sequences to explode against the restrained backdrop of everything that surrounds them. The contrast is the point: the rare moments when a character in the project announces emotion carry weight precisely because announcement has been withheld for hours of screen time leading up to them. The pair of films treats dramatic announcement as a limited resource to be spent at pressure points rather than as the baseline mode of communication.

What restraint enables is the accumulation of subtext. A Bollywood film built on announcement tends to reset its emotional register scene by scene: each scene makes explicit what it needs to make explicit, and the next scene starts fresh. A film built on restraint builds a charge across scenes, because nothing is resolved verbally. By the time Hamza confronts the knowledge that his adopted Lyari family will be destroyed by the operation he has been building for a decade, the watcher has absorbed dozens of scenes in which Hamza has not said what he is feeling, and the accumulated weight of those withholdings collapses into the confrontation scene with devastating force. This is the craft payoff of restraint: the ending lands because the middle did not spend its emotional capital.

Restraint also allows Dhar to make his characters readable through behaviour and not through commentary. A Bollywood screenplay that announces emotion tends to tell the viewer what a character feels; a work that withholds emotion forces the attentive reader to infer it from behaviour, and the inference is more vivid than the telling. When Hamza returns to the Lyari house after a covert meeting, hangs his jacket a certain way, pours water for himself before anyone else, and sits at the corner of the mat, the viewer reads his state from these choices. No line announces his exhaustion; the blocking announces it, and the blocking is more trustworthy than a line would be because behaviour does not lie the way speech can.

The risk of restraint is under-reading. Someone watching who has not learned to read the pair's subtext idiom may find the Bansal-Hamza exchanges dry or the deflection sequences inert. Dhar accepts this risk. The two films is not written for the viewer who wants to be told what to feel; it is written for the viewer willing to do the inferential work that restraint demands. ## Mass-Appeal and Literary: Calibrating for Two Audiences

The standard criticism of literary screenwriting in Bollywood is that it narrows the audience. A film that rewards inference, layers subtext, and refuses to announce emotion is assumed to be a film that urban multiplex viewers will enjoy and mass single-screen viewers will find alienating. Dhar's screenplay rejects this binary. The two-film project generates single-screen cheers and multiplex readings from the same scenes, and it does so through a calibration that combines surface intensity with layered subtext. Each major line operates at two registers: a surface register that announces enough to land emotionally for a first-time viewer, and a subtextual mode that rewards the viewer willing to parse what the surface is really doing.

Consider the confrontation between Ajay Sanyal and a visiting delegation in the Part 1 sequence at the Karachi safe-location. On the surface, Sanyal delivers a clipped declaration about operational priorities, and the scene ends with a line that carries dramatic weight any first-time audience member can register. Underneath, the line is doing three things at once: it is reassuring the delegation that the operation is on track, it is warning a compromised member of the delegation that Sanyal knows about their compromise without stating this knowledge directly, and it is setting up a reversal that will occur two acts later. The surface viewer gets the dramatic closing; the attentive audience member gets the warning; the second-viewing viewer gets the foreshadowing.

Dhar writes this calibration by constructing lines that are grammatically simple but semantically layered. The surface sentence is short and direct. The layered meaning emerges from which word the character stresses, which phrase they omit, which reference they make obliquely. Ranveer Singh's Hamza performs this calibration most visibly in the early Lyari sequences. His lines to Rehman's lieutenants are literally compliments and pleasantries, and they play as compliments at the mass register; at the attentive register, they are threat-assessment conversations in which Hamza is cataloguing which men know what about him and which men suspect what about his past. The same sentence is a toast and a memo depending on how closely the watcher is reading it.

The calibration mechanism is also temporal. Lines that land immediately often gain new meaning on second viewing. The mass-appeal reading of Rehman's opening sequence, in which the Karachi underworld boss introduces his operation to a new lieutenant (who is actually Hamza), plays as a menacing character-introduction scene. On second viewing, after the viewer knows Rehman's fate, the scene reads as a tragic introduction: the man is talking about his future, which the attentive reader now knows he will not have, to an operative who will be partially responsible for ending it. The same lines carry two wholly different emotional charges across the two viewings, which is the mark of prose that has invested in durability rather than just in immediate effect.

The bilingual layering compounds the calibration. A line delivered in Urdu that plays as colloquial warmth to a Hindi speaker may carry a cultural reference that an Urdu speaker registers as a veiled allusion to a historical moment, an idiom from a poet, a phrase from a religious text. Dhar does not explain these layers; he writes them and trusts the viewers who catch them to appreciate the depth while the viewers who do not catch them are not alienated, because the surface still communicates. This is the opposite of literary obscurity, which requires inference just to reach the surface meaning. Dhar writes surface-accessible lines whose depth is a bonus for the attentive viewer.

The viewership-calibration approach also manages a problem unique to dialogue-driven cinema in multilingual contexts: the risk that culturally layered writing will exclude viewers outside a particular linguistic or regional community. Dhar solves this by making the subtext rewardable but not required. A Tamil-speaking viewer watching the two installments in dubbed form gets a full cinematic experience from the surface dramatic register; a Delhi Hindi-speaking someone watching gets the subtextual register as well; an Urdu-literate viewer from Pakistan or from an Urdu-educated Indian family gets a further textual layer involving references to shared Indo-Islamic cultural material. The film does not punish the person watching for missing these layers, because the surface is self-sufficient. The film rewards the viewer who catches them.

This calibration is, by any reasonable accounting, the most technically demanding dimension of Dhar's text. Dialogue that works only for one audience is easier to write than writing that works for multiple audiences simultaneously. The standard compromise position, writing lines for the mass audience and accepting that attentive viewers will find the film shallow, is the path most Bollywood commercial films take. The less common alternative, script for the attentive audience and accepting that mass viewers will find the film inaccessible, is the path art-house Bollywood often takes. Dhar's screenplay refuses both compromises. It insists that the mass and attentive registers are not in conflict, and that the right craft can serve both in the same line.

The commercial validation for this approach is visible in the duology's repeat-viewership pattern, which diverges from the norm for commercial action cinema. Where most action films front-load their theatrical earnings and collapse after the first weekend, the Dhurandhar films held their numbers across multiple weeks with significant repeat viewership as their driver. Viewers who caught the surface reading returned for the subtextual one, which is exactly the behaviour that the calibration approach predicts. ## The Bilingual Texture: Language as Identity

The bilingual texture of this two-film arc's spoken work is one of the most closely observed cultural achievements in recent Bollywood. Hindi and Urdu occupy different registers in the screenplay, Balochi and a Sindhi-inflected speech characterise certain neighbourhood community members, and English appears in institutional contexts, particularly in the Delhi RAW sequences. Dhar uses these linguistic layers not as texture or ornament but as a functional system: language choice communicates identity, code-switching communicates performance, and the seams between languages are where cover cracks appear.

In the cultural context of the duology's Lyari setting, Urdu carries Pakistani Muslim cultural identity, while Hindi carries Indian cultural identity. Hamza's linguistic task is to speak in the cover language so fluently and naturally that his Lyari neighbours do not detect the Hindi-speaking Jaskirat underneath. Ranveer Singh's code-switching work, which Dhar wrote as a collaboration with the performance instead of as text for the performance to execute, is one of the project's most technically impressive achievements. Hamza code-switches with a fluency that has been built across the cover's decade: he speaks in Urdu when the cover demands Urdu, and he speaks in Hindi only in the Delhi sequences when Bansal and Sanyal are the interlocutors. The code-switching is so clean that the first-time audience member learns to read Hamza's linguistic choice as the flag for which identity he is occupying at any given moment.

Balochi and the Sindhi-inflected language that characterises certain Lyari community members in the duology's screenplay is used to establish geographic and cultural setting rather than to communicate content. Dhar uses these linguistic markers impressionistically rather than for semantic content: the presence of Balochi or Sindhi-inflected speech in a scene communicates that the scene is occurring in a Lyari that is linguistically layered, ethnically mixed, historically distinct from the more homogeneous Pakistani-Karachi of the institutional sequences. A viewer who does not speak Balochi does not miss narrative information; a viewer member who does speak Balochi gains a layer of cultural texture that grounds the scenes in the actual neighbourhood in place of a generic criminal Karachi.

English appears in the institutional sequences, particularly in the Delhi RAW contexts, and its appearance is culturally consistent. The Delhi institutional voice of Indian intelligence and military communication is bilingual in practice, with English carrying the weight of bureaucratic precision and Hindi carrying the weight of interpersonal register. Dhar writes his Delhi scenes in this bilingual institutional range without explaining the convention to the viewer, which is correct: the convention is the actual practice, and the project's authority rests on its willingness to reproduce practice and not stylize it. When Bansal speaks to a senior officer in English, the scene reads as institutional; when Bansal speaks to his subordinate in Hindi, the scene reads as interpersonal. The language switch carries the register switch.

The most complex linguistic work in the pair of films occurs in the sequences where Hamza's cover speech slips toward a Punjabi substrate or toward a Hindi-inflected construction. These slippages are rare, and Dhar writes them as the visible seams of Hamza's performance. When Hamza is under pressure in Rehman's compound in the second act of Part 1, his Urdu becomes slightly more formal, slightly less Lyari-accented, and a Pakistani-born viewer in the audience can register the shift as the flag of cover strain. Dhar does not have Rehman's lieutenants notice the shift in-scene; the watcher registers what the characters miss, and the dramatic irony of the withholding generates tension. The same craft-mechanism that works in the English-language tradition with an undercover agent's regional accent slipping under pressure is, in Dhar's text, made to work across a Hindi-Urdu-Punjabi linguistic landscape.

The linguistic texture extends to the script's handling of religious register. the cover language in the Lyari sequences carries Islamic cultural references that are integrated into everyday greeting and speech, not foregrounded as religious markers. Hamza's ability to deploy these references fluently is the cover's deepest layer: he is not just speaking Urdu, he is participating in an Islamic Pakistani cultural fabric that would take a decade of lived practice to inhabit convincingly. Dhar writes this fabric without exoticising it. The Lyari sequences feel like Lyari, not like an Indian-filmmaker's externally observed notion of Lyari, and this authenticity is one of the reasons both installments reads as serious lines rather than performative research.

The linguistic layering is also used as a tool for economy. A scene that would require thirty seconds of exposition in a monolingual register can be communicated in five seconds through a language choice. When Hamza switches to English in a Karachi sequence to handle a particular business interaction, the switch tells the viewer that the interaction is with a multinational institutional counterparty, that the relationship is transactional instead of communal, that Hamza is performing a particular cosmopolitan register that is one of his many linguistic registers. No exposition required, and the scene moves. The language system is doing narrative work that a monolingual screenplay would have to do with words.

What the linguistic layering rewards is the viewer who is curious about the Lyari setting, the Pakistani-Indian cultural fabric, and the actual practice of cross-border espionage as a linguistic challenge. An attentive reader without this curiosity still gets the scenes, because the visual and performance information is sufficient. A curious viewer gets something closer to a documentary texture, in which the languages are not props but ecosystems the characters inhabit. This is the kind of writing that typically appears in translated world cinema and rarely in commercial Indian-language film. Its appearance in Dhurandhar is part of what makes both installments feel like a rebalancing of what Bollywood commercial his pages can attempt.

## Characterization Through Speech: How Characters Are Who They Sound Like

The third dimension along which Dhar's screenplay distinguishes itself is the use of speech as primary characterisation. In conventional Bollywood writing, a character's identity is often communicated through costume, blocking, music cues, and expository lines that describe the character's personality to the viewer. Dhar's prose works harder than that. His characters are who they sound like: their cadence, their vocabulary, their rhythm of speech, their willingness or unwillingness to be interrupted, the kinds of topics they raise and the kinds they avoid, all carry the weight of what a conventional script would communicate through other means.

Rehman Dakait's speech is the clearest example. In Sanjay Dutt's rendering, Rehman speaks in long, measured sentences that carry the cadence of a man who has spent years controlling rooms through the pace of his voice. He does not interrupt. He does not accelerate. When he issues a threat, the sentence builds toward the threat through subordinate clauses, and the clauses themselves are content-rich not decorative. A lesser screenplay would write Rehman's lines as declarative menace, short sentences in which the threat is the point. Dhar writes Rehman as a man whose menace lives in the fact that he is taking his time, that he is confident enough of the room to speak at his own pace, that he is in no hurry to arrive at his point because no one is going to interrupt him before he arrives there.

Analysing the syntax of Rehman's most threatening exchanges, the text reveals one of the most precise character portraits in recent Bollywood. Rehman avoids the conventional criminal-threatening-speech patterns, which typically consist of explicit threat statements and power assertions, in favour of a more complex idiom: he describes situations, he narrates possible futures, he asks questions whose answers are obvious, and the threat emerges from the accumulation of these non-threatening surfaces. The viewer feels the threat through the pattern rather than through any single line, which is how threat operates in actual social practice in place of in action-genre convention.

Rehman's speech also reflects his community position in ways relevant to the Lyari setting. His authority carries the weight of someone who is not just personally powerful but institutionally embedded: his declarative mode reflects years of social practice in which his interlocutors do not correct him, disagree with him, or interrupt him. Dhar writes this ambient authority into the sentences themselves, and not telling the person watching about it through other characters' commentary. The writing trusts the viewer to register the pattern, and the performance, which is Sanjay Dutt's most controlled work in years, delivers it with precision.

Hamza's speech is the inverse. His cover demands that he be ordinary, unnoticed, someone the room does not turn to look at. Ranveer Singh performs Hamza at a register that actively subtracts attention from himself: his sentences are short, his cadence is neighbourhood-casual, his vocabulary is the vocabulary of a person who has chosen to fit in rather than to stand out. The dialogue supports this performance by giving Hamza few long monologues and many short observations, by allowing other characters to dominate scenes he is in, by keeping him verbally small in rooms where a conventional protagonist would be verbally large. A first-time audience member watches Hamza recede, and the recession is the characterisation.

When the Jaskirat identity surfaces through slippage, it surfaces through micro-deviations from Hamza's established speech patterns. A slightly more Hindi-flavoured construction, a Punjabi rhythm peeking through a cover-language sentence, a delay in responding to a family member's question that would be instantaneous if he were truly their son. These micro-deviations are the film's richest craft moments for an attentive viewer, and they work only because Dhar has established the baseline Hamza register with enough fidelity that the small deviations register. A text that wrote Hamza with less precision would lose these moments; Dhar's precision enables them.

Ajay Sanyal's speech, as rendered by Sanjay Dutt's counterpart performer in the institutional scenes, is a different register again. Sanyal is a man whose authority lives in brevity. His sentences are clipped, his instructions are operational, his emotional register is deliberately flat. The clipped mode tells the audience member that Sanyal has seen too much to still invest energy in rhetorical flourish, that the agency has trained him into a communication style that produces minimum risk of misinterpretation, that his decades in the institutional world have reduced his interest in performance to zero. Dhar's lines makes Sanyal's brevity carry the weight that Rehman's measured length carries: two authorities, two registers, each built from the opposite material.

Sushant Bansal's dialogue reflects the institutional position he occupies: maximum knowledge of the operational reality, minimum authority to act on that knowledge, and the resulting pressure that builds in the gap between those two poles. His lines are the duology's most restrained because his institutional position constrains even the expression of constraint: he cannot say what he is thinking about Hamza's condition to Hamza, he cannot say what he is thinking about the operation to his superiors, he cannot say what he is thinking about his own career to his wife. Dhar writes Bansal as a character whose interior life is almost entirely sealed off from verbal expression, and the performance registers this containment through the way the actor holds his mouth during pauses, the way he exhales before answering routine questions, the way his vocal pitch drops when Hamza enters the room.

Major Iqbal, the Pakistani counterpart and Part 2's principal antagonist, is written with a calculated loudness that contrasts with the ambient restraint of the rest of the paired project. Iqbal is a Pakistani institutional figure whose position gives him permission to be verbally assertive, and Dhar writes him into that permission. His interrogation sequences are among the most verbally explicit in the Dhurandhar films, and their explicitness reads as characterisation instead of as writing inconsistency: Iqbal is a man who has been promoted into a role where his voice is supposed to fill the room, and the role has shaped his speech. The contrast with Hamza's self-erasing speech is the point; the two men inhabit opposite strategies for occupying space with language.

What the character-through-speech approach enables is a duology in which the viewer can identify which character is speaking by the manner of the speech, not only by the content. In practice, this is a test of script craft that most commercial screenplays fail: in most films, if you covered the faces and stripped the music, you could not tell which character was delivering a given line because the speech patterns have not been differentiated. In the Dhurandhar pair, the differentiation is deep enough that the test works. Rehman sounds like Rehman; Hamza sounds like Hamza; Bansal sounds like Bansal; Iqbal sounds like Iqbal. The verbal work is doing the job that a conventional screenplay outsources to other departments.

## Where the Dialogue Falls Short

A serious account of the pair's dialogue craft has to name the places where it does not work as well as it does elsewhere. The restraint approach carries costs, and Dhar's screenplay is not exempt from them. Three dimensions in which the work is weaker than its best work deserve honest acknowledgement.

The first is the writing of certain institutional briefing sequences. The two-film project contains a number of scenes in which senior RAW or ISI figures review the state of an operation, update their superiors, or debate policy. These scenes function as exposition, and Dhar's text is noticeably tighter when it is building character than when it is delivering operational information. The briefing scenes occasionally feel over-written, with officials reciting information the viewer needs but the characters would not naturally say to each other in that form. The best of these scenes dramatize the exposition by giving the officials competing agendas that the briefing surfaces, but not every briefing achieves this, and some read as expository blocks that the lines has not fully integrated into the drama.

The two installments' writing in sequences involving characters who appear primarily to deliver institutional information, certain minor RAW figures in the Delhi sequences, occasionally lacks the textured variation that the script gives to the major characters. These figures speak in a standardised institutional register that does not differentiate one from another in the way that Rehman's voice differentiates him from Bansal. The screenplay's craft investment is concentrated on the major characters, and the minor institutional figures suffer for the concentration. This is a triage choice a writer has to make under the time pressures of commercial production, and Dhar made the right triage, but the result is a slightly less uniform texture across the project's character bench.

The second dimension where the writing is uneven is in the occasional over-reliance on exchanges that rest on cultural knowledge the general watcher does not have. This two-film arc's Pakistani-Indian linguistic layering is one of its strengths, as argued above, but there are moments when the rewards for catching a reference are disproportionate to the accessibility cost of not catching it. A reference to a Lyari neighbourhood figure, a half-quoted line from an Urdu poet, an allusion to a mid-1990s Karachi political moment, each requires cultural familiarity that many viewers will not possess. When both parts does this well, the reference is a bonus layer; when it does it less well, the unfamiliar viewer feels excluded from a meaning they can sense but cannot access. Dhar's draft is almost always in the first camp, but not entirely.

The third dimension is the occasional dilution of Hamza's verbal discipline in the more action-heavy sequences of Part 2. Part 1 is strictly disciplined about keeping Hamza verbally small; Part 2 occasionally loosens this discipline, particularly in the climactic confrontation sequences, where Hamza delivers lines that feel closer to conventional action-cinema protagonist speech than to the recessive register the paired project has established for him. This is a genre pressure Dhar navigates imperfectly: Part 2 is structured as a revenge saga, the genre expects the protagonist to claim verbal space at the climax, and Dhar's prose partially concedes to this expectation. The concession is understandable, but it means the character Hamza becomes at the climax is a slight reversion to Bollywood protagonist conventions the pair of films has otherwise refused. A more disciplined Part 2 might have held the restraint all the way through; the Part 2 we have loosens it at the edges.

These limitations are visible as craft limitations when compared with the duology's best work, and the comparison reveals that the most achieved writing is concentrated in the Part 1 Lyari sequences and the Part 1 Delhi safe-house exchanges. The climactic action sequences and the briefing scenes are good; the Lyari material is exceptional. This is honest critical accounting not dismissal. The two-film project's dialogue is better than almost any recent Bollywood the text and less uniformly excellent than its best passages suggest.

A further limitation worth naming is the duology's handling of certain women's roles, particularly in Part 1. The primary female characters in the Lyari cover, including Hamza's adopted family members, are written with care, but the scripted talk sometimes leaves them as registers of Hamza's moral situation rather than as full participants with their own verbal signatures. The duology is aware of this risk and addresses it most effectively in the scenes where Hamza's adopted mother figure delivers long speeches in the second act; those scenes carry the richest writing of any of the Lyari family material. But the texture is uneven across the family bench, and a Dhurandhar film that gave all its women the lines investment that Rehman and Bansal received would be, by any reasonable measure, even stronger than the one we have.

## The Bigger Argument: What the Dialogue Reveals About the Franchise

The text of the Dhurandhar duology is not an accidental feature of a well-made film. It is the expressive channel through which the pair makes its deepest argument, which is that espionage is, at its most fundamental level, a dialogue problem. Hamza must maintain a version of himself in speech that is consistent across a decade of conversations with people who, if they detected the inconsistency, would kill him and destroy the operation. His survival depends not on combat skills, not on tradecraft in the narrow sense, but on his ability to speak as someone else for ten years without a single slip that a careful listener could catch.

The two films argues that this task is not just difficult but transformative. Speaking as someone else, for long enough, changes who the speaker is. The decade of Hamza's cover speech-inflected Lyari speech has not just maintained the cover; it has shaped Jaskirat into someone who thinks in Urdu, dreams in Urdu, grieves in Urdu. When the cover ends, Jaskirat cannot simply return to the Hindi-Punjabi identity he had before. The language he has used has become part of who he is, and the person who emerges from the cover is neither Jaskirat nor Hamza but a third person whose identity is constituted by the linguistic and performative work of the decade.

This argument about language and identity is one of the most philosophically ambitious claims any recent Hindi-language film has made, and the writing is the vehicle through which it is made. The pair of films does not state the claim through a monologue or a voice-over; it demonstrates it through a decade of Hamza's speech, through the way his Hindi feels stiff when he finally returns to Delhi in the late Part 2 sequences, through the Punjabi substrate that now surfaces in Hamza's cover speech in place of the other way around. The reversal tells the attentive reader that the adopted language has become the deeper one. The identity has shifted, and the shift is audible.

The two installments' evolution across both films is the textual evidence for this claim, available to any viewer who tracks the way Hamza's speech changes from his initial arrival in Lyari to his final scenes in Part 2. The early Hamza is a performance of a Lyari Urdu-speaker; the late Hamza is an actual Lyari Urdu-speaker who remembers being a Pathankot Hindi-Punjabi-speaker. The difference is audible even if the mechanism is not consciously identified by the listener, and it is one of the primary reasons that the duology's scenes feel emotionally earned at the ending. The ending lands because the middle has built the identity shift that the ending confronts.

What this means for Bollywood dialogue, as a tradition, is worth stating plainly. The dominant Indian cinema mode treats dialogue as a vehicle for emotional announcement, cultural celebration, and audience satisfaction. Dhar's screenplay treats dialogue as a vehicle for character construction, identity formation, and thematic argument. These are different writing philosophies, and this two-film arc's commercial success, its sustained repeat viewership, and its cultural impact all argue that the second philosophy has a larger audience than the commercial-film industry has historically believed. The viewer who will sit through two long films built on text and not on spectacle is a larger viewer than the industry has invested in finding.

The industry implications are not trivial. If the Dhurandhar both parts's lines approach is replicable, then the window for Hindi-language commercial films to be built on inference, subtext, and layered language is wider than recent industry practice has assumed. This is good news for writers who want to work at the depth that Dhar has worked at, and it is good news for viewers who want their Bollywood to reward the attention they bring to it. ## Pacing, Pause, and the Aural Architecture of the Lines

A dialogue-centric film is ultimately an aural experience, and Dhar's work deserves analysis not just as text on the page but as sound arranged in time. The craft of where a pause falls, how long a character holds a breath before replying, what tempo the exchange settles into across an extended sequence, is as much part of the writing as the words chosen. Dhar's screenplay contains explicit pace marks, and the edit preserves them with a rigour that Hindi cinema does not always respect. The result is an aural architecture in which silence is measured, cadence is deliberate, and the rhythm of each scene carries as much meaning as the words spoken.

Consider the opening Lyari sequence, where Hamza greets neighbours on a morning walk before his operational day begins. On the page, the sequence is short: a handful of polite greetings, a brief exchange about a shared acquaintance, a comment on the weather. On screen, the sequence runs nearly two minutes because every exchange breathes. A neighbour asks how Hamza's family is; Hamza pauses half a beat longer than a confident answer would require; then he responds in a style of warm familiarity that the pause has set up. That half-beat is the text working at its most disciplined. It tells the audience that Hamza is thinking about the answer, that the answer is not automatic, that the cover requires verbal care at every moment. Without the pause, the line would play as confident chatter; with the pause, it plays as the visible effort of a man maintaining a decade-long performance.

The pause as structural element shows up across all four major characters. Rehman Dakait's pauses are different in kind from Hamza's. Rehman pauses because his position gives him the room's time, and the pause asserts control over the exchange. When Rehman lets a question hang in the air before answering, the people around him wait, which is the mechanism by which Sanjay Dutt performs the character's ambient authority. Dhar's screenplay marks these as written beats rather than as actor inventions, and the two departments collaborate to produce the cadence the screen delivers. A screenwriter less interested in sound would write these scenes as quicker exchanges; the slower tempo is what Dhar built in.

Bansal's pauses are the pauses of containment. When Hamza enters the Delhi safe room and Bansal has to perform the operational greeting, Bansal pauses not because his position permits it but because his internal state requires it. The work indicates these pauses through stage directions or through the structural placement of Bansal's lines, giving the actor the architectural permission to hold the moment. What results is a character whose verbal tempo tells the viewership about his emotional weight before any information about his emotional state is exchanged through content. The pause is the character.

Major Iqbal's pauses are weaponised. Iqbal holds silence during interrogations not because he is thinking but because he knows the silence pressures the person across the table. This is a different use of pause from Rehman's authority or Bansal's containment: Iqbal's pause is active dominance, and the screenplay marks these sequences with different tempo instructions than the Lyari scenes. The contrast across the four characters illustrates the granularity of Dhar's aural planning. Each major character has an associated tempo, and the tempos operate as sonic signatures across the entire runtime.

The music department, collaborating with the draft in place of competing with it, respects the pauses by holding silence where the verbal work holds silence. Shashwat Sachdev's score in the Lyari sequences often drops out during Hamza's verbal exchanges, leaving the room tone carrying the scene. The score returns when the scene breaks toward action or toward internal reflection. This is a collaboration between departments that mass Hindi cinema often does not achieve: the score underlines the lines rather than covering them, and the viewer hears the pause because the score has been composed to include the silence.

Cadence also operates at the sentence level. Dhar writes sentences with built-in pause points: mid-sentence commas that the actor honours with a breath, multi-clause constructions whose final clause lands after the earlier clauses have set up the tempo. This is a feature of the Indo-Islamic writing traditions that Urdu poetry draws from, and Dhar imports the technique into film dialogue without stylising it. The average scene in the project runs slower than the average scene in recent commercial Indic cinema, and the slower pace is the screenplay's invitation to listen at a deeper register than action film tempos typically accommodate.

Pause placement also interacts with the bilingual layering. When Hamza switches languages mid-exchange, the switch is almost always preceded by a pause that tells the listener the switch is deliberate. When Rehman addresses a business counterparty in English, the shift from Urdu carries a half-beat that signals tone change. These micro-pauses do the work that a monolingual the pages would do with an explicit cue, and they are invisible to the inattentive listener but functional to the attentive one. The craft is in the architecture rather than in any single moment, which is consistent with the restraint philosophy of the screenplay overall.

The aural architecture extends to the way Dhar handles overlapping speech. In most commercial Indian films, overlapping speech indicates informal domestic warmth or chaotic action; in Dhurandhar, overlap is reserved for family scenes and ordinary Lyari community life, and it is strictly absent from the institutional and criminal sequences. The absence of overlap in the Karachi underworld scenes tells the audience that these are rooms in which people do not talk over each other, rooms in which turn-taking is formalised, rooms in which the right to speak is conferred instead of claimed. The texture of how people occupy aural space is doing narrative work, and the script has planned the distinctions at the architectural level not leaving them to production decisions downstream.

## Dhar's Evolution as a Dialogue Writer: From Uri to Dhurandhar

A writer's career is visible in the evolution of the craft across their work, and Aditya Dhar's trajectory from Uri: The Surgical Strike to Dhurandhar shows a significant deepening in screenplay technique. Uri is a confident first feature with strong scripted talk, but its dialogue operates primarily at the announcement register: characters state their intentions, their fears, their operational plans, and their personal commitments in lines that a conventional Bollywood audience can follow without inference. The military idiom of Uri is clipped and professional, but the film does not ask its audience to read subtext the way Dhurandhar does.

What Dhar appears to have learned in the seven years between Uri and Dhurandhar, if one reads the films as a craft sequence, is that the audience can sustain more weight than commercial convention assumes. Uri trusts its those watching to follow an operation and an emotional arc; Dhurandhar trusts its audience to close the gap between what characters say and what they mean, to follow bilingual code-switching without explanation, to read a pause as communicative rather than as empty. The trust is the authorial advance. A writer who distrusts the audience will announce everything; a writer who trusts the audience will withhold. Dhar's evolution is an evolution in trust.

The evolution is also technical. Uri's dialogue is written almost entirely in Bollywood, with occasional English for operational clarity; the bilingual layering that Dhurandhar performs is absent. Dhar's ability to write Urdu, Balochi-inflected speech, and Sindhi-inflected community language with authority was not visible in Uri and is central to Dhurandhar. This is a significant expansion of craft range, and it reflects the research investment the Dhurandhar project demanded: Dhar could not have written the Lyari sequences without immersion in the linguistic and cultural fabric of the setting, and the immersion is visible in the text's density.

A second technical advance is in character-voice differentiation. Uri's characters speak a lightly differentiated common idiom. Dhurandhar's characters speak from distinct verbal signatures that an attentive listener can distinguish without visual cues. This is the single most difficult craft skill in dialogue writing, and Dhar's Dhurandhar text does it across a large cast. The skill is acquired through years of reading, listening, and writing, and the gap between Uri and Dhurandhar in this dimension is one of the clearest indicators of Dhar's craft maturation between the two projects.

A third evolution involves the handling of what might be called institutional speech: the way senior figures in intelligence, military, and government settings talk to each other. Uri's institutional sequences sometimes fall into the familiar trap of having officials deliver exposition that the audience needs but that the characters would not plausibly say to each other in that form. Dhurandhar's institutional sequences are cleaner, with information emerging from the interactions in place of being recited at them. The writer has learned to trust the spectators to pick up operational context from fragmentary exchanges rather than from complete briefings, and the trust produces scenes that feel overheard rather than explained.

The evolution is not uniform. Dhurandhar retains some of Uri's habits in its action sequences, where the restrained approach loosens and the characters occasionally announce their feelings in ways the rest of the screenplay has refused. This is partly genre pressure, as action cinema conventionally calls for protagonist self-assertion at the climax, and partly a conservative choice that Dhar makes to ensure the mass-market mode remains accessible in the set-pieces. A writer with more confidence in the restraint approach might have held it all the way through; Dhar has not quite taken that step. What Dhurandhar does that Uri did not is hold the restrained approach for the overwhelming majority of the runtime, which is itself a major advance.

For readers interested in how this craft trajectory maps onto other aspects of Dhar's filmmaking, the cinematography, score, and editing choices across both Uri and Dhurandhar have evolved in parallel, and the [analysis of Dhar's broader filmmaking approach](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/04/24/aditya-dhar-filmmaking-style-analysis/) traces these parallel evolutions in more detail. What the speech specifically demonstrates is that Dhar has become, in the span of two films and seven years, one of the most technically capable screenwriters in Hindi cinema, and the Dhurandhar text will likely be studied by other writers as a reference for what commercial Indian cinema dialogue can aim at when it refuses the announcement convention.

## How the Lines Land in Translation: Dubbing and Subtitles

A work this linguistically specific faces a translation problem that most commercial Indian films do not have to solve, because most commercial Bollywood films operate in a single linguistic tone that transfers cleanly to dubbed Tamil, Telugu, Malayalam, or Kannada versions. Dhurandhar's bilingual Urdu-Hindi register cannot transfer cleanly, because the distinction between The poetic register and Hindi is a culturally specific one that does not translate into a language that does not have the same distinction. The dubbing teams for the Southern Indian releases had to make decisions about how to represent the Urdu-Hindi code-switching in a language whose speakers do not experience that axis as a lived cultural reality.

The most common solution in the dubbed versions is to maintain the Urdu-inflected vocabulary choices in key moments and to let the Southern-language translator's craft carry the key shift through tone and formality instead of through vocabulary. This works partially: an attentive Tamil-speaking audience member who is also literate in Hindi cinema's use of Urdu register picks up the shift, and a Tamil-speaking audience member without that literacy experiences the film as a more monolingual drama than the Hindi-Urdu bilingual audience does. This is a loss, but it is the least bad loss available, and the Southern dubs preserve the film's core dramatic architecture even if they cannot preserve its full linguistic texture.

Subtitled international releases face a different set of decisions. The English subtitles for the international release had to decide whether to flag the Urdu-Hindi distinction at all, and the decision seems to have been to flag it occasionally, when a character uses a word whose resonance in the original is culturally marked, and to translate transparently otherwise. This is the correct decision for the Anglophone audience, which generally lacks the cultural context to experience the Urdu-Hindi axis as meaningful. An Anglophone viewership member reads the subtitles as straightforwardly dramatic text, and the sustained deep performance carries the scenes even without access to the linguistic subtext.

What survives translation in every version is the aural architecture, the pause placement, the tempo differentiation across characters, and the performance voice. These are language-independent elements of the screenplay's craft, and they carry the film's drama for audiences without access to the Hindi-Urdu subtext. What is lost in translation is the subtextual layer of identity performance that the Hindi-Urdu code-switching generates for bilingual audiences. This is an expected consequence of culturally specific writing, and it is a feature not a bug of the draft's ambition. A less linguistically committed screenplay would translate more completely but would have less to translate in the first place.

For the bilingual Indian audience, which is the primary target of the pages, the transfer between Bollywood and Urdu is not a translation question but a lived linguistic reality. The screenplay meets this audience where it is, and the audience responds with the sustained repeat viewership that drove the film's commercial pattern. This mutual recognition, between a script written with full faith in its audience's bilingual literacy and a viewer that rewarded the faith with engagement, is one of the most significant industry signals Dhurandhar has sent. It suggests that commercial Indian cinema has been underserving a bilingually literate audience that has been waiting for screenplays to match its competence, and that the market for such screenplays is larger than the industry has historically believed.

## The Reception Arc: How Critics and Viewers Processed the Dialogue

Serious film criticism does not often focus on the artistry of Bollywood commercial dialogue, because Bollywood commercial practice has not historically rewarded such focus. Dhurandhar broke this pattern by generating a volume of craft-attentive criticism that has been rare for popular Indian releases. Reviewers working for trade publications spent paragraphs on sequence analysis, pause placement, and verbal differentiation across the ensemble, and these discussions reached general readers who had not previously engaged with screenwriting craft as an analytical object. The film's commercial success and its critical reception compounded each other: mass audiences returned for repeat viewings and brought back attention to the textual layers that critical readings had flagged, while critics found themselves addressing a readership that had already engaged with the material.

Social media threads devoted to individual scenes ran for months after release. Viewers catalogued moments, paraphrased exchanges, debated meanings, and the catalogues reached a granularity that is uncommon for commercial Bollywood films. A sequence involving Rehman Dakait hosting a dinner became the subject of a multi-thread analysis tracking every conversational move, every pause, every code-switch. A sequence involving Hamza returning to his adopted Lyari household after a particularly heavy operational day generated parallel threads analysing the blocking and the verbal texture. These community readings accumulated into a shadow commentary track that ran alongside the film itself, and the commentary track lifted casual viewers into the analytical register over time.

The critical response also registered disagreement, which is its own evidence of engagement. Some reviewers argued the restraint approach became repetitive across five hours; others argued the loosening of restraint in Part 2's climactic passages undermined the discipline of the earlier material; others argued the bilingual layering excluded non-bilingual audiences despite evidence to the contrary. These criticisms are serious and worth engaging. A film that provokes disagreement of this quality has earned the disagreement, and the disagreement itself has elevated the craft conversation around popular cinema in ways that serve writers working on future projects. The critics who pushed back on the restraint approach have given the next generation of Hindi screenwriters a vocabulary for thinking about restraint's costs as well as its benefits.

Scholarly attention to the film has also begun to emerge, with early papers analysing the code-switching patterns as sociolinguistic artefacts and reading the cover narrative as an allegory for diasporic identity formation. Academic criticism of popular Hindi cinema has been thin historically, and Dhurandhar's appearance in academic venues marks a shift in what gets considered worth studying seriously. The shift is partly due to the film's craft, partly due to its commercial scale, and partly due to the generational turnover among film scholars, some of whom are bilingually literate in Hindi-Urdu in ways that earlier generations of Indian film scholars were not. These scholars can read the film's linguistic layering from the inside, which is a precondition for the sort of close reading the material rewards.

What the reception trajectory suggests is that commercial Indian cinema has been capable of sustaining craft-attentive audiences for longer than the industry has admitted, but has not invested in producing work that rewards such audiences. Dhurandhar made the investment, the audience responded, and the industry's development slates are now adjusting to reflect the lesson. The next five years of Hindi commercial cinema will include more craft-attentive projects than the previous five did, and the projects that succeed commercially will validate the shift in development philosophy. This is how industries change: not through manifestos but through commercial evidence accumulated across successful projects that earlier industry logic would have rejected as uncommercial.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Q: What makes Dhurandhar's dialogue lines different from typical Bollywood the pages?

Conventional Bollywood the script announces emotion. Characters say what they feel, what they want, what they are about to do, and the writing ensures that no significant emotional content is left for the person watching to infer. Dhar's screenplay in the Dhurandhar project inverts this approach. Characters say less than they mean, and the viewer has to close the gap between surface speech and underlying emotion. The lines that carry the most weight are often the lines where a character deflects, substitutes an operational briefing for an emotional exchange, or falls silent at the moment a conventional text would deliver a climactic utterance. This restraint-first approach is uncommon in commercial Indic cinema and represents a structural departure from the tradition rather than a tonal adjustment within it.

### Q: Who wrote the dialogue for Dhurandhar?

Aditya Dhar is the director and principal writer of the pair of films. The dialogue writing is attributed to Dhar with collaboration on particular sequences, and the screenplay bears the marks of a single controlling intelligence across both installments: the restrained approach, the bilingual layering, and the character differentiation are consistent in a way that typically indicates a primary writer in place of a committee. Dhar's approach to prose is visible in his earlier work, but the paired project represents a significant expansion of his craft, particularly in the bilingual texture and the cross-character verbal differentiation.

### Q: Why does Hamza speak differently in the Lyari sequences than in the Delhi scenes?

Because he is a different person in each setting. The Lyari Hamza is the cover identity that Jaskirat has inhabited for a decade; his Urdu has absorbed the rhythms, idioms, and cultural references of the Karachi neighbourhood in which he lives. The Delhi Hamza is Jaskirat in the brief moments when the cover is suspended for a debrief, and his Hindi-Punjabi range emerges from under the Urdu. The duology's writing treats these as distinct verbal signatures, and Ranveer Singh's code-switching work performs the distinction so precisely that an attentive a first-time audience member can detect the seam between identities. The divergence is one of the duology's most careful craft investments.

### Q: How does the duology handle the Hindi-Urdu bilingual register?

Dhar writes his Lyari sequences in a language that carries Pakistani Muslim cultural identity and his Delhi sequences in a national tongue that carries Indian institutional identity, without explaining the convention to the viewer or stylising the switch. The text trusts the viewer to register which language is operating in any given scene and to read the language choice as a flag for identity and register. The bilingual texture is also extended with Balochi and Sindhi-inflected speech in certain Lyari community scenes, and with English in the Delhi institutional sequences. The result is a linguistic ecosystem and not a translated work, and the ecosystem carries narrative work that a monolingual screenplay would have to do with exposition.

### Q: What is the "substitution" technique in Dhar's dialogue?

Substitution is the technique by which an emotional exchange is written as an operational briefing, a family conversation is written as a logistical question, or a confession is written as a routine observation. The form of the scene is procedural; the content underneath is emotional. Bansal and Hamza's safe-house meetings are the clearest example: they discuss operational details while performing an act of care that the institutional frame will not permit them to perform verbally. The audience member reads the care through the form, and the substitution carries the emotional load without ever announcing it. Substitution is one of several restraint mechanisms the writing deploys.

### Q: Does Dhurandhar's spoken work land for mass audiences or only for attentive viewers?

Both, which is one of the Dhurandhar films' most significant craft achievements. Dhar writes each major line at two registers: a surface tone that announces enough to land emotionally for a first-time mass viewer, and a subtextual register that rewards viewers willing to parse what the surface is actually doing. Mass-appeal theatre-cheers and multiplex analytical readings often come from the same lines. The calibration refuses the standard compromise between commercial accessibility and literary depth, and the duology's sustained repeat viewership is evidence that the calibration works for both audiences simultaneously rather than for one at the expense of the other.

### Q: How is Rehman Dakait's speech written differently from other characters?

Rehman speaks in long, measured sentences with a cadence of a man who controls rooms through the pace of his voice. His menace lives in the fact that he takes his time, that he does not interrupt or accelerate, that the threat emerges from the accumulation of seemingly non-threatening surfaces. He describes situations, narrates possible futures, asks rhetorical questions, and the threat builds through the pattern instead of through any single line. The syntactic choice matches his community position: he is institutionally embedded in the Karachi underworld, and his ambient authority is built into the rhythm of the lines itself not commented on from outside.

### Q: How is Sushant Bansal's dialogue constructed?

Bansal's lines are the most restrained in the pair because his institutional position constrains even the expression of constraint. He has maximum knowledge and minimum authority, and the pressure that builds between those two poles cannot be verbalised within his institutional role. Dhar writes Bansal's speech as an interior life sealed off from verbal expression: the actor's delivery carries the containment through pauses, micro-adjustments in pitch, and the way his voice drops when Hamza enters. The script is disciplined about giving Bansal lines the character cannot actually say, which is the writing's honesty about his situation.

### Q: Are the dialogues paraphrased or quoted verbatim in this analysis?

Paraphrased, which is the approach this analysis consistently takes. The two films's dialogue is discussed through its mechanisms, its idiom, and its dramatic effects rather than through reproduction of particular lines. This is both a craft choice (the analysis is about how the draft works in place of about which lines are memorable) and a practical one: the writing's effect is often in the cadence, pause placement, and verbal register that a transcribed line cannot fully represent. A watcher who has seen the duology will recognise the scenes under discussion from the paraphrase; a reader who has not seen the two installments can understand the analysis without requiring verbatim reproduction.

### Q: What is the most technically difficult verbal work sequence in the pair of films?

The mehendi sequence in Part 1, in which Hamza's adopted Lyari family is celebrating and Hamza is simultaneously mourning a death he cannot acknowledge, is the most technically demanding sequence in the text. The scene is built almost entirely on withheld speech: Hamza participates in the celebration, engages with family members, performs the rituals of the occasion, and the lines gives him very few lines with which to do this work. The performance carries what the writing refuses to announce, and the sequence's emotional power comes from the viewer's inference that Hamza is doing something the script is not showing. This is the purest test of the restraint approach, and this two-film arc passes it.

### Q: How does the dialogue handle humour in both parts?

Humour in the duology is mostly situational and observational and not scripted as stand-alone comic lines. Characters are funny in the way that people are actually funny, through timing, through apt observations about a shared situation, through the small ironic distances they take from their own positions. Rehman has a dry comic mode that surfaces in unexpected moments; Hamza's Lyari neighbours have a communal comic rhythm that Dhar writes into their background exchanges. The screenplay avoids the Mainstream Indian-cinema convention of the comic subplot, which the duology's restraint register could not accommodate, in favour of humour integrated into the ordinary texture of the scenes.

### Q: Why does Major Iqbal's dialogue sound different from the rest of the duology?

Iqbal is written with a calculated loudness that contrasts with the ambient restraint of the rest of the writing. His institutional position, as a senior Pakistani military figure, gives him permission to be verbally assertive, and Dhar writes him into that permission. Iqbal's interrogation sequences in Part 2 are among the most verbally explicit passages in both installments, and their explicitness reads as characterisation rather than as the pages inconsistency: Iqbal is a man whose role has shaped his speech toward domination of the room. The contrast with Hamza's self-erasing register is the point, and the contrast operates at the level of individual exchanges and at the level of the Part 2 architecture.

### Q: Does the dialogue writing improve or decline from Part 1 to Part 2?

This is a question on which honest criticism can land in different places. The Part 1 Lyari sequences and Part 1 Delhi safe-house exchanges represent the peak of the duology's prose, with the restraint register at its most disciplined and the character differentiation at its most precise. Part 2 is good the text but less uniformly excellent: the climactic action sequences occasionally loosen Hamza's verbal discipline toward a more conventional Bollywood protagonist register, and a few briefing scenes feel expository instead of fully dramatised. An attentive reader who values the purity of the restraint approach will prefer Part 1; a viewer who values the emotional catharsis that Part 2 earns will be more forgiving. Neither reading is wrong.

### Q: How does Dhurandhar compare to Raazi in terms of scripted talk writing?

Meghna Gulzar's Raazi is the closest tonal precursor to the Dhurandhar duology in Hindi-language spy cinema, and its dialogue shares some of the same virtues: restraint, bilingual layering, a refusal to announce emotion. The scale and depth differ. Raazi operates over months not a decade, and its central protagonist's speech work is correspondingly less extensive. Dhar's Dhurandhar films attempts a longer, deeper identity inhabitation and writes accordingly, with Hamza's Urdu absorbing Lyari rhythms across a timescale that Raazi does not attempt. The two films are in dialogue with each other as the lines projects, and Dhurandhar reads as a deepening rather than a repudiation of Raazi's approach.

### Q: What does "calibrating for two audiences" mean in practice?

It means that each major line is written with a surface meaning accessible to a first-time mass someone watching and a layered meaning rewarding to a returning attentive viewer. The surface might be a compliment delivered at a dinner; the layered meaning might be a threat assessment Hamza is making while he delivers the compliment. Both meanings are in the text; neither is privileged. The person watching reads the one they are equipped to read. This calibration is technically more demanding than writing for a single spectators, and the duology's commercial success is evidence that the demand pays off in reach: mass viewers get a film that lands on first viewing, and attentive viewers get a film that rewards re-viewing.

### Q: How does the script handle women characters in the Lyari cover?

The primary female characters in Hamza's adopted Lyari family are written with care, and the Part 1 scenes involving his adopted mother figure carry some of the richest speech in the pair. The writing is less uniformly strong across the full bench of women characters, and some supporting female figures in the cover are left more as registers of Hamza's moral situation than as full participants with their own verbal signatures. A duology that invested the same screenplay energy across all its women characters as it does across Rehman, Bansal, and Iqbal would be, on this dimension, even stronger than the one we have. This is a limitation the criticism should name honestly.

### Q: Does the text work in dubbed versions, or is it tied to the original bilingual register?

The duology's surface dramatic register translates well into dubbing, and viewers watching the film in Tamil, Telugu, or Malayalam dubbed versions have reported strong emotional engagement with the story. What does not fully translate is the Hindi-Urdu code-switching layer, which is specific to the Indo-Islamic linguistic fabric the writing draws on. Viewers watching a dubbed version get the drama, the character differentiation through vocal register, and the restraint approach, but lose the subtextual layer that the bilingual lines generates for viewers literate in both languages. This is an expected consequence of the duology's commitment to the linguistic ecosystem, and it is a limitation in place of a failure.

### Q: Why does Ajay Sanyal speak in such clipped sentences?

Because his authority lives in brevity. Sanyal is a senior operational figure whose decades in the institutional world have reduced his verbal performance to operational minimum. His clipped mode communicates that he has seen too much to still invest energy in rhetorical flourish, that the agency has trained him into a communication style producing minimum risk of misinterpretation, and that his ambient authority does not require verbal elaboration to range. Dhar's script makes Sanyal's brevity carry the weight that Rehman's measured length carries elsewhere: two authorities, two registers, each constructed from the opposite material. Sanjay Dutt's performance embodies the brevity with an economy that matches the writing.

### Q: What should a reader watch for on a second viewing?

The micro-deviations in Hamza's Urdu that flag Jaskirat surfacing under pressure. The rhythm of Rehman's rhetorical questions and how they build threat through accumulation and not through explicit assertion. The clipped-vs-measured contrast between Sanyal and Rehman as authority styles. The substitution work in Bansal-Hamza exchanges, in which operational briefings carry emotional content. The calibration of surface and subtext in each major scene: what the line says and what the line is also doing. And the linguistic layering: Urdu vs North-Indian dialect vs Balochi vs Sindhi-inflected speech as flags for setting and identity. A second viewing that tracks these dimensions will surface roughly half the duology's his script achievement that first-time viewing necessarily misses.

### Q: How does Dhurandhar's the text compare to recent world cinema in the spy genre?

The closer comparison is probably to the best British spy writing of the past two decades than to Hollywood action prose. Works in the le Carre tradition share the restraint register, the subtext-heavy approach, and the interest in identity formation through prolonged deception. What Dhar's this two-film arc adds is the bilingual cultural layering that no English-language spy film has attempted at this depth, and the mass-commercial style that the art-house British work typically does not pursue. Both parts is closer in spirit to a Hindi-Urdu le Carre than to a Bollywood spy thriller in the Tiger or Pathaan lineage, and the text is the primary channel through which this kinship is expressed.


### Q: How do the Lyari community scenes avoid feeling like external observation?

By grounding their language, behaviour, and social texture in the way Lyari actually functions rather than in a screenwriter's generic notion of Pakistani working-class life. The script gives community members their own names, their own greetings, their own concerns about shared acquaintances, and these details compile into a lived neighbourhood rather than an observed setting. When neighbours ask about a shared relative's health, the exchange feels drawn from a specific household rather than invented to provide local colour. Dhar's research investment is audible in this texture: he knows enough about the community to write ambient conversations that would be impossible to write from secondary sources alone, and the knowledge is the shield against the externality trap.

### Q: What role does silence play in the Part 2 climax?

Silence in the Part 2 climax carries the accumulated weight of everything the project has withheld across its runtime. Hamza's final confrontation with Major Iqbal is written so that the verbal exchange is compressed to a minimum and the silences are extended to a maximum, which reverses conventional action-climax pacing. The silences in these scenes are doing the work that a conventional action climax does with announcement: they communicate resolution, cost, and emotional settlement without naming any of those things. This is the craft payoff of five hours of prior restraint, and the climax lands because the middle has saved its verbal capital for other purposes.

### Q: How does the writing handle Hamza's psychological fragmentation?

Through linguistic fragmentation rather than through internal monologue. Hamza's Urdu begins the duology as a careful performance and ends the duology as a lived identity; his Punjabi substrate begins as an ever-present interference and ends as a vestigial memory. Dhar tracks the psychological fragmentation through these linguistic markers rather than through voice-over or confessional monologue. When Hamza in late Part 2 stumbles briefly in his Urdu during a family confrontation, the stumble carries the psychological payload that a conventional screenplay would carry through a flashback montage. The script trusts the audience to read the linguistic slip as the surface of the deeper fracture.

### Q: Does the script use silence differently in Lyari scenes versus Delhi scenes?

Yes, and the difference is one of the manuscript's most disciplined achievements. The district silences are communal: they emerge from social comfort, from a room that does not need to fill itself with talk, from people who have known each other long enough that silence is not awkwardness. Delhi silences are institutional: they emerge from role constraints, from what colleagues are not permitted to say to each other, from the pressure of operational secrecy on personal expression. The same silent beat performs two entirely different dramatic functions depending on the setting, and the script trusts the audience to read the difference from context rather than explaining it.

### Q: Why does Dhar paraphrase actual historical speech rather than quote it?

Because paraphrase preserves the dramatic function of the historical reference without forcing the audience to react to the specific historical words. When the duology references real events that readers of contemporary Indian history will recognise, it references them through characters who respond to the events rather than through reproductions of public statements about them. This is the correct authorial choice: the duology is not a documentary of the events it invokes; it is a dramatic work whose characters live through the atmospheric consequences of those events. Paraphrase respects the line between dramatic engagement and documentary reproduction, and the text maintains the line with consistency throughout both installments.

### Q: How does the script's restraint approach affect its international accessibility?

Surprisingly well, because the restraint approach translates more readily than announcement does. Subtitled versions of the pair of films preserve the core dramatic architecture because the architecture is carried by behaviour, blocking, and pacing rather than by verbal announcement. Anglophone audiences who lack access to the Hindi-Urdu subtext still experience the films as serious dramatic works rather than as action spectacles, because the restraint approach signals seriousness regardless of whether the subtextual layer is accessible. This is one reason the duology has attracted critical attention outside India in ways that more spectacle-heavy Hindi commercial cinema typically does not.

### Q: What distinguishes Dhar's work from other recent Bollywood thrillers on writing the work alone?

Three distinctions. First, the sustained commitment to restraint across a five-hour runtime, where most Indian thrillers restrict themselves to two-hour windows and cannot afford the slow accumulation restraint requires. Second, the bilingual cultural fluency, which most Bollywood thrillers do not attempt at this depth. Third, the cross-character verbal differentiation, which most Indian thrillers outsource to costume, blocking, and music rather than performing through the script itself. Each of these three craft investments is expensive, and few recent Hindi commercial productions have had the development time or the authorial confidence to make all three.

### Q: How does the script handle the passage of time across the cover decade?

Largely through accumulated verbal weathering rather than through explicit time markers. Hamza's Urdu deepens, his Lyari community relationships settle into routines, his knowledge of the neighbourhood gains granularity that can only come from years of presence. The audience reads the passage of time through these accumulations without requiring date cards or voice-over exposition. This is another test of the restraint approach: a script that needed to announce its chronology would undermine the premise of lived immersion that the cover narrative requires. Dhar's script passes the test by building time into the texture rather than into the exposition.

### Q: Is the script's bilingual approach likely to influence future Hindi thrillers?

The influence is already visible in the development slates of several projects announced after the duology's release, which feature bilingual Hindi-Urdu registers, cross-border settings, and restraint-first dialogue approaches. Whether these projects will execute the craft at the level Dhar achieved remains to be seen, as the craft demands are considerable and the production timelines are often insufficient. What the duology has demonstrated is that the commercial risk of bilingual cultural layering is smaller than the industry has historically assumed, and the demonstration will likely embolden producers to approve projects they would have rejected five years ago.

### Q: What should a Bollywood film writer learn from Dhurandhar's script?

That restraint is a craft advantage rather than a commercial liability, that bilingual cultural fluency can be written from the inside rather than performed from the outside, that cross-character verbal differentiation is achievable in a large ensemble if the writer invests in each character's verbal signature from the first page, and that the viewership has more appetite for inference than the commercial industry's development practices typically assume. These are four lessons, each individually significant and collectively transformative, that both installments make available to the writers who will follow Dhar into the craft space he has opened.


### Q: How does the script differentiate between Hamza's conscious performance and his absorbed second nature?

Through the distribution of verbal labour. When Hamza is performing consciously, early in the cover, or in high-pressure sequences where he is aware he is being evaluated, his speech carries small signs of effort: a slightly over-articulated Urdu consonant, a formal construction where colloquial would suit, a sentence that lands exactly on its intended meaning with no casual surplus. When Hamza has absorbed the register into second nature, in ordinary household moments with his adopted Lyari family, his speech loses the effort marks and gains colloquial surplus. He says things that serve no strategic purpose, makes jokes that do not land cleanly, uses idioms that a non-native speaker would not have stumbled into. The absorbed register is readable by its imperfections rather than by its correctness, and Ranveer Singh's performance honours this distinction with precision across both installments.

### Q: What craft lessons can screenwriters outside the thriller genre take from the script?

The restraint philosophy, the verbal differentiation technique, and the trust-the-listener principle all transfer cleanly to other genres. A family drama built on restraint would reward attention the way this script does. A historical epic that differentiates its ensemble through verbal signature would produce the same clarity this script achieves. A coming-of-age story that trusts its audience to infer rather than to be told would land with the emotional force this script generates. None of these lessons are genre-specific; they are craft-general lessons that the thriller genre in Dhar's hands happens to demonstrate with particular clarity. Any Indian screenwriter working on any serious project would benefit from studying the text as a craft reference, and the craft benefits would be independent of whether their own project was a thriller, a melodrama, a comedy, or an epic.

### Q: How does the Part 2 script handle the temporal compression of the revenge arc?

By reducing the number of ordinary household beats and increasing the operational stakes per scene. Part 1 can afford long stretches of Lyari community texture because the cover narrative requires establishing the neighbourhood as Hamza's lived reality; Part 2 has less runtime to cover this ground again, because the audience already knows the Lyari setting from Part 1. The script compensates by front-loading the emotional weight into tighter scenes, and the tightness occasionally costs the pages the breathing room that made Part 1's restraint so effective. This is a visible craft tension, and some reviewers have identified it as the principal limitation of Part 2 relative to Part 1. The criticism is fair, and a version of Part 2 with an additional thirty minutes of breathing room would likely have been an even stronger film than the one we have.

### Q: Does the script engage with the political register of the events it dramatizes?

Obliquely rather than directly, which is one of its more interesting authorial choices. The events the duology references carry heavy political meaning in the Indian and Pakistani public spheres, and a script less confident about its material would have staked out explicit political positions on those events. Dhar's script refuses this move consistently. It dramatizes characters responding to the events without editorialising about them, and it trusts the audience to bring their own political frames to the material. The restraint approach, which governs the script's emotional register, also governs its political register: announcement is avoided, and the audience's inferential work carries the political weight that a more editorialising script would carry through explicit authorial voice. Whether this is political courage or political evasion is a question serious viewers can reasonably disagree on, and the disagreement is productive.

### Q: What happens when Hamza breaks pattern briefly in moments of stress?

The breaks are small, brief, and the script never comments on them from outside the character. A sentence that begins in Urdu ends in a Mainstream Indian construction; a greeting Hamza would normally deliver with warmth arrives with half a second of delay; a joke he would normally laugh at loses him for a beat. These pattern breaks are where Jaskirat surfaces under pressure, and the script trusts the audience to read the surfacing without a cue. Other characters in the scene may or may not register the break; sometimes they do and respond subtly, sometimes they miss it entirely and the dramatic irony lives with the audience. The manuscript's confidence about when to let the breaks go unnoticed and when to let them register is one of its most precise technique investments, and it is the mechanism through which the cover narrative generates its sustained tension across the duology's five-hour span.


### Q: Why does the pair treat silence as a communicative act rather than as pause?

Because in the world the cover inhabits, speech is the primary medium of survival. Every utterance is load-bearing: it can reveal the cover, maintain it, or extend it. Silence in such a world is not absence of communication but its most intense form, the moment where both speakers know that what remains unsaid is heavier than anything that could be said. Dhar's script respects this by treating silences as scripted beats with specific dramatic functions: some are containment, some are deflection, some are recognition, some are refusal. The audience learns across the runtime to distinguish one function from another through context, and the distinctions become part of the texture the paired project rewards.

### Q: How does the script balance exposition with restraint for viewers unfamiliar with Indo-Pakistani intelligence operations?

Through inference scaffolding. The paired project does not lecture viewers on the operational details of RAW, ISI, or cross-border espionage, but it places enough institutional signposts in the ambient texture that attentive viewers can reconstruct the operational logic without explicit exposition. A briefing scene may mention a protocol without defining it; a later scene shows the protocol in practice; a third scene shows the consequences of deviating from the protocol. The viewer absorbs the system through its operation rather than through its description. This is a considerable authorial investment, and it is one of the reasons the script rewards repeat viewing.
