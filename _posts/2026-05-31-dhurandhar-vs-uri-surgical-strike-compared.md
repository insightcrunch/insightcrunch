The question that frames this comparison is not which film is better. It is what the distance between them reveals about an artist in the process of becoming. Uri: The Surgical Strike and the Dhurandhar franchise are separated by approximately six years of filmmaking development, three years of production between projects, and an enormous creative ambition gap that the commercial success of Uri made possible but did not guarantee would be reached. The comparison reveals an artist who used a proof of concept to earn the right to attempt something much harder, and who then attempted it with the specific risks and the specific rewards that much harder things produce when they are attempted by someone who has genuinely prepared to attempt them.

Uri is a tightly made, morally clear, emotionally direct film about a military operation conducted in response to a specific provocation. It does exactly what it sets out to do with more craft and confidence than most debut feature films achieve. Its famous rallying cry, the "How's the josh?" exchange that became a cultural phenomenon, is the film's thesis in dialogue form: it is about a group of people whose institutional identity gives them access to a specific kind of elevated collective purpose that makes them effective, and the film presents this identity and this purpose as straightforwardly admirable. The film's moral architecture is clean because the situation it depicts is morally clean: India was attacked at Uri, India struck back with precision and minimal collateral damage, and the operational success is also a moral success. Uri is the kind of nationalist action film that Indian cinema has made in various forms for decades, but it is made with a cinematographic intelligence and a narrative economy that most of its predecessors do not bring.

The Dhurandhar franchise is not a bigger or more expensive version of Uri. It is a fundamentally different project that asks fundamentally different questions. Where Uri asks what makes soldiers effective, Dhurandhar asks what intelligence operations cost the people who conduct them. Where Uri presents the state as a reliable moral authority that responds to aggression with proportionate force, Dhurandhar presents the state as an institution whose legitimate objectives are pursued through means that extract a human cost the institution is structurally unable to fully acknowledge. Where Uri's nationalism is emotional and communal, Dhurandhar's nationalism is complicated and individual: the franchise endorses the strategic objective while documenting what the individual human cost of pursuing it actually is. [The franchise's complete analysis](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/03/19/complete-analysis-dhurandhar-part-1/) develops these dimensions in full; this article traces the development from Uri's creative choices to Dhurandhar's through the specific analytical dimensions where the distance between the films is most instructive.

Both films are, in the most precise sense, films that their specific historical and cultural moments required. Uri arrived when Indian cinema needed a film that could present nationalist pride in military response to provocation without apology and without irony, that could make the "How's the josh?" moment land as genuine celebration rather than as jingoism, and that could do so with enough cinematic intelligence that the celebration carried artistic credibility alongside emotional force. Dhurandhar arrived when Indian cinema needed something different: a film that could take the nationalist pride that Uri had expressed and ask what it requires at the individual level, that could honor the strategic necessity without ignoring the human cost, that could be nationalist and morally complex simultaneously. The two films are not just two of Aditya Dhar's projects. They are two chapters in a developing conversation between Indian cinema and the Indian state that neither film alone could have conducted.

## Scale: What Dhar Does With More Time

The runtime difference between Uri and Dhurandhar is not merely a production decision. It is a statement of creative intent that precedes every other creative decision and shapes the specific kinds of storytelling each film can attempt. Uri is 138 minutes. Dhurandhar Part 1 is approximately 214 minutes, and Part 2 is comparable. The gap is not a director running long: it is a director who has made a structural choice that the story requires a different relationship to time than commercial convention prescribes.

Uri's 138 minutes is tight by most standards, and the tightness is a virtue. The film's narrative moves with a quality of operational efficiency that mirrors the military operation it depicts: every scene has a clear function, the pacing does not linger, and the film reaches its climax having established exactly the emotional and narrative context the climax requires and nothing more. The tightness is the expression of a creative philosophy: this is the minimum time required to tell this story with this quality of emotional impact. More time would dilute the film's concentrated forward momentum, and the concentration is part of what makes the film's josh moment land with the impact it does.

Dhurandhar's extended runtime represents a different creative philosophy: the story this film is telling cannot be told in 138 minutes because the story's central argument depends on duration. The franchise is arguing that a decade-long intelligence operation has a specific human cost that accumulates with the time the operation requires, and that the cost can only be fully registered by an audience that has spent enough time with the cover's construction to understand, at the level of felt experience rather than intellectual proposition, what the decade has produced. An audience that spends 138 minutes with Hamza understands the cover's construction as a narrative fact. An audience that spends 214 minutes with Hamza understands it as a human experience. The additional 76 minutes of Part 1 alone is not padding. It is the cost of the argument the franchise is making, and the argument requires the time.

Whether the scale represents growth or indulgence is the question that serious analysis of both films must address honestly rather than resolving in the direction of either film's admirers. The answer is that it represents growth in principle and occasionally indulgence in execution. The structural case for Dhurandhar's extended runtime is sound: the story genuinely requires the time. The execution case is slightly more uneven. There are sequences in both franchise films that are longer than the story strictly requires, that are present not because the argument demands them but because the creative ambition of the world-building has produced material that the filmmakers were reluctant to cut. These sequences are not failures: they are often strong in isolation. But they are places where scale tips from growth into indulgence, and honest comparison requires acknowledging both.

## Moral Clarity vs Moral Ambiguity: The Biggest Difference

The most fundamental difference between Uri and Dhurandhar is not scale or runtime or even creative ambition. It is the moral register each film operates in, which is ultimately a question about what kind of audience engagement each film wants to produce.

Uri's moral register is clarity. The situation is legible: Indian soldiers were killed in an attack on the Uri base in Kashmir, and the film depicts India's military response in the form of a surgical strike across the Line of Control. The moral case for the response is never complicated by the film: the attack was a provocation, the response was proportionate, the soldiers who conducted it were professional and admirable, and the film wants the audience to feel the specific pride that identification with effective institutional response to injustice produces. The moral clarity is not naivety or propaganda in the pejorative sense. It is an accurate representation of the moral register that military operations conducted in response to specific provocations genuinely occupy when they are conducted competently and with appropriate constraint. The surgical strike was morally straightforward, and the film's moral clarity is honest to that straightforwardness.

Uri's clarity is also a creative choice, not just a reflection of its subject matter's moral simplicity. Dhar could have complicated the film's moral architecture by developing the Pakistani characters more fully, by examining the strategic context of the Uri attack in ways that introduced geopolitical complexity, or by showing the collateral costs of the surgical strike at the level of the Pakistani communities near the strike sites. The film makes none of these choices. It is not ignorant of the complexity that any military operation carries. It is choosing to tell a specific story about a specific institutional response, and the clarity is in service of that specific story's specific emotional and cultural purpose.

Dhurandhar's moral register is ambiguity, and the ambiguity is earned rather than imposed. The franchise depicts an intelligence operation in which the principal instrument is a human being who builds genuine relationships with the people he is eventually required to betray, in a community that has given him genuine belonging in exchange for a decade of his authentic self. The moral case for the operation is not in doubt: the strategic objective is legitimate, the intelligence is valuable, the eventual disruption of the network that funds cross-border terrorism is justified. But the moral simplicity of Uri's situation is unavailable to Dhurandhar because the franchise insists on registering what the legitimate operation costs, and what it costs is not collateral damage or institutional inefficiency but the specific human relationships that the operation itself creates and then deploys as weapons. [The franchise's central themes](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/04/14/themes-symbolism-dhurandhar-duology/) return to this moral ambiguity consistently because it is the franchise's central subject: not the operation's strategic success but the question of what that success required.

The franchise's treatment of the enemy is the most direct expression of the moral ambiguity. In Uri, the Pakistani militants are functional antagonists: threatening, lethal, and morally straightforward in their opposition to Indian military effectiveness. There is no significant attempt to render them as full human beings with their own perspectives on the conflict because the film's moral architecture does not require it. In Dhurandhar, the Lyari characters who are the criminal network's members are rendered with sufficient human complexity that the audience develops genuine investment in them, which is the precondition for the franchise's moral argument: that disrupting the network costs something to the people in it who are human beings regardless of their connection to the network's violent activities. Uri does not need this complexity because its situation does not create the specific moral problem that complexity would address. Dhurandhar requires it because the franchise's moral argument is built on it.

The moral register comparison also reveals something important about the two films' implicit arguments about Indian nationalism specifically. Uri's argument is that Indian nationalism, expressed through military response to provocation, is straightforwardly admirable. The argument is not qualified by the recognition that military nationalism has costs, because in Uri's specific situation the costs are borne by the adversary rather than by Indian citizens. Dhurandhar's argument is more complicated: Indian nationalism, expressed through intelligence operations of the kind the franchise depicts, is strategically legitimate and humanly devastating simultaneously. The franchise does not resolve this tension because the tension is honest to the specific situation it depicts: the intelligence operation is both necessary and costly, and the cost is borne by a specific Indian citizen who volunteered for the bearing without full knowledge of what the bearing would require. The distance between the two films' nationalism arguments is the distance between a story about what India can do and a story about what India must ask of the people through whom it does things.

## Action Philosophy: What Violence Reveals About Each Film

The difference between Uri's action philosophy and Dhurandhar's is the most technically specific dimension of the comparison, and it is the dimension where the artistic development from one project to the next is most directly observable.

Uri's action sequences are organized around the principle of tactical clarity: the audience should understand where everyone is, what the objective is, and how the operation is progressing toward or away from it at every moment of the action. The surgical strike sequences in Uri are the fullest expression of this philosophy: the camera is positioned to maximize spatial clarity, the editing is timed to make the operation's progress legible, and the result is a sequence that the audience can follow with the same quality of understanding that a military briefing would provide. The tactical clarity serves the film's emotional objective: the audience's pride in the operation's success is only fully available if they understand the operation well enough to appreciate the specific professional competence that the soldiers demonstrate.

Dhurandhar's action philosophy rejects tactical clarity in favor of psychological immediacy. [The action sequences ranking](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/05/14/dhurandhar-action-sequences-ranked-analyzed/) analyzes this in detail, but the comparison to Uri reveals the specific development Dhar has undergone between the two projects. The warehouse fight in Part 1, which is the franchise's most discussed action sequence, is shot in ways that deliberately sacrifice tactical clarity for physical and psychological truth: the camera is inside the fight rather than observing it, the editing is timed to the action's physical reality rather than to a conventional dramatic beat, and the result is a sequence that the audience survives rather than watches. The deliberate abandonment of Uri's tactical clarity is not a failure of filmmaking craft. It is the expression of a different creative argument: violence in Dhurandhar is not tactical. It is desperate, ugly, and costly in ways that Uri's action does not show because Uri's situation does not require those qualities to be shown.

The comparison reveals a specific creative insight that Dhar appears to have developed between the two projects: that the camera's relationship to violence is a moral statement about what kind of violence is being depicted. Uri's cleanly staged action is the visual expression of a morally clean operation. Dhurandhar's chaotic, ugly action is the visual expression of a morally complicated one. The filmmaker who made Uri's action could have made technically competent action sequences for Dhurandhar that were cleaner and more visually conventional. The choice to make them ugly is the choice to be honest about what the franchise's specific context requires violence to look like.

## Nationalism: From Clarity to Complication

Uri is unambiguously nationalist, and its nationalism is its most commercially and culturally significant dimension. The film gave Indian popular cinema a new vocabulary for nationalist sentiment that was neither the crude propaganda of earlier decades nor the embarrassed ambivalence about nationalist feeling that much educated urban cultural production has adopted as a defensive posture. "How's the josh?" works as a rallying cry because it captures something genuine about the specific experience of institutional collective purpose that military service can provide, and the film's willingness to present this experience without irony or qualification earned it a cultural centrality that its pure cinematographic achievements alone would not have produced. Uri is a film that gave a specific social cohort permission to feel what they already felt about India's response to the Uri attack, and the permission was meaningful because the film's craft gave it credibility.

The specific quality of Uri's nationalism deserves more analytical attention than it typically receives. The film's nationalism is institutional rather than ethnic: it is not about what it means to be Hindu or to be Indian in the cultural or ethnic sense. It is about what it means to serve an institution whose purpose is the protection of the nation, and the specific emotional register of the "How's the josh?" moment is the specific pride of institutional belonging and institutional effectiveness rather than the vaguer pride of ethnic or cultural national identity. This distinction matters because it explains the film's unusual cross-demographic appeal: the josh moment resonates with people across India's significant internal cultural differences because it is about the experience of institutional collective purpose, which is available to anyone who has been part of an effective institution working toward a shared goal, rather than about any particular version of Indian identity.

Dhurandhar's nationalism is considerably more complicated, and the complication is visible in the franchise's specific reception among different audience segments. The franchise endorses the strategic objective unambiguously: Operation Dhurandhar's disruption of the cross-border terrorism network is presented as justified and necessary, and the film does not waver from this endorsement. What the franchise complicates is the endorsement's relationship to the human cost through which the objective is achieved. The franchise's Hamza develops genuine empathy for Rehman, genuine affection for Jameel, genuine investment in the community life of Lyari, and these genuine emotions are the instruments through which the operation achieves its objectives. The franchise's nationalism is therefore not the simple version in which India's strategic interests and India's moral position are identical. It is the complicated version in which India's strategic interests are legitimate, India's moral position in the specific means of pursuing those interests is more uncertain, and the person who pays the cost of that uncertainty is not an abstract operative but a specific Punjabi Sikh man named Jaskirat Singh Rangi.

This complication is the most significant marker of Dhar's development from Uri to Dhurandhar as a filmmaker who takes seriously the responsibility of nationalism as a subject rather than as a sentiment. Uri could be described as a film that makes nationalism feel good. Dhurandhar could be described as a film that makes nationalism feel real, in all the complexity and discomfort that real entails. Both descriptions are accurate within their films' specific creative contexts, and the distance between them is the measure of an artist who has decided that the responsible exercise of nationalist creative power requires more honesty about its costs than its pleasures.

The specific question of how the two films' nationalism will age over time is worth addressing, because nationalist films are more vulnerable to dating than most other genres: the specific political context that made a nationalist argument feel timely can shift in ways that make the argument feel dated or uncomfortable. Uri's nationalism was calibrated to a specific political moment and a specific military event, and the film's emotional resonance may become more historically contingent as the specific political moment recedes. Dhurandhar's nationalism is built on a more durable foundation: the relationship between the state and the individual who serves it is a philosophical question rather than a political position, and the franchise's exploration of this question will remain analytically relevant regardless of how the specific political context of the India-Pakistan relationship evolves. The franchise may therefore age better than Uri not because it is a better film but because its nationalist argument is grounded in something more durable than the specific political enthusiasm that Uri's nationalism required.

## Technical Craft: What Improved, What Changed, What Stayed

The technical craft comparison between Uri and Dhurandhar reveals a filmmaker who has developed across several specific dimensions while maintaining consistency in others.

The most significant technical development is in [cinematography and visual language](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/05/28/dhurandhar-cinematography-visual-style/). Uri's cinematography is competent and occasionally distinguished: the surgical strike sequences are spatially clear and kinetically effective, and the character sequences use framing and composition with more intentionality than most comparable commercial films. Dhurandhar's cinematography is analytically sophisticated in ways that Uri's is not: the color grammar, the camera movement as cover-condition indicator, the compositional power argument, are all visual storytelling instruments that Uri did not deploy and that represent genuine development of the filmmaker's visual intelligence. The development is not a repudiation of Uri's visual approach, which was appropriate to Uri's creative objectives. It is an expansion of the filmmaker's visual vocabulary that the more ambitious creative objectives of Dhurandhar required.

The sound design development is similarly significant. Uri's sound design is sharp and precise, with the specific quality of military action sound that makes the surgical strike sequences viscerally impactful. The sound of boots on ground, the specific quality of weapons fire in the outdoor environments of the strike sequences, the controlled sound environment that mirrors the operation's professional execution: all of these are deployed with care and technical competence. Dhurandhar's sound design does different things, specifically the [soundtrack and score's integration with the narrative's psychological architecture](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/05/04/dhurandhar-soundtrack-background-score-analysis/). The franchise's use of sound as a psychological rather than purely sensory instrument represents a development beyond Uri's approach, deploying sound in the service of the franchise's identity-fragmentation argument in ways that Uri's cleaner narrative structure did not require.

The narrative structuring shows the most significant development. Uri's narrative structure is classical in the best sense: it moves forward, it knows where it is going, and it gets there with minimal detour. The structure is appropriate to a film about a specific operation that has a specific beginning, middle, and end. Dhurandhar's narrative structure is more ambitious and more complex, organizing a decade-long timeline across two films while maintaining the specific dramatic tension that the franchise requires to sustain audience engagement at this scale. The franchise is not uniformly successful in this structural ambition, as the comparison article's honest analysis must acknowledge: there are structural sequences in both films that do not achieve the coherence that the most accomplished portions demonstrate. But the ambition itself, the willingness to attempt a narrative structure of this complexity, represents a significant development from Uri's more modest structural approach.

The editing has developed in ways that are closely connected to the cinematographic development: Uri's editing is efficient and serves narrative clarity. Dhurandhar's editing is patient and serves psychological accumulation. The development from efficient to patient represents a different theory of what commercial cinema's editing rhythm should do, and it is a theory that the franchise's commercial success has validated: the patient editing did not cost the franchise its audience. It produced an audience that was willing to be patient alongside the editing, which is a different and in some ways more valuable commercial achievement than generating an audience that needs efficiency to stay engaged.

The production design development between the two films also deserves specific attention. Uri's production design is appropriate and professional: the military environments are accurate, the operational planning spaces look like operational planning spaces, and the Uri base location establishes the film's geographic context with the efficiency the film's pacing requires. Dhurandhar's production design is a creative achievement in its own right: the Lyari world is built with the specific attention to geographic and cultural detail that the franchise's world-building ambitions require, and the contrast between Lyari's visual texture and Delhi's institutional spaces is as much a product of production design as of cinematography. The franchise's production design team created a world rather than a setting, which is the highest level of achievement available in the discipline and which represents a significant development beyond Uri's more conventionally accomplished production design.

## Vicky Kaushal vs Ranveer Singh: How the Lead Changes Everything

The comparison between Vicky Kaushal's Uri performance and Ranveer Singh's Dhurandhar performance is the most subjectively contested dimension of this comparison, because it involves a choice between two different theories of what a nationalist spy thriller's lead performance should do.

Kaushal's Major Vihaan Singh Shergill is a performance of controlled intensity. The character is a professional whose emotions are managed rather than expressed, whose grief for his wife exists in a specific compartment that the professional identity keeps sealed during operational hours, and whose effectiveness as a military leader depends precisely on the quality of personal compartmentalization that makes him able to direct others under extreme stress. Kaushal inhabits this controlled intensity with complete conviction and with the specific physicality of someone who has done the physical preparation to make the military bearing authentic at the level of the body rather than of costume and gesture. The performance's great achievement is its specificity: it is not a generic military hero but a specific version of military heroism that is recognizable to anyone who has been around professional soldiers, the version that combines genuine competence with the specific kind of emotional economy that professional violence requires.

Singh's Hamza Ali Mazari is the opposite of controlled intensity. It is managed volatility: a performance of someone who contains multiple psychological states simultaneously, any of which might surface at any moment depending on what the social situation requires, and who has learned to make this volatility an operational asset rather than a liability. [The complete analysis of Singh's performance](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/06/13/ranveer-singh-career-best-performance-dhurandhar/) documents this in full. What the comparison to Kaushal adds is the specific insight that the two performances represent different theories of what the human cost of service looks like at the level of the body and face. Kaushal's Vihaan contains his cost in the compartment that his grief occupies; the cost is real but managed. Singh's Hamza's cost is visible in the volatility that the performance maintains throughout both films: the cost has been distributed through the entire personality rather than sequestered in a specific compartment, which is the franchise's argument about what a decade of deep-cover work does to identity that a military operation of weeks cannot.

Neither performance is better than the other in the absolute sense. They are better and worse at different things: Kaushal is better at the specific performance of professional military identity, which is what his film requires. Singh is better at the performance of identity fragmentation under extended institutional pressure, which is what his franchise requires. The comparison reveals that both films cast exactly the right lead for what the film is doing, which is a significant production achievement in both cases and a specific marker of how clearly each film's creative team understood what the creative objectives required at the level of the lead performance.

## What Uri Does Better

The comparison must be honest about the dimensions in which Uri outperforms the franchise, because failure to identify them is not critical honesty but franchise partisanship.

Uri is better at emotional directness. The film's climactic sequences land with an emotional impact that is immediately available and does not require the specific investment of prior engagement that Dhurandhar's most affecting sequences require. A viewer who watches Uri without prior familiarity with the political context, without specific knowledge of the Uri attack or Indian military culture, will still be moved by what happens at the film's end because the film's emotional architecture has done the work of building that response within its 138 minutes. A viewer who watches Dhurandhar Part 2 without having seen Part 1 or without significant prior engagement with the franchise will have access to most of the plot but will not receive the emotional full weight of the franchise's most affecting moments, because those moments depend on accumulated investment that only the full franchise experience produces.

Uri is better at pace. The film moves with a confidence and forward momentum that the franchise occasionally sacrifices to its world-building ambitions. The sequences in Dhurandhar that feel slightly long are long because the creative ambition of building a real world requires screen time that narrative efficiency would trim. Uri's tighter runtime means it never faces this tradeoff, and the result is a film that never asks the audience to wait for things to become relevant again. The franchise occasionally does ask this of its audience, and while the audience the franchise has built has demonstrated willingness to wait, the waiting is a cost that Uri's more modest ambitions do not impose.

Uri is better at the specific pleasures of genre satisfaction. The surgical strike is a satisfying genre event: a precisely executed mission that delivers the cathartic outcome that the film has been building toward. The franchise's genre satisfactions are more complicated and more deferred: Part 1 ends without the cathartic resolution that conventional spy thriller structure would provide, and Part 2's resolution is emotionally complex in ways that make it less immediately satisfying even as it is more lasting in its emotional impact. The audience that wants genre satisfaction in the clean form that Uri provides will find the franchise a more demanding experience.

## What Dhurandhar Does Better

What the franchise does better is almost everything that requires depth, duration, and the specific kind of complexity that moral ambiguity and psychological accumulation produce.

The franchise is better at world-building. Uri's Uri is a setting. The franchise's Lyari is a world, built from the specific human details that [the supporting characters](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/05/24/supporting-characters-analysis-dhurandhar/) provide and from the production design's commitment to geographic specificity. The world has a texture and a life that Uri's settings do not aspire to, and the franchise's sustained engagement with this world is part of what gives its eventual disruption its specific weight.

The franchise is better at character psychology. Major Vihaan is a fully realized professional figure. Jaskirat Singh Rangi becoming Hamza Ali Mazari is a fully realized psychological tragedy, and the depth of psychological realization is the franchise's most significant creative achievement relative to Uri. The distance between what the two films do with character psychology is the distance between craft and ambition: Uri demonstrates genuine craft in its character work. Dhurandhar demonstrates ambition that occasionally exceeds its craft but that also occasionally achieves something that craft alone cannot produce.

The franchise is better at sustaining narrative across duration. A film like Uri has the simpler creative task of sustaining audience engagement across 138 minutes, which is the duration range that commercial cinema has the most experience and the most developed techniques for managing. A franchise like Dhurandhar must sustain audience engagement across 214 minutes of Part 1 and a comparable runtime for Part 2, which is a creative challenge that requires techniques and structural approaches that commercial cinema's established conventions do not fully provide. The franchise is not uniformly successful at this challenge, as the honest analysis of both films acknowledges. But its successes at the challenge are more significant precisely because the challenge is harder: the franchise has accomplished sustained audience engagement across a combined runtime of over seven hours in ways that demonstrate genuine structural and narrative innovation rather than the application of established techniques to familiar problems.

The franchise is better at the long game, commercially and artistically. Uri's commercial achievement was substantial and its artistic achievement was genuine within its specific register. The franchise's commercial achievement exceeded Uri's significantly, and its artistic achievement operates in a register that Uri did not attempt. The comparison of the two reveals an artist who used the commercial security that Uri provided to attempt artistic risks that Uri's more modest creative scope did not require, and who produced, at the franchise level, something that the proof of concept could not have predicted but that the proof of concept made possible.

The franchise is also better at building the kind of audience relationship that sustains commercial performance beyond the opening weekend. Uri's audience came for the event of the film and had the experience the event promised. The franchise's audience came for the event and then returned for the world, which is a fundamentally different commercial relationship and one that produces the sustained holdover performance that [the franchise's box office records](https://reportmedic.org/tools/bollywood-box-office-explorer.html) document in detail. The franchise built a world that its audience wanted to return to, and the commercial difference between Uri's respectable theatrical run and the franchise's extraordinary holdover performance is the commercial expression of the creative difference between a film that offers an event and a franchise that offers a world.

## The Connective Thread: What Remains Consistent

The comparison of the two projects must also identify what remains consistent across the creative development, because an artist's consistent preoccupations are as revealing as their developments.

Dhar is consistently interested in institutional competence as a subject. Uri celebrates military institutional competence: the soldiers are effective, the planning is thorough, and the operation succeeds because the institution has trained and equipped and deployed its people appropriately. Dhurandhar examines intelligence institutional competence with the same basic respect: the operation is well designed, the tradecraft is appropriate to the situation, and the strategic objective is legitimate. Both films treat their respective institutions as functional and professional rather than as corrupt or incompetent, which is a creative choice that distinguishes Dhar from many filmmakers who use institutional settings primarily as backgrounds for individual heroism or institutional critique.

Dhar is consistently interested in the specific quality of professional identity that high-stakes institutional work produces. Vihaan's controlled intensity and Hamza's managed volatility are both performances of what professional identity looks like when it is fully inhabited, and the specific quality of each performance reflects the specific demands of each institutional context. The through-line between the two is the filmmaker's consistent fascination with what it looks like, from the outside and from inside the performance, to be someone who has allowed their professional identity to shape their personal identity at a fundamental level. This fascination is the creative source from which both films draw, and it is the preoccupation that most clearly connects them despite their significant differences in tone, scale, and moral register.

Dhar is also consistently interested in the relationship between individual sacrifice and institutional purpose. Uri examines this relationship at the level of military service, where the sacrifice is visible, honored, and understood by the community in which it occurs. Dhurandhar examines it at the level of intelligence service, where the sacrifice is invisible, unacknowledged by the community because it cannot be, and honored only in the specific private knowledge of the operational participants. Both films take the relationship between individual sacrifice and institutional purpose seriously rather than treating it as a given, and both films ask what it costs and what it produces rather than simply celebrating it as patriotic virtue. The development from Uri's relatively uncomplicated treatment of this relationship to Dhurandhar's more complex treatment is the specific marker of how the filmmaker's engagement with his central preoccupation has deepened across the two projects.

The comparison of Uri and Dhurandhar is ultimately a study of what creative ambition looks like when it is allowed to grow rather than being constrained to repeat the formula that proved commercially successful. [The complete guide to the Dhurandhar franchise](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/06/17/dhurandhar-franchise-complete-guide/) documents what the ambition produced. This article documents where it came from. And [the box office data for the franchise's commercial trajectory](https://reportmedic.org/tools/bollywood-box-office-explorer.html) is the evidence that the audience for ambitious Hindi cinema is large enough to reward the filmmaker who takes the risk of going further than the proof of concept required. Uri showed that Dhar could make a great commercial film within the established parameters of the nationalist action genre. Dhurandhar showed that he could redefine what the genre was capable of being. The distance between those two achievements is the distance between a filmmaker who has arrived and a filmmaker who is still becoming, and becoming is always the more interesting story.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Q: Which film is better, Uri or Dhurandhar?

The question is understandable but analytically unhelpful, because the films are doing different things with different creative ambitions and different creative registers. Uri is better at what it is trying to do than almost any comparable Hindi film has been at attempting the same thing. Dhurandhar is better at what it is trying to do than any Hindi film has previously attempted its specific combination of psychological depth, operational sophistication, and moral ambiguity. Judging them against each other requires establishing a single standard that applies to both, and the only standard that applies equally is craft: both films are made with genuine craft and intelligence. Dhurandhar applies that craft to more ambitious creative objectives and achieves more of what it attempts, but what it attempts is also harder to fail at in ways that are immediately obvious. Uri is the better film at what it does. Dhurandhar attempts more. These are not contradictory statements.

### Q: Did Uri's commercial success create pressure on Dhurandhar to be more of the same?

The creative and commercial history of the Dhurandhar project suggests that Uri's commercial success created the opposite of pressure to repeat: it created the creative and financial freedom to attempt something significantly different. An Aditya Dhar who had made a commercial failure with Uri would have faced significant pressure to stay within conventional genre expectations for his second project. An Aditya Dhar who had made a major commercial success was in the position to take significant creative risks, because the commercial track record provided the investor confidence required to support an expensive and ambitious two-film franchise that was considerably more commercially unconventional than Uri. The "How's the josh?" phenomenon did not trap Dhar in a nationalist action film formula. It gave him the commercial standing to abandon that formula for something harder and more ambitious.
