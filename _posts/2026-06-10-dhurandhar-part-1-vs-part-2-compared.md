Part 1 and Part 2 are not two halves of the same film. They are two different films that share a story, and the difference between them is not simply a matter of where the narrative cuts occur. Part 1 is an infiltration thriller: its organizing logic is the slow construction of trust, the patient accumulation of access, and the psychological cost of maintaining a performance across years of daily pressure. Part 2 is a revenge saga: its organizing logic is the deployment of everything Part 1 built toward a conclusion that is simultaneously the operation's strategic success and Hamza's most complete personal devastation. The same character crosses both films, but the character he is in each is as different as the genres the two films are working in. The analysis of what changes between Part 1 and Part 2, and what stays consistent despite the changes, is the analysis of how a franchise evolves without losing its identity.

## Narrative Structure: Infiltration vs Execution

The structural difference between Part 1 and Part 2 is the structural difference between building and deploying, and every other difference between the films is downstream from this one. Part 1's narrative is organized around the construction of something: the cover, the relationships, the intelligence picture that the operation's strategic objective requires. Its temporal organization is chronological with selective flashback, moving forward through the decade while occasionally pulling back to the pre-operation context that established who Jaskirat was before he became Hamza. The chronological progression is the expression of the building logic: the narrative moves forward because the operation moves forward, and the audience's sense of time passing is the audience's sense of what the accumulation of the operation's years produces.

Part 2's narrative structure is more compressed and more aggressive in its temporal management. Where Part 1 builds through accumulated duration, Part 2 deploys through escalating intensity. The decade that Part 1 constructs across its runtime becomes the material that Part 2 weaponizes: the relationships, the access, the intelligence, and the psychological investment are all converted from accumulated assets into operational tools in Part 2's execution phase. The structural compression of Part 2 relative to Part 1 is not a failure of patience: it is the appropriate structural expression of what execution feels like relative to what construction feels like. Building requires duration. Deploying requires momentum.

The two films' structural differences are visible most immediately in how they handle their midpoints. Part 1's midpoint is a deepening: the point where the cover has become sufficiently established that the cover's social integration rather than its technical maintenance is the primary operational challenge. The midpoint of Part 1 is the point where Hamza stops being someone maintaining a cover and starts being someone living inside one, and the shift is the structural pivot on which Part 1's entire emotional architecture turns. Part 2's midpoint is a no-return point: the moment where the execution phase has committed sufficiently that withdrawal would cost more than continuation. The structural pivot of Part 2 is not a deepening but a crossing: once crossed, the franchise's remaining questions are not about whether the operation will succeed but about what the success will cost.

The flashback structure evolves between the two films in ways that communicate the franchise's changing relationship to the past. Part 1's flashbacks are primarily expository: they establish who Jaskirat was, what was taken from him, and how the recruitment occurred. They are strategically positioned in the narrative to provide the psychological context for specific operational moments, and they function as explanations for why Hamza makes specific choices at specific points in the construction phase. Part 2's relationship to the past is different: the Part 1 events are no longer in the past requiring explanation but in the present requiring reckoning. The moments that Part 2 returns to from Part 1 are not returned to for explanation but for emotional weight: the audience is being invited to bring the full accumulated significance of those moments to bear on the Part 2 sequences that reference them. The shift from explanatory flashback to emotionally weighted callback is one of the most specific structural markers of how Part 2 treats Part 1 as its premise rather than as its backstory.

The structural comparison also reveals a difference in how the two films manage their endings. Part 1 ends on an open note that is simultaneously the operational midpoint and the emotional cliff: the cover has held, the access has been built, and the conclusion is still ahead. The ending is structurally honest to the operation's status at that point, but it requires the audience to accept an ending that does not deliver the cathartic resolution that conventional narrative structure promises. Part 2's ending delivers that resolution while complicating it: the operation succeeds, the strategic objective is achieved, and the human cost is simultaneously fully visible and fully uncompensated. The two endings together produce a complete emotional arc: Part 1 withholds and Part 2 delivers, but what Part 2 delivers is more complicated than what Part 1's withholding seemed to promise.

## Emotional Register: Slow-Burn vs Escalation

The emotional registers of the two films are as different as their narrative structures, and the difference is not a tonal inconsistency but a tonal evolution that reflects the logical progression from the operation's construction phase to its execution phase.

Part 1's emotional register is defined by containment. The dominant emotional experience of Part 1 is the specific quality of sustained attention that the cover's maintenance requires: the audience is always aware that something could go wrong, that the fragile social reality Hamza has constructed could crack at any moment, and that the stakes of any specific social interaction are higher than the interaction's surface content would suggest. This awareness generates a specific kind of sustained low-level tension that is different from conventional thriller tension: it does not spike and release on the rhythm of conventional dramatic beats but accumulates steadily across the film's runtime, producing an emotional weight that is felt most completely only in retrospect, when the full extent of what Part 1 built becomes visible in contrast to what Part 2 deploys.

Part 2's emotional register is defined by release: not the clean cathartic release of a resolved conflict but the more complicated release of a decade's accumulated pressure being discharged through actions whose consequences are immediate and visible in ways that the slow construction of Part 1 never allowed. The emotional escalation of Part 2 is the direct expression of what the release of a decade's contained pressure actually feels like, and the franchise is honest that this release is not pleasurable in the conventional action cinema sense. The revenge arc's emotional register is not triumphant: it is the specific emotional quality of someone doing what they have spent a decade preparing to do and finding that the preparation did not adequately account for what the doing would cost.

The intimate space of Part 1's Lyari sequences and the more open geography of Part 2's execution phase are the spatial expressions of the two emotional registers. Part 1 is contained: the Lyari neighborhood's tight streets and domestic interiors are the physical expression of the cover's psychological compression. Part 2 opens up: the execution phase moves through larger spaces, more varied locations, and more physically expansive sequences that express the release of the contained pressure. But the opening up is not liberation: it is exposure, and the franchise's visual language, examined in [the cinematography analysis](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/05/28/dhurandhar-cinematography-visual-style/), makes this distinction through the specific quality of the open spaces Part 2 inhabits, which carry the emotional register of vulnerability rather than freedom.

## Hamza in Each Film: Becoming vs Unbecoming

The most analytically important question the comparison raises is whether Hamza is the same character in both films, and the honest answer is that he is the same character in the sense that a person is the same across ten years of significant life change: the continuity is real but the content has shifted in ways that make the early and late versions recognizably related without being equivalent.

Part 1's Hamza is in the process of becoming. The construction phase of the cover produces a version of Hamza who is continuously developing the cover's social and psychological reality: accumulating community membership, deepening relationships, building the specific knowledge of the Lyari world that genuine belonging requires. The Hamza of Part 1 is defined by the process of becoming Hamza, which means he is always slightly in motion: slightly more embedded at the end of any given sequence than he was at the beginning, slightly further from Jaskirat and slightly more completely Hamza. [The origin story analysis](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/05/08/jaskirat-singh-rangi-origin-story-analysis/) tracks this becoming from the recruitment through the cover's construction; the comparison adds that the becoming is the structural principle of Part 1 rather than merely one of its narrative dimensions.

Part 2's Hamza is in the process of unbecoming. The execution phase requires Hamza to dismantle the very thing Part 1 spent its entire runtime building: the relationships, the community membership, the decade of accumulated social reality. Each operational action in the execution phase is also an act of self-destruction: the cover that took a decade to build is being dismantled in the service of the institutional objective that the decade served. The Hamza of Part 2 is defined by the process of unbecoming Hamza, which means he is always moving slightly backward toward a self that is no longer the self he started from. The unbecoming is not the restoration of Jaskirat: it is the erasure of Hamza without the recovery of the original self, producing the specific kind of existential nowhere that the franchise's [ending analysis](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/06/23/dhurandhar-ending-explained-both-parts/) examines.

[Ranveer Singh's performance](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/06/13/ranveer-singh-career-best-performance-dhurandhar/) carries both phases with physical specificity that the comparison makes most visible. Part 1 Singh occupies space with a deliberate ease that communicates the cover's management: the body is performing naturalness rather than expressing it. Part 2 Singh occupies space differently: there is a quality of compression in the physical performance, as if the body has been under sustained pressure for so long that it has learned to take up less space than it naturally would. The physical comparison across the two films is the most immediate available evidence that the comparison between the two Hamzas is the comparison between two phases of a single progressive damage rather than between two distinct characters.

## The Villain Shift: Rehman vs Major Iqbal

The shift in primary antagonist between Part 1 and Part 2 is not simply a personnel change: it is a fundamental change in the franchise's threat landscape that reflects the structural transformation from infiltration thriller to revenge saga.

Rehman Dakait, Part 1's primary antagonist, is a specific kind of threat: intimate, community-embedded, relational. His menace comes from his proximity to Hamza, from the decade of trust that the cover has cultivated and that makes his potential discovery of the cover's falseness more devastatingly consequential than it would be from a stranger's discovery. Rehman is threatening precisely because the relationship between him and Hamza is genuine on Rehman's side: he trusts Hamza in ways that make the eventual betrayal of that trust the franchise's central moral event. The threat Rehman represents is not the threat of external power but the threat of the relationship's moral weight: the longer the cover holds, the higher the moral cost of its eventual deployment.

Part 2's primary antagonist, Major Iqbal of Pakistani intelligence, represents an entirely different threat architecture. Iqbal is an institutional threat rather than a relational one: he is dangerous not because he knows Hamza but because he knows about Hamza, or because the institutional intelligence apparatus he represents is capable of discovering what Hamza is in ways that Rehman's personal trust cannot protect against. The shift from Rehman's relational threat to Iqbal's institutional threat is the structural expression of the shift from infiltration to execution: in the infiltration phase, the primary threat is to the cover's social authenticity in the community it inhabits. In the execution phase, the primary threat is to the operation's security against the institutional counter-intelligence apparatus that the execution phase activates.

The quality of menace in the two antagonists is as different as their structural functions. Rehman's menace is warm in the way that genuine human connection can be threatening when its basis is false: he is dangerous because you care what he thinks of you, and you care because the decade has produced genuine investment on your side as well as on his. Iqbal's menace is cold in the way that institutional power is always cold: he is dangerous because he does not care about you at all, only about whether you represent a threat to the institutional interests he serves. The two registers of menace correspond to the two registers of vulnerability the franchise explores: Part 1's vulnerability is the vulnerability of an authentic relationship built on a false premise, and Part 2's vulnerability is the vulnerability of an individual against an institutional apparatus that is designed to be impervious to individual authenticity.

The specific performances of Rehman and Iqbal reflect the different registers of menace their characters embody. Rehman's performance carries the specific warmth of community authority: threatening because the warmth is real and because the power behind the warmth is capable of becoming something else. Iqbal's performance carries the specific coldness of institutional authority: threatening because the power is real and because the institution behind the power has resources that no individual can match. The franchise casts and performs both antagonists with the understanding that threat in both its forms, relational and institutional, must be specifically embodied rather than generically represented, and both performances succeed at this specific task.

The comparison between the two antagonists also reveals something about the franchise's understanding of what makes power dangerous in its different forms. Relational power is dangerous because it produces genuine vulnerability in the person who holds it as well as in the person over whom it is held: Rehman's power over Hamza is real, and Hamza's authentic investment in the relationship is the source of that power. Institutional power is dangerous because it requires no genuine relationship and no genuine vulnerability: Iqbal's threat to the operation does not depend on any authentic connection between him and Hamza and cannot be neutralized by any authentic connection that Hamza attempts to build. The franchise's shift from Rehman to Iqbal as primary antagonist is the shift from a story about the danger of genuine connection to a story about the danger of institutional power, and the comparison between the two antagonists is the comparison between the franchise's two most specific accounts of what makes human beings vulnerable to each other and to the structures they inhabit.

## Technical Evolution: Bigger vs Better

The technical comparison between Part 1 and Part 2 requires making the distinction between films that are technically better and films that are technically more ambitious, because the two are not always the same and the franchise's relationship between them is specific and worth tracing.

Part 2 is technically more ambitious than Part 1 in almost every dimension: larger action sequences, more elaborate production design, wider geographic scope, more complex visual effects. The larger budget that the franchise's Part 1 commercial success enabled is visible in Part 2 at every level of production, and the specific ambitions that Part 2 pursues with this larger budget are generally well-executed within their own terms. The harbor chase sequence, the ISI compound confrontation, and several other Part 2 sequences are technically spectacular in ways that Part 1, with its more modest production scale, could not have attempted.

Part 1 is technically better in the specific sense that its technical achievements are more consistently aligned with its creative objectives. ## Which Film Is Better?

The franchise's own brief demands that this comparison take a position rather than retreat to the comfortable conclusion that both films are excellent in different ways. The position: Part 1 is the better film. The argument follows, and then the complication of the argument.

Part 1 is the better film because its creative achievements are more consistently aligned with its creative objectives across its full runtime. The infiltration thriller's philosophical commitment to the slow accumulation of pressure, the patient construction of a world worth caring about, and the psychological precision of the cover's maintenance is maintained with greater consistency in Part 1 than the revenge saga's philosophical commitments are maintained in Part 2. Part 1 never sacrifices its creative vision for commercial imperatives in the specific sequences where Part 2 occasionally does. Part 1's restraint is more complete than Part 2's, and the restraint is the franchise's most valuable creative quality.

Part 1 earns the most sustained attention to the smallest moments: the dinner table conversation, the neighborhood gathering, the specific quality of a shared silence between Hamza and Jameel. These moments are the franchise at its most ambitious and its most accomplished, and they belong overwhelmingly to Part 1. The Part 1 Lyari is the franchise's most significant creative achievement in any single category, and it is an achievement that Part 2 can deploy but cannot match in the sustained quality of its construction. Part 1 is the better film in the way that a foundation is a better architectural achievement than the building it supports: the building is larger and more visible, but the foundation is what makes it possible and what required more precise craft.

Part 1 is also the better film in the specific quality of its surprise. The audience that encounters Part 1 is encountering a kind of Hindi commercial cinema it has not previously experienced: a spy film organized around the slow accumulation of human cost rather than around the efficient delivery of operational excitement. The surprise of the discovery is part of Part 1's achievement, and Part 2, arriving with the franchise's reputation established, cannot replicate the specific quality of that surprise. Part 2 is expected to be what it is. Part 1 was not expected to be what it is.

The complication of this argument is the observation that Part 1 is the better film only because Part 2 exists to be its consequence. Part 1's achievement is partly dependent on the promise of Part 2: the restraint, the withholding of resolution, and the sustained accumulation of emotional weight are only fully justified by Part 2's eventual deployment of what Part 1 has built. A Part 1 without a Part 2 would be a film that builds something and then withholds the conclusion, which would be dramatically unsatisfying in ways that genuine artistic achievement alone cannot compensate for. Part 1 is the better film within a franchise context where Part 2 exists to redeem its withholding. It is not clearly the better film as a standalone creative object, because standalone creative objects must be complete, and Part 1's creative vision requires Part 2's completion to achieve its full meaning.

The further complication is that the position "Part 1 is better" is available only to a viewer who has seen both films and who is comparing them analytically. The viewer experiencing both films chronologically in their first viewing will likely experience Part 2 as the more emotionally powerful film, because Part 2 contains the resolution that Part 1 withholds, and the resolution's emotional weight, earned by Part 1's patient construction, belongs to Part 2's viewing experience rather than to Part 1's. A viewer who experiences Part 1 as the better film on first viewing is a viewer who has not yet received what Part 2 delivers, which is not a valid basis for the comparison.

The honest summary: Part 1 is the more consistent and more restrained creative achievement. Part 2 is the more necessary one and the more emotionally complete experience for the viewer who has been present for both. The franchise's genius is in needing both things to be true simultaneously, and the comparison article's job is to make that simultaneity visible rather than to resolve it into a verdict that the franchise itself refuses to deliver.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Q: Which film should a new viewer watch first?

New viewers should watch Part 1 before Part 2, and not because the narrative requires it, though it does. The more important reason is that Part 1 establishes the franchise's specific emotional grammar: the pace, the restraint, the specific quality of world-building investment that makes Part 2's deployment meaningful. A viewer who watches Part 2 without having watched Part 1 will follow the narrative and receive the commercial blockbuster experience that Part 2's larger production delivers. A viewer who watches Part 1 first will bring to Part 2 the specific accumulated investment that the franchise requires to deliver its fullest emotional impact. The viewing order is the difference between receiving the franchise's conclusion and receiving the franchise's argument.

### Q: What does Part 2 do better than Part 1?

Part 2's specific achievements that exceed Part 1's include: the ISI compound confrontation, which is the franchise's most formally accomplished action sequence and arguably its most morally complex; the execution phase's sustained emotional momentum, which generates a quality of irreversible forward motion that Part 1's patience cannot produce; the specific handling of Hamza's psychological condition in the final sequences, which is Part 2's most psychologically precise contribution to the franchise's central argument; and the resolution's specific quality of moral complexity, which is more demanding and more honestly painful than the conventional revenge saga resolution that Part 2's genre would have permitted. ### Q: What does Part 1 do better than Part 2?

Part 1's specific achievements that exceed Part 2's include: the Lyari world-building, which is the franchise's most sustained and most accomplished creative investment; the dialogue restraint's consistency, which is more complete in Part 1 than in Part 2; the visual language's alignment with its creative objectives, which is tighter in Part 1's more modest production context; the Hamza-Jameel relationship's specific emotional texture, which is established in Part 1 and deployed in Part 2 but never as fully inhabited in Part 2 as it is in Part 1; and the specific quality of the cover's psychological management, which is Part 1's central subject and which is given more sustained and more precise attention in Part 1 than in Part 2's more narratively compressed execution phase.

### Q: How does the villain shift between the two films affect the audience experience?

The villain shift from Rehman to Iqbal produces a specific change in the emotional register of the franchise's threat landscape that the audience experiences differently on first viewing and on subsequent viewings. On first viewing, the shift is partly disorienting: Rehman's intimate, relational menace has been the franchise's primary threat for an entire film, and Iqbal's institutional coldness is a different emotional register from what the franchise has trained the audience to receive as threatening. On subsequent viewings, the shift is more clearly legible as the structural expression of the shift from infiltration to execution: the threat that matters in each phase is the threat appropriate to that phase, and the franchise's ability to produce two distinct and genuinely threatening antagonists in different registers is evidence of the franchise's understanding that threat is not a single quality but a structural relationship between a vulnerability and what exploits it.

### Q: Is the franchise's two-film structure its greatest creative achievement?

The two-film structure is the franchise's most fundamental creative achievement in the sense that it is the precondition for everything else. The choice to tell the story across two films rather than compressing it into one was the choice to honor the decade's actual duration rather than to dramatize it efficiently: the two-film structure is the formal expression of the franchise's argument that the decade's weight cannot be adequately communicated in the time a single film provides. This choice enabled Part 1's sustained world-building and Part 2's emotionally weighted deployment, and the specific qualities of each film are available only because the other film exists to complement them. The two-film structure is not simply a distribution of narrative content across two containers: it is a creative decision about what kind of experience the franchise wants to produce, and the experience requires both films in the specific order and the specific relationship that the franchise establishes between them.

### Q: How does Part 2's opening sequence compare to Part 1's?

Part 2 opens with the specific quality of momentum already established: the world is known, the characters are known, and the opening sequence is organized around the resumption of a narrative rather than the initiation of one. The contrast in opening sequences is the most concentrated expression of the difference between a film that builds its world and a film that inhabits a world already built.

### Q: How does the treatment of the RAW institutional world differ between the two films?

The RAW institutional world receives significantly more narrative attention in Part 2 than in Part 1, and the expansion reflects the structural shift from the infiltration phase, where the institutional context is present but secondary to the cover's social management, to the execution phase, where the institutional context becomes the primary driver of the narrative's escalation. Part 1's Delhi sequences establish the institutional architecture and the key figures within it but spend relatively limited time there: the institutional context is the frame within which the Lyari story is set rather than an active narrative location. Part 2's Delhi sequences are more numerous, more dramatically central, and more explicitly engaged with the institutional decision-making that drives the execution phase. The expansion of the institutional world in Part 2 reflects the structural truth that the execution phase is a more collaborative institutional enterprise than the collection phase: the collection phase can run on the minimal communication between Hamza and his handlers, while the execution phase requires active institutional coordination and decision-making that the narrative must follow.

### Q: How does the franchise handle the challenge of making Part 2 satisfying for viewers who found Part 1 too slow?

The Part 2 pacing is noticeably faster than Part 1's from the opening sequences, and this acceleration is partly a deliberate response to the pacing criticism that Part 1 generated from the segment of the audience that found its slow-burn approach demanding. Part 2's faster pacing serves both the narrative and the commercial requirement of delivering the escalated experience that a franchise sequel traditionally promises its audience. The acceleration is not without cost: some of the specific qualities that Part 1's patience produces, the specific texture of the Lyari community life, the slow accumulation of relationship depth, the sustained attention to the cover's micro-management, are less present in Part 2 precisely because Part 2's faster pacing cannot accommodate them. The franchise made a considered creative choice to accelerate for Part 2, and the choice reflects an honest assessment that the audience that found Part 1's pace appropriate would accommodate an acceleration while the audience that found Part 1 too slow would find Part 2 more accessible. Both audiences received something they wanted, and both gave up something in exchange.

### Q: How do the two films handle the support characters differently?

The supporting cast's treatment evolves significantly between Part 1 and Part 2, reflecting the structural shift from world-building to world-unbuilding. Part 1 invests in the Lyari supporting characters with the patience that the franchise's world-building requires: Jameel, Aalam, and Ulfat receive screen time and character development that serves the accumulation of the Lyari world's human texture rather than the advancement of the operational plot. Part 2 deploys the supporting characters in ways that reflect the execution phase's priorities: the relationships built in Part 1 become the mechanisms through which the execution is possible, and the characters who built those relationships are present primarily as the instruments of their own eventual disruption. The supporting cast is richer in Part 1 as a world-building resource and more operationally significant in Part 2 as a dramatic consequence. The shift is emotionally devastating precisely because [the supporting characters](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/05/24/supporting-characters-analysis-dhurandhar/) have been made fully human in Part 1, which is the precondition for their deployment as operational instruments in Part 2.

The scene is Part 1's fullest expression of what the decade has built: a table shared between people who genuinely know each other, where the warmth is real even though the premise on which it rests is false.
