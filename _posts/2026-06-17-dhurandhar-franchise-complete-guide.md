## Franchise Overview: What This Is and Why It Matters

The Dhurandhar franchise consists of two films: Dhurandhar, released in 2025, and Dhurandhar: The Revenge, released in 2026. Both films are directed by Aditya Dhar, produced by Ronnie Screwvala and Aditya Dhar, and star Ranveer Singh as the central protagonist Jaskirat Singh Rangi, who operates under the cover identity of Hamza Ali Mazari. The franchise is classified as an A-rated spy thriller, set primarily in Karachi's Lyari neighborhood and in Delhi, spanning a fictional timeline of approximately fifteen years from the late 1990s through the mid-2010s.

The franchise's creative significance is examined in detail across [the complete Part 1 analysis](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/03/19/complete-analysis-dhurandhar-part-1/) and [the complete Part 2 analysis](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/03/23/complete-analysis-dhurandhar-the-revenge/). This guide summarizes what those analyses establish and provides the structured reference that makes them most useful.

The franchise's place in Hindi cinema is as the most commercially successful A-rated franchise in the genre's history and the first Hindi franchise to produce two films each crossing 1000 crore domestically. Its creative significance is as the most sophisticated treatment of intelligence work and the human cost of covert service that Hindi commercial cinema has produced. Both the commercial and creative significance are developed in [the franchise's impact analysis](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/06/20/why-dhurandhar-changed-bollywood-forever/).

The franchise's director, Aditya Dhar, established his commercial credentials with Uri: The Surgical Strike in 2019 before developing the Dhurandhar franchise concept across the subsequent years. The development period between Uri and Dhurandhar is the period during which Dhar's creative ambitions expanded from the proven military action format toward the more demanding psychological and operational complexity that the franchise requires. [The comparison between Uri and Dhurandhar](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/05/31/dhurandhar-vs-uri-surgical-strike-compared/) traces this development in detail.

The franchise's star, Ranveer Singh, brings to the Hamza role a career that had developed the specific capacities the character requires across a series of prior performances: Band Baaja Baaraat's social ease, Ram-Leela's emotional volatility, Bajirao Mastani's physical discipline, Padmaavat's menace, Gully Boy's restraint, and 83's mimicry. [The career-best performance analysis](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/06/13/ranveer-singh-career-best-performance-dhurandhar/) examines how these capacities converge in the Hamza performance.

The franchise's production represents a significant investment by Ronnie Screwvala's RSVP Movies, and the specific creative latitude that the production company provided to Dhar, including support for the A-rated content, the extended runtime, and the two-film narrative arc, is the institutional condition of possibility for the franchise's creative achievements. The commercial outcome, which made the franchise the most profitable investment in the production company's history, validated the creative latitude and established new precedents for what commercially ambitious Indian cinema can attempt.

## Dhurandhar (Part 1): Film Summary and Analysis

Part 1 covers the collection phase of Operation Dhurandhar, spanning approximately a decade from Jaskirat Singh Rangi's recruitment by RAW through the establishment and deepening of his cover identity as Hamza Ali Mazari in Karachi's Lyari neighborhood. The film ends at the operational midpoint: the cover has been established, the intelligence picture has been substantially developed, and the execution phase that Part 2 depicts has not yet begun.

The film's central dramatic arc is the construction of the cover and the accumulation of its psychological cost. Hamza builds genuine relationships with Rehman Dakait, with Jameel Jamali, with Ulfat, and with Yalina Jamali, and the authenticity of these relationships is both the cover's greatest operational success and its greatest personal cost. By Part 1's end, the audience has spent more than three hours watching the construction of something that Part 2 will deploy as an operational weapon, and the weight of that construction is the film's central emotional achievement.

## Dhurandhar: The Revenge (Part 2): Film Summary and Analysis

Part 2 covers the execution phase of Operation Dhurandhar, following the 26/11 Mumbai attacks which provide both the intelligence confirmation of the network's connection to the strategic target and the political authorization for RAW to move from intelligence gathering to active disruption. The film spans from the decision to execute through the operation's completion, and its ending addresses both the strategic success and the specific human cost that the success required and left unacknowledged.

The film's central dramatic arc is the deployment of what Part 1 built. The relationships Hamza constructed across the decade are the instruments of the operation's execution: the trust Rehman placed in him, the community belonging Jameel represented, the domestic life Yalina shared. Each of these is weaponized in the execution phase in ways that Part 1's construction phase made specifically devastating rather than generically tragic. The film ends with the operation's strategic success fully achieved and the human cost fully present and fully unaddressed by the institutional framework that authorized the cost's extraction.

## Complete Character Guide

**Jaskirat Singh Rangi / Hamza Ali Mazari** - The protagonist. A Punjabi Sikh man from Pathankot recruited by RAW following a personal tragedy that makes him emotionally recruitable. He spends a decade as Hamza Ali Mazari, a Lyari community member, building the cover and the intelligence that the operation requires. [The origin story analysis](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/05/08/jaskirat-singh-rangi-origin-story-analysis/) covers his pre-cover identity. [The Ranveer Singh performance analysis](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/06/13/ranveer-singh-career-best-performance-dhurandhar/) covers how Singh inhabits the dual identity.

**Ajay Sanyal** - The architect of Operation Dhurandhar within RAW. The senior intelligence officer who designed the operation, recruited Jaskirat, and manages the institutional decisions about the operation's continuation and eventual execution. His institutional authority is complete; his emotional access to the human cost he is authorizing is structurally limited by his position in the hierarchy.

**Sushant Bansal** - Hamza's field handler. The intermediate figure between the asset's daily operational reality and the institutional decision-making structure. He knows more than Sanyal about what the operation is costing Hamza and has less authority than Sanyal to act on that knowledge.

**Rehman Dakait** - The primary antagonist in Part 1. A Lyari community figure whose criminal organization is connected to the cross-border terrorism network that Operation Dhurandhar is designed to disrupt. Played with specific community authority rather than generic criminality, he is the franchise's most morally complex antagonist because the trust he extends to Hamza is genuine.

**Yalina Jamali** - The cover's domestic relationship. The franchise's most morally weighted supporting character because the relationship between Yalina and Hamza is the cover's most intimate deception and simultaneously the franchise's most authentic-feeling human connection. [Her dedicated analysis](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/04/11/yalina-jamali-character-analysis/) examines her in full.

**Jameel Jamali** - The World-Builder. The Lyari neighborhood resident whose warmth and community presence make the Lyari world feel like a community rather than a setting. He is the franchise's most beloved supporting character and the clearest evidence of the franchise's world-building investment.

**Uzair Baloch** - The rival power center in the Lyari criminal hierarchy. A complication for both Hamza and Rehman, representing the franchise's Complications category in [the supporting characters analysis](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/05/24/supporting-characters-analysis-dhurandhar/).

**Major Iqbal** - The primary antagonist in Part 2. A Pakistani military intelligence figure whose institutional threat represents a different danger from Rehman's relational threat: cold, institutional, and impervious to the authentic connections that the cover has built.

**Ulfat** - Rehman's wife. The franchise's most analytically rich domestic character, whose specific intelligence and agency within the domestic constraint of being married to a crime lord give her a complexity that transcends the typical supporting domestic role.

**Devavrat Kapoor** - The Minister. The political dimension of the institutional architecture that authorizes Operation Dhurandhar, representing the democratic political system's consent to what the operation requires.

**Shazia Bano** - A Part 2 cameo connecting the franchise to the wider intelligence cinematic universe Dhar is developing.

## The Operational Timeline

**Pre-recruitment (late 1990s):** Jaskirat Singh Rangi is a young Punjabi Sikh man whose specific personal tragedy makes him emotionally available for RAW recruitment. The specific events of this period are established through the Part 1 flashback sequences.

**Recruitment and cover construction (approximately 1999-2001):** Ajay Sanyal recruits Jaskirat and designs the Hamza Ali Mazari cover identity. The early years of the cover's construction, which [the operation's complete analysis](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/05/18/operation-dhurandhar-explained/) traces in detail, are the establishment phase: Hamza builds community presence without yet having access to the operational intelligence the mission requires.

**Access development and collection (approximately 2001-2007):** The middle years of the operation, in which the cover deepens, the relationships with Rehman and the Lyari community solidify, and the intelligence picture begins to develop at the operational level. This is the period that Part 1's primary narrative inhabits most fully.

**Collection plateau and 26/11 (approximately 2007-2008):** The operation reaches its maximum sustainable collection capacity. The 26/11 Mumbai attacks provide the intelligence confirmation of the network's connection to the strategic target and the political authorization for the execution phase.

**Execution phase (Part 2, approximately 2008-2014):** The operation transitions from collection to execution. The relationships built across the decade are deployed as operational instruments. The strategic objective is achieved and the operation concludes.

## Key Themes

The franchise's [central themes](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/04/14/themes-symbolism-dhurandhar-duology/) are examined in full in the dedicated analysis. This guide summarizes them for orientation.

**Identity and its accumulation:** The franchise's primary argument is that sustained performed identity is not merely a professional strategy but a transformative psychological process. Hamza Ali Mazari, performed across a decade, becomes something that Jaskirat Singh Rangi can no longer simply set aside. Identity is not fixed: it accumulates, and the accumulation of a performed identity is indistinguishable, from the inside, from the accumulation of an authentic one. This argument is the franchise's most philosophically ambitious claim and the one that makes it most analytically interesting as a work about performed selfhood in the broadest sense, extending beyond the spy thriller context into any situation where a person sustains a performed version of themselves for extended periods.

**Institutional service and its human cost:** The franchise is not anti-institutional: it endorses the operation's strategic objective and presents the institutional actors as professional and sincere. What it insists on is that the institutional framework is structurally unable to fully acknowledge the human cost of the operations it deploys, and that this inability is a feature of institutional logic rather than a failure of specific institutional actors. Sanyal is sincere. Bansal is caring. The institution is legitimate. And none of these qualities prevents the institution from extracting a cost from Jaskirat that it cannot acknowledge or repay. This specific argument is the franchise's most distinctive political and moral contribution: it refuses both the anti-institutional cynicism that would make the institution simply villainous and the pro-institutional celebration that would make its costs simply worthwhile.

**The ethics of covert betrayal:** The franchise's most specific and most uncomfortable moral argument is that the cover's most successful dimension, the authentic relationships it builds, is precisely the dimension that the operation eventually deploys as its most effective weapon. The authenticity of the relationships is not incidental to the betrayal: it is the condition of the betrayal's specific moral devastation. Jameel's trust in Hamza is genuine, and the genuine trust is what the operation uses. Rehman's trust is genuine, and the genuine trust is what the operation weaponizes. The franchise insists that this weaponization of genuine trust is what the operation required and that the institution authorized it, which is the franchise's most uncomfortable specific moral claim: that the state sanctioned the use of genuine human connection as an operational instrument.

**The question of home and belonging:** Running through both films is the franchise's implicit argument about what belonging actually consists of and what it costs to construct it artificially over an extended period. Hamza belongs to Lyari by Part 1's end in a way that cannot be entirely attributed to the cover's successful performance: the belonging is genuine because the decade has produced genuine knowledge, genuine relationships, and genuine investment in the community's specific human life. The question the franchise asks without answering is whether this genuine belonging, produced through a false premise, is as valid a form of belonging as genuine belonging produced through authentic identity. The franchise refuses to answer this question because the honest answer is that there is no clear principle for distinguishing the two once the decade of genuine experience has occurred.

**What the state owes the individual:** The franchise ends without institutional acknowledgment of what Jaskirat paid. This ending is the franchise's most direct statement about the relationship between the state and the individual who serves it: the state's legitimate objectives are served, and the individual's cost is absorbed into the institutional accounting without specific acknowledgment. This is not presented as a failure of specific institutional actors or as a conspiracy: it is presented as the structural reality of how institutions relate to the individuals who serve them at the level of covert operations. The state owes Jaskirat something. The franchise ends with the evidence that the debt exists. What it cannot provide is the evidence that institutional logic has any mechanism for paying it.

## What Comes Next

The franchise's future is the most actively discussed question in Indian commercial cinema at the time of this guide's writing, and the answer involves several distinct questions that must be kept separate.

**A third Dhurandhar film:** The creative case for a third Dhurandhar film is not obvious from the franchise's narrative conclusion, which is complete in ways that do not require extension. The commercial case is clear: a franchise with this track record has commercial demand for further installments. Whether the creative team has a story that is genuinely required by the characters and the world, rather than one generated by the commercial demand, is the question that will determine whether a third film is made at the franchise's creative level or simply at its commercial level.

**The connected universe:** [The Shazia Bano cameo](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/05/24/supporting-characters-analysis-dhurandhar/) establishes that the franchise exists within a larger connected intelligence cinema universe that Dhar is developing. Future installments in this universe, whether within the Dhurandhar franchise specifically or in connected properties, will develop the specific dimensions of the institutional landscape that the cameo implies.

**Aditya Dhar's next project:** Whatever Dhar makes next, the franchise has established the creative and commercial terms within which that project will be evaluated. ## Frequently Asked Questions

### Q: Do I need to watch the films in order?

Yes, and for reasons that go beyond narrative coherence. Part 1 is not a story that requires Part 2 for its plot to make sense: it is a complete narrative phase that can be understood without Part 2's resolution. But the emotional and psychological weight of Part 2 depends entirely on the investment that Part 1 builds: watching Part 2 without Part 1 is watching a narrative conclusion without the accumulated significance that makes the conclusion meaningful. The correct viewing order is Part 1 then Part 2, with the two-year interval between theatrical releases ideally reflected in some gap between viewings even if the gap is hours rather than years.

### Q: How long are the films?

Dhurandhar (Part 1) runs approximately 214 minutes. Dhurandhar: The Revenge (Part 2) runs approximately 208 minutes. Combined, the franchise is approximately 422 minutes, or approximately seven hours.

### Q: What is the A certificate about?

Both franchise films carry an A certificate, India's adult-only classification, reflecting the films' mature content including graphic violence, morally complex situations, and the specific quality of psychological darkness that the franchise's subject requires. The A certificate restricts the films to viewers 18 and older. The commercial evidence, documented in the box office records, demonstrates that the A-rated franchise is the most commercially successful in Hindi cinema history, challenging the industry assumption that adult-only certification is a commercial limitation.

### Q: What is Operation Dhurandhar?

Operation Dhurandhar is the covert intelligence operation that is the franchise's central narrative subject. It is a decade-long RAW operation designed to infiltrate and ultimately disrupt the financial and logistical network connecting Karachi's organized criminal infrastructure to the cross-border terrorism responsible for major incidents of violence against Indian interests. [The complete operation analysis](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/05/18/operation-dhurandhar-explained/) covers its strategic logic, key players, timeline, and relationship to real intelligence operations.

### Q: Who is Rehman Dakait based on?

Rehman Dakait is a fictional character inspired by the real criminal landscape of Karachi's Lyari neighborhood and by real figures in that landscape's documented history. The franchise carries the standard fictional disclaimer that its characters are invented rather than directly representing real people. [The real events analysis](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/04/28/real-events-inspired-dhurandhar/) examines the relationship between the franchise's fictional world and the historical reality it draws from.

### Q: Is the franchise based on a true story?

The franchise is a work of dramatic fiction inspired by real events including the IC-814 hijacking and the 26/11 Mumbai attacks, and by the documented criminal and intelligence history of the India-Pakistan relationship in the relevant period. No specific intelligence operation called Operation Dhurandhar is publicly documented. The franchise uses real events as narrative context while inventing the specific characters and the specific operation that its story requires.

### Q: What makes the franchise different from other Bollywood spy films?

The franchise differs from other Bollywood spy films in its refusal to present intelligence work as a series of dramatic confrontations separated by brief logistical intervals. It presents intelligence work as a decade-long process of patient accumulation, with the human cost accumulating alongside the operational progress. The complete comparison is examined in [the Bollywood spy thriller genre comparison](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/05/01/dhurandhar-vs-bollywood-spy-thrillers/).

### Q: How does the franchise handle the 26/11 attacks specifically?

The franchise treats the 26/11 Mumbai attacks as the narrative event that converts Operation Dhurandhar from a collection operation to an execution operation. Within the franchise's fictional logic, 26/11 provides both the intelligence confirmation that the Lyari network's financial connections extend to the cross-border terrorism responsible for the attacks and the political authorization for RAW to move from passive collection to active disruption. The franchise's depiction of 26/11 is aligned with the Indian government's official position on the attacks' origins and their connection to Pakistani state actors, and the [controversies analysis](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/06/03/dhurandhar-controversies-explained/) examines the contested dimensions of this alignment. Within the franchise's narrative, 26/11 is the event that makes the decade of collection finally deployable: not a random narrative trigger but the specific event that the operation was designed to respond to and that the intelligence picture was designed to make actionable.

### Q: What is the franchise's relationship to the real Uzair Baloch?

The franchise explicitly states in its disclaimer that its characters are fictional and that Uzair Baloch in the franchise is a character inspired by real events and real figures rather than a direct representation of the real Uzair Baloch. The real Uzair Baloch was a significant figure in Karachi's criminal landscape whose arrest and subsequent cooperation with Pakistani authorities generated major coverage in the relevant period. The franchise uses the structural position that the real Uzair Baloch occupied in Lyari's criminal hierarchy as inspiration for its fictional Uzair Baloch character without claiming to depict the real person's specific actions or motivations. ### Q: What is the significance of the Lyari neighborhood as a setting?

Lyari is one of Karachi's oldest neighborhoods, historically associated with specific ethnic communities including the Baloch community, and historically connected to both organized criminal activity and political organization. The franchise uses Lyari as its primary setting because the neighborhood's specific combination of ethnic community cohesion, political volatility, criminal infrastructure, and ISI connections makes it the most accurately located site for the kind of criminal-intelligence network that Operation Dhurandhar is designed to penetrate.
