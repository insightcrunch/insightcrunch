Every significant film generates controversy proportional to its ambitions, and the Dhurandhar franchise generated controversy proportional to its significance. The debates surrounding the franchise, which range from the propaganda question to the Baloch community's representational objections to the Gulf region's political responses, are not distractions from the films. They are the films' most honest critical reception: they reveal where the franchise pressed on real fault lines in Indian cinema's relationship to ideology, nationalism, and the ethics of representation, rather than on comfortable territory where agreement comes easily. This article presents each controversy with the intellectual rigor it deserves, offering the strongest available case for each position rather than adjudicating between them.

The franchise occupies a specific and uncomfortable position in Indian cultural life: it is too cinematically sophisticated to be dismissed as simple propaganda, too politically engaged to be treated as pure entertainment, and too commercially successful to be contained within the smaller space that India's more explicitly ideological films typically occupy. This combination, cinematic ambition, political engagement, and massive commercial reach, is what makes the franchise uniquely controversial and uniquely important as a case study in how Indian cinema navigates the relationship between storytelling and ideology. The controversies do not resolve into a comfortable conclusion about whether the franchise is good or bad for Indian culture. They produce a more interesting and more honest map of the tensions that serious cinema about serious political subjects inevitably generates.

The analysis that follows presents each major controversy through the lens of the strongest available arguments on multiple sides. The analysis does not conclude which side is right. Its purpose is to equip the reader to think more clearly about the questions the controversies raise, not to replace that thinking. [The franchise's complete analysis](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/03/19/complete-analysis-dhurandhar-part-1/) and [the Part 2 analysis](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/03/23/complete-analysis-dhurandhar-the-revenge/) develop the narrative and thematic dimensions. The controversies analysis examines the political and cultural dimensions, which are inseparable from the narrative and thematic ones but require a different analytical approach.

A preliminary observation about the nature of controversy in commercial cinema is necessary before the specific controversies are addressed. Controversy in commercial cinema operates differently from controversy in other cultural domains: it is amplified by the scale of the audience and by the commercial infrastructure that large-scale distribution creates, and it often generates reactions that are disproportionate to the specific content that triggered them because the commercial scale turns the specific content into a cultural event rather than a private viewing experience. The franchise's controversies are all real in the sense that the positions advanced on various sides represent genuine values and genuine interpretive positions. But they are also artifacts of the franchise's specific commercial scale, which made it visible enough to be contested. A film with the same content reaching a fraction of the audience would have generated a fraction of the controversy. The controversy is, among other things, a function of the audience's size, which is itself a function of the franchise's quality. The quality produced the scale, the scale produced the visibility, and the visibility produced the controversy.

This circularity is worth acknowledging because it points to something important about what the controversy reveals: the franchise is controversial precisely because it succeeded at what it was trying to do, at a scale that made the attempt unavoidable as a cultural event. A propaganda film that fails to reach its audience generates little controversy. A film that critics consider morally complex but commercially niche generates limited controversy. The franchise generated controversy because it succeeded at being both sophisticated and massively commercially visible, and this specific combination forced the cultural conversation about what the films are doing into the public domain in ways that neither pure propaganda nor pure art house cinema typically produces. The controversy is, in this specific sense, a measure of the franchise's success rather than of its failure, and the specific shape of the controversies reveals what the franchise's success accomplished culturally that its predecessors in both the nationalist cinema and the art house cinema traditions could not have accomplished.

## The Propaganda Debate: The Strongest Cases on Both Sides

The propaganda debate is the franchise's most contested critical question, and it is contested precisely because both sides have strong cases that deserve serious engagement rather than dismissal. Presenting the strongest version of each case is the prerequisite for thinking clearly about the question.

The strongest case that the franchise is propaganda rests on several specific textual and contextual observations. Textually, the franchise presents the Indian state and its intelligence apparatus in an unambiguously positive light: the strategic objective is legitimate, the institutional design is competent, the political authorization is appropriate, and the operation succeeds. The Pakistani antagonists range from criminal to complicit in cross-border terrorism, with no significant counterweight in the form of Pakistani characters who challenge the franchise's framing of the India-Pakistan relationship from an alternative perspective. The franchise's treatment of the 26/11 attacks, which are used as the operational trigger for the execution phase, is explicitly aligned with the Indian government's official account of the attacks' origins and its assessment of Pakistani state complicity. Contextually, the franchise was produced during a period of specific Indian political conditions that generated significant institutional support for cinema presenting the Indian security apparatus in a positive light, and the franchise's creators have documented relationships with political figures whose positions align with the franchise's political framing. The combination of textual alignment with official positions and contextual production relationships constitutes a legitimate basis for the propaganda reading, regardless of whether the creative team's motivations were primarily ideological or primarily artistic.

The propaganda case is strengthened by examining the franchise's specific selection of which elements of the India-Pakistan relationship to depict and which to elide. The franchise depicts Pakistani state complicity in cross-border terrorism as established fact rather than as contested claim, reflecting the Indian government's official position but not the position of all analysts and international observers. The franchise depicts the Indian intelligence operation as strategically necessary and institutionally legitimate without any significant exploration of whether alternative approaches to the cross-border terrorism problem existed or might have produced different balances of achievement and cost. The franchise's political framing is not just positive about India and negative about Pakistan in a general way: it is specifically aligned with the specific official Indian government positions on the most contested dimensions of the India-Pakistan relationship. This specific alignment is the strongest evidence for the propaganda reading, because it suggests that the franchise's political content is not incidental to its dramatic choices but is organized around specific political positions rather than emerging from independent artistic engagement with the subject matter.

The strongest case against the propaganda reading is equally specific and equally textual. The franchise's moral ambiguity in its treatment of the operation's human cost is not consistent with the propaganda model, which characteristically presents institutional action as both effective and morally clean. The franchise insists that the operation is effective and morally complicated simultaneously, and the moral complication is not resolved in the direction of institutional justification: the franchise ends with the human cost of the operation fully visible and fully uncompensated, without any institutional acknowledgment that this cost was paid or any suggestion that the institution is capable of full acknowledgment. A propaganda film would resolve the moral complication in the direction of institutional heroism. The franchise refuses this resolution, which is evidence against the propaganda reading at the level of the franchise's most important creative choices.

Additionally, the franchise's treatment of the Lyari community, which gives Pakistani and Balochi characters enough human complexity that the audience develops genuine investment in their wellbeing, is inconsistent with the dehumanization of the adversary that propaganda typically requires. Propaganda depends on the audience's willingness to identify with the institutional position without empathizing with those the institution acts against. The franchise makes empathy with the Lyari community structurally necessary for its emotional architecture to function: without the investment in Jameel, in Ulfat, in the specific human warmth of the community Hamza has inhabited, the franchise's most devastating emotional sequences have no foundation. [The supporting characters analysis](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/05/24/supporting-characters-analysis-dhurandhar/) develops this in detail. A propaganda film does not require the audience to genuinely care about the people on the other side of the institutional interest; Dhurandhar does, and the caring is not instrumental to the propaganda argument but essential to the human argument the franchise is making.

The against-propaganda case is also supported by the franchise's explicit engagement with the institutional costs of the operation's legitimacy. The franchise does not present the Indian state as a moral authority that transcends the specific consequences of its institutional choices. It presents it as an institution that makes legitimate choices and that is unable to fully account for the human costs those choices impose. This distinction, between moral legitimacy and moral accountability, is one that propaganda typically elides: propaganda presents the state as both legitimate and accountable, or legitimate in ways that make accountability unnecessary. The franchise makes the accountability deficit visible and morally significant, which is an artistic choice that propaganda has no use for and that genuine artistic engagement with the subject matter produces.

The honest analytical conclusion is that the franchise does not fit cleanly into either the propaganda category or the purely artistic category, because it operates in both registers simultaneously in ways that produce genuine tension rather than resolving into one or the other. Specific sequences feel like propaganda: the IC-814 references, certain scenes of institutional deliberation, the franchise's consistent alignment with official Indian positions on cross-border terrorism. Other sequences feel like genuine artistic complexity that propaganda cannot accommodate: the Jameel scenes, the Hamza-Bansal relationship, the franchise's refusal of institutional heroism at the moral level. The tension between these registers is what makes the franchise genuinely controversial rather than simply politically incorrect, and the controversy is more interesting than either a clean propaganda verdict or a clean artistic vindication would be.

The meta-question that the propaganda debate raises, but that neither side typically addresses directly, is whether the propaganda category is a useful analytical tool for a work that simultaneously exhibits propagandistic characteristics and resists them. The propaganda category was developed to describe a specific kind of political communication that is organized around producing specific political beliefs or behaviors in its audience and that subordinates artistic and human complexity to this organizational purpose. The franchise's artistic and human complexity is not subordinated to its political alignment: the complexity is the franchise's most significant creative achievement and its political alignment is one of its most significant limitations, and the two are in genuine tension rather than in the relationship that the propaganda model describes. A more accurate description might be "politically aligned serious cinema," which is a category that acknowledges both the political content and the artistic ambition without reducing the work to either one.

## Baloch Community Objections: Representation and Ethics

The Baloch community's objections to the franchise constitute a different kind of controversy from the propaganda debate, one that is primarily about the ethics of representation rather than about the franchise's political alignment. These objections deserve specific and serious engagement because they raise questions that the propaganda debate does not: about the ethics of using living people as characters in a commercial narrative without their consent, about the specific responsibilities that large-scale commercial cinema has to the communities it depicts, and about the relationship between artistic freedom and representational justice.

The objections center on the franchise's use of Uzair Baloch as a character, which is acknowledged by the franchise to be inspired by but not a direct representation of the real Uzair Baloch, who is a living Pakistani criminal figure whose actual story is significantly more complex than the franchise's dramatic simplification of it. The Baloch diaspora's objections extend beyond the Uzair character to the franchise's broader treatment of the Baloch community within the Lyari narrative: the franchise's Lyari is populated primarily by characters identified as Balochi, and the community's dominant narrative role in the franchise's criminal ecology is a representational choice that some Baloch community members and advocates have identified as perpetuating stereotypes about Balochi people as criminals and as threats to Indian national security.

The strongest case for the community's objections rests on several specific grounds. The franchise reaches hundreds of millions of viewers across India and globally, and its depiction of Balochi characters as the primary population of a criminal underworld connected to cross-border terrorism constitutes a representational impact at a scale that individual artistic choices rarely achieve. The franchise's specific use of Uzair Baloch as an inspiration for a character who is presented as a criminal threat raises ethical questions about the use of living people's identities and stories in commercial narratives, particularly when those people have not consented and when the commercial narrative benefits from the association with a real story that it simultaneously fictionalize. The franchise does not present any Baloch characters in roles that counterbalance the criminal representation, which means the representational picture available to audiences unfamiliar with the real Baloch community is determined entirely by the franchise's dramatic choices.

The strongest case for the franchise's representational choices rests on the principle of artistic freedom and on the specific context of the franchise's geographic and dramatic setting. Lyari is historically a real neighborhood whose connection to criminal activity and political violence is documented in Pakistani journalism and in academic accounts of Karachi's political history, and a realistic depiction of Lyari's criminal ecology necessarily includes Balochi characters because Balochi people constitute a significant portion of Lyari's population. The franchise's disclaimer that its characters are fictional and inspired by rather than directly representing real people is legally and artistically conventional, and the disclaimer is appropriate to a work of dramatic fiction based on real events and real geographic contexts rather than on specific individual accounts. The argument that commercial cinema must ensure that every community depicted in a crime narrative is represented in roles that counterbalance the criminal depiction would impose a representational responsibility on commercial fiction that no other art form is required to meet and that would significantly constrain the kinds of stories that can be told about real communities with real criminal histories.

The legal dimensions of the Baloch objections are the most practically significant for the franchise's commercial life. Legal challenges initiated in Pakistani courts by figures connected to the real Uzair Baloch's legal situation created the possibility of injunctive relief against the franchise's distribution in certain territories, and the franchise's production company navigated these challenges through the standard combination of legal defense and the fictional disclaimer's protective function. The Indian legal system's treatment of the objections is consistent with established precedent for commercial fiction based on real events, providing the franchise with the legal protection that the disclaimer and the fictional framing afford.

## CBFC Certification and the A-Rating

The franchise's A certificate, the Indian censor board's classification for films restricted to adults only, is both a controversy in its own right and a factor that shaped several other controversies by limiting the franchise's demographic reach while simultaneously enhancing its cultural credibility.

The CBFC certification process for both franchise films involved the specific negotiations between the production company and the certification board that any controversial film requires. The publicly available information about what was cut or modified during the certification process is limited, because these negotiations are typically conducted in confidence, but industry reporting at the time of both films' releases identified several categories of content that the CBFC required modification of: specific sequences depicting violence at a level of graphic detail that the board determined exceeded U/A certification standards, certain dialogue exchanges that the board determined were insufficiently distanced from real political positions on the Pakistan-India relationship, and at least one sequence in Part 2 that was flagged for its specific treatment of Pakistani institutional figures in ways that the board determined required clarification of the fictional framing.

The CBFC's role in shaping the franchise's final cut is analytically significant beyond the specific modifications it required. The certification process creates a specific dynamic in which politically engaged commercial cinema must navigate between the creative team's intentions, the board's assessment of what the public interest requires, and the commercial imperatives that drive the production company's decisions about what modifications are acceptable relative to the commercial and artistic investment at stake. The franchise's navigation of this process, specifically the decision to accept the A certificate rather than to cut additional material that would have secured a U/A certificate, reflects a creative and commercial judgment that the content integrity at the adult-certification level was more valuable than the demographic breadth that U/A certification would have provided. This judgment proved commercially correct, but it was not a commercially obvious choice at the time it was made.

The A rating's commercial and cultural effects are among the controversy's most analytically interesting dimensions. Commercially, the A rating restricts the audience to adults, which reduces the family viewing segment that typically drives weekend multiplex performance for major Hindi releases. The franchise's commercial team initially assessed the A rating as a commercial liability, and some early projections discounted the franchise's potential commercial performance on this basis. The actual commercial performance, which [the franchise's box office records](https://reportmedic.org/tools/bollywood-box-office-explorer.html) document in detail, refuted these projections: the franchise became the highest-grossing A-rated franchise in Hindi cinema history by a significant margin, demonstrating that the adult demographic alone is commercially sufficient to support blockbuster-tier performance when the content quality generates the specific kind of deep audience investment that drives repeat viewing.

The cultural argument that the A rating actually helped the franchise commercially and culturally is the most counterintuitive dimension of the CBFC controversy. The argument runs as follows: the A rating functions as a quality signal in the specific cultural context of Indian commercial cinema, where the majority of major releases carry U/A certificates calibrated for maximum demographic reach. A film that carries an A rating is implicitly signaling that its creative team prioritized content integrity over demographic breadth, which is a credibility signal for the specific audience segment that values creative ambition and is skeptical of commercial cinema's tendency to soften difficult material for family viewership. The franchise's A rating made it a film that serious adult viewers could take seriously, and the seriousness was commercially productive because the serious adult viewer is also the repeat viewer whose return attendance drives the holdover performance that the franchise's commercial trajectory documents.

The rating was a commercial restriction and a commercial advantage simultaneously, and the franchise's extraordinary performance represents the first clear demonstration in Hindi cinema that adult-only certification is not a blockbuster ceiling. This demonstration has specific implications for future commercially ambitious Indian cinema: filmmakers and producers who want to make adult-oriented content that compromises narrative for demographic accessibility now have the franchise's commercial evidence that the demographic cost of adult certification can be more than offset by the quality benefits of content integrity. The CBFC certification controversy is therefore not just a story about the franchise's specific regulatory experience but a story about how the franchise changed the commercial calculus for adult-oriented content in Hindi cinema more broadly.

The specific question of what the franchise would have looked like with a U/A certificate is worth addressing directly, because it illuminates what the A rating's content protection actually produced. The material that would have required modification or removal to achieve U/A certification includes some of the franchise's most analytically significant sequences: the [warehouse fight's specific depiction of physical violence](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/05/14/dhurandhar-action-sequences-ranked-analyzed/) and its aftermath, certain dialogue exchanges in the Hamza-Rehman relationship that approach the specific quality of genuine human connection rather than cover performance, and several sequences in Part 2's execution phase that require the specific kind of moral discomfort that the A rating's adult-only classification is designed to allow. A U/A franchise would have been a different franchise, not just in its specific content but in its overall tonal and moral register: the adult-only content is not incidental to the franchise's creative achievement but is part of the architecture on which the achievement is built.

## Gulf Region Bans and Restrictions

The franchise's reception in Gulf Cooperation Council territories generated a separate controversy that intersects with the propaganda debate, the Pakistan representation debate, and the practical commercial dimensions of distributing politically engaged cinema in territories where the India-Pakistan relationship is a diplomatically sensitive subject.

The specific pattern of restrictions and bans across Gulf territories reflected varying assessments by different national censorship authorities of the franchise's diplomatic implications. Some GCC territories cleared the franchise for theatrical release without significant restriction. Others required specific cuts or modifications that addressed content the territorial authorities identified as potentially offensive to Pakistani national interests or to the broader Islamic community that the franchise's Karachi setting depicts. A small number of territories declined to authorize theatrical release at all, citing the franchise's political content and its depiction of Pakistani institutions in ways that the territorial authorities determined were inconsistent with their diplomatic positions.

The commercial impact of the Gulf restrictions was real but limited relative to the franchise's overall performance, because the Gulf territories' contribution to the franchise's worldwide gross is significant as an overseas market but is not the dominant overseas revenue source for Hindi films, which remains the North American diaspora market. The franchise's North American performance was unaffected by the Gulf controversy and generated the overseas records documented in [the franchise's commercial record](https://insightcrunch.com/2026/05/21/dhurandhar-franchise-records-broken/).

The political dynamics underlying the Gulf restrictions are more analytically interesting than the commercial impact. The Gulf states occupy a specific position in the India-Pakistan diplomatic relationship: they have significant economic relationships with both countries, significant populations from both countries within their borders, and strong interests in avoiding the kind of diplomatic friction that publicly supporting either country's cultural product against the other's objections would create. The Gulf restrictions are therefore not simply a response to the franchise's specific content but a diplomatic signal about the GCC states' preferred position relative to the India-Pakistan relationship that the franchise's political content forced them to take. The franchise's very existence as a major commercial product with explicit India-Pakistan political content put the Gulf states in a position where some response was required, and the varying responses across different territories reflected different assessments of how to minimize diplomatic friction while managing the commercial interests of both the Indian film industry and the Gulf territories' existing audience for Hindi cinema.

## The "Hindu Nationalist Cinema" Framing in International Coverage

The franchise's reception in international media, particularly in Western critical coverage and in coverage by Indian liberal critics writing in English, generated a controversy about the appropriateness of the "Hindu nationalist cinema" framing that was applied to the franchise by a significant portion of this coverage.

The framing emerged from several specific elements of the franchise's political content: the franchise's positive treatment of the Hindu Punjabi protagonist whose identity is suppressed in service of a Muslim cover, the franchise's treatment of Pakistani institutions as complicit in cross-border terrorism, the franchise's alignment with official Indian government positions on the 26/11 attacks and their origins, and the production context's documented connections to political figures associated with the Indian ruling party. The combination of these elements led a significant portion of international and English-language Indian criticism to frame the franchise primarily as a product of the Hindu nationalist political current that has dominated Indian politics since 2014, and to read its specific creative choices through this political lens.

The strongest case for the "Hindu nationalist cinema" framing rests on the contextual observation that the franchise's specific political positions, on the India-Pakistan relationship, on Indian intelligence operations, and on the legitimacy of covert counter-terrorism, align precisely with the positions of the dominant political current in India at the time of the franchise's production and release. A film whose political positions align this precisely with the ruling party's official positions is reasonably identified as having a political orientation, even if the creative team's motivations were primarily artistic rather than ideological. The framing is not simply about the franchise's content in isolation: it is about the franchise's content in the specific political context of its production and reception, and the context is relevant to any honest analysis of the franchise's cultural significance. The argument that the political context does not matter because the films speak for themselves is an argument that applies equally to all politically engaged cinema and that would render political analysis of cinema impossible, which is not a position that most serious film critics accept.

The case for the framing is also supported by the specific way the franchise treats its protagonist's religious identity. Jaskirat Singh Rangi is a Sikh man who adopts a Muslim identity for the cover, and the franchise's treatment of this religious crossing is not neutral: the franchise frames the assumption of the Muslim identity as a sacrifice and as a form of loss rather than as a neutral cover detail. The Sikh identity is presented as something worth preserving and recovering, and the Muslim cover identity is presented as an imposition rather than as an alternative valid identity. This framing reflects a specific view about the relative value of Hindu and Sikh identities on one hand and Muslim identity on the other that is consistent with the Hindu nationalist political current's specific value hierarchy, and critics who notice this framing have legitimate grounds for identifying it as part of the franchise's political orientation.

The strongest case against the "Hindu nationalist cinema" framing as the primary analytical lens for the franchise rests on the observation that the framing reduces a complex creative work to its political alignment and thereby loses most of what makes the franchise analytically interesting. The franchise's moral ambiguity, its treatment of the operation's human cost, its genuine investment in the humanity of its Pakistani characters: all of these are elements that do not fit the "Hindu nationalist cinema" model and that the framing is structurally required to minimize or ignore. The framing also conflates the franchise's political positions, which are real and worth analyzing, with its creative intentions, which are more complicated and which the evidence of the films themselves does not reduce to ideological messaging. The strongest works of serious cinema are typically not reducible to the political positions they hold, and the franchise's specific creative achievements, the psychological depth, the operational sophistication, the world-building ambition, are not products of its political alignment but of artistic investment that would be present regardless of which political positions the franchise expressed.

The international coverage debate is also partially a meta-debate about how Western critical frameworks for analyzing politically engaged cinema apply or fail to apply to Indian commercial cinema. Western critical frameworks for "nationalist cinema" or "propaganda cinema" were developed primarily in relation to Hollywood and European national cinemas, and their direct application to Indian commercial cinema's specific relationship to nationalism and ideology involves assumptions about what the relationship between cinema and the state should be that may not translate accurately to the Indian context. The franchise's critics who apply these frameworks to the franchise without acknowledging the translation problem produce analyses that are formally consistent but contextually distorted, and the controversy about the framing is partly a controversy about the adequacy of the analytical frameworks being applied. The more productive response to this meta-controversy is not to abandon the international critical frameworks entirely but to apply them with sufficient awareness of their limitations and their assumptions to produce analyses that are honest about both what the frameworks reveal and what they obscure.

The specific comparison between the franchise's international critical reception and the international critical reception of politically aligned cinema from Western countries is worth making explicit, because the comparison reveals a double standard that the controversy has not adequately addressed. American, British, and French cinema regularly produces films with strong nationalist political alignments that receive international critical attention primarily for their artistic qualities rather than for their political orientation. The same critics who frame Dhurandhar primarily as "Hindu nationalist cinema" typically do not apply an equivalent framing to comparable American military films or British intelligence films, even when those films have equally clear political alignments with their national security establishments. The double standard does not invalidate the concerns about the franchise's political content, but it does suggest that the concerns are being applied selectively in ways that reflect the specific geopolitical position of India in international critical discourse rather than a consistent analytical standard.

## The Controversy as Commercial and Cultural Amplifier

The franchise's controversies, taken as a whole, had a specific and demonstrable effect on the franchise's commercial performance that is analytically counterintuitive but commercially significant: they amplified rather than suppressed audience engagement with both films.

The amplification mechanism is straightforward once identified: controversy generates cultural conversation, cultural conversation generates audience awareness, and audience awareness is the prerequisite for theatrical attendance. The franchise was genuinely controversial in ways that motivated people who had already decided to see it to discuss it publicly and extensively, and those discussions reached people who had not yet decided and converted a portion of them into additional audience members. The controversy also motivated a specific category of audience attendance that is commercially significant but rarely analyzed: the attendance of people who go to see a film primarily to form their own opinion about a controversy they have heard about rather than because they expected to enjoy the film in the conventional entertainment sense. This controversy-motivated audience is not a reliable commercial mechanism for most films, but for a franchise with the Dhurandhar franchise's specific combination of political engagement and cinematic quality, the controversy-motivated audience that came to assess the propaganda question or the representation question or the nationalist cinema question found a film that was more complex and more cinematically accomplished than the controversy framing had prepared them for, and this positive surprise produced a portion of the repeat viewership that the holdover data documents.

The social media dimension of the controversy-as-amplifier was particularly significant for the franchise's second-week commercial performance. Films that generate genuine debate rather than simply positive or negative audience reaction benefit from the specific way social media handles contested content: debate-generating content receives more sustained algorithmic attention than consensus content, because debate involves more interactions per post and more diverse interaction types than simple positive or negative responses. The franchise's controversies generated exactly the kind of sustained, multi-directional social media debate that produces algorithmic amplification, and the amplification kept the franchise in the cultural conversation throughout the second week and beyond in ways that purely positive reception would not have sustained. The controversy was, in algorithmic terms, a form of extended marketing that the franchise did not pay for.

The relationship between controversy and commercial performance is not a simple one in which more controversy produces more commercial performance: there are thresholds at which controversy becomes commercially damaging rather than commercially productive, typically when the controversy reaches the level of organized boycott campaigns with sufficient social infrastructure to reduce attendance materially. The franchise faced several such boycott campaigns, particularly in territories where the Baloch community objections had organized political expression, but the boycott campaigns' commercial impact was limited by the franchise's strong base audience and by the specific quality of the films themselves, which generated advocacy from their engaged audience that counterbalanced the boycott advocacy in the broader public conversation.

The cultural dimension of the controversy as amplifier is more significant than the commercial dimension in the long run. The franchise's controversies ensured that it was discussed not just as entertainment but as a cultural event with political and ethical dimensions, which is the kind of cultural status that sustains a film's relevance beyond its theatrical run. Films that are commercially significant but culturally uncontested tend to fade from cultural memory as soon as the theatrical run ends. Films that generate genuine cultural controversy remain subjects of discussion and analysis for years after their theatrical runs, because the controversies they generate are connected to ongoing cultural and political questions that the films' existence has made more visible. The franchise's controversies have ensured its cultural longevity in this sense: the debates about its propaganda status, its representational ethics, and its political framing are debates that will continue to be productive for Indian cultural and political analysis for years after the films themselves have transitioned from current events to historical objects.

The franchise's complete commercial data, which you can examine through [the franchise's box office records tool](https://reportmedic.org/tools/bollywood-box-office-explorer.html), shows the specific commercial pattern that the controversy amplification produced: not a front-loaded event performance that exhausts quickly but a sustained demand curve that remained elevated well past the conventional decay timeline. This pattern is the commercial signature of a film that is generating ongoing cultural conversation rather than simply delivering entertainment, and the controversies are the primary mechanism through which the ongoing cultural conversation was sustained beyond the opening weeks.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Q: Is Dhurandhar propaganda?

The honest answer is that the franchise is neither clearly propaganda nor clearly free of propagandistic elements, and that the most useful answer to the question is a map of the specific evidence on both sides rather than a verdict. The franchise's consistent alignment with official Indian positions on Pakistan, its positive treatment of the Indian intelligence apparatus, and its production context's connections to politically aligned institutions constitute legitimate grounds for the propaganda reading. The franchise's moral complexity in its treatment of the operation's human cost, its genuine investment in the humanity of its Pakistani characters, and its refusal of institutional heroism at the level of the franchise's most significant moral choices constitute legitimate grounds for the against-propaganda reading. The question is ultimately about what "propaganda" means as a critical category and whether the franchise's specific combination of political alignment and artistic complexity fits that category or requires a more nuanced description. The propaganda debate is most productively understood as a debate about critical vocabulary rather than as a debate about the franchise's content, which is specific and analyzable regardless of which vocabulary is applied to it.

### Q: What were the specific Baloch community objections to the franchise?

The Baloch community's objections focused on two primary concerns. The first was the use of the real Uzair Baloch's identity as inspiration for a character in a commercial narrative that he had not authorized and that placed a version of his identity within a story that served the narrative's purposes rather than his own. The second was the franchise's treatment of Balochi characters more broadly: the franchise's Lyari is populated primarily by Balochi characters, and their dominant narrative role as members of a criminal network connected to cross-border terrorism was identified by community advocates as perpetuating stereotypes about Balochi people that have real effects on how the community is perceived in India and internationally. The objections also included specific legal dimensions related to the use of a living person's identity in a commercial narrative, particularly in the context of Uzair Baloch's ongoing legal situation at the time of the franchise's release.

### Q: Why did the Gulf states restrict or ban the franchise?

The Gulf state restrictions reflected the specific diplomatic position that GCC states occupy relative to the India-Pakistan relationship rather than a straightforward response to the franchise's content. The Gulf states have significant economic and political relationships with both India and Pakistan, and a major Indian commercial film with explicitly anti-Pakistan political content put those states in a position where some response was required to manage their relationship with Pakistan while also managing their commercial relationships with the Indian film industry. Different GCC states reached different conclusions about how to balance these competing interests, producing the varied pattern of full release, modified release, and full restriction that the franchise encountered across the Gulf region. The diplomatic dynamics rather than any specific content objection are the most accurate explanation for the pattern of restrictions.

### Q: Did the A certificate hurt the franchise commercially?

The franchise's commercial performance demonstrates that the A certificate did not hurt the franchise commercially in the way that conventional commercial logic predicted. The franchise became the highest-grossing A-rated franchise in Hindi cinema history by a significant margin, demonstrating that the adult demographic alone is commercially sufficient to support blockbuster-tier performance when the content quality generates deep audience investment and repeat viewing. The A certificate did restrict the franchise's demographic reach relative to what a U/A certificate would have allowed, and this restriction was a real commercial limitation. But the specific audience the A certificate reached, adults who were prepared to engage with demanding content and who were willing to return for repeat viewings, proved to be a more commercially productive audience than the conventional commercial model had assumed. The franchise's A certificate story is the strongest available evidence that adult-only certification is not a commercial ceiling for Hindi cinema when the content quality is high enough to generate the specific kind of audience engagement that drives sustained commercial performance.

### Q: How did Indian and international critics differ in their reading of the franchise's politics?

The most consistent difference between Indian and international critical readings of the franchise's politics was in the priority given to the political alignment versus the artistic complexity. International critical coverage, particularly in Western media, tended to frame the franchise's political alignment as its primary and most analytically significant feature, treating the artistic complexity as secondary to or in service of the ideological content.
